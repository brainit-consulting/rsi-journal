Second Movement · Engineering the Anchor

The Anchor

Series I ended with a verdict and a leverage point: a frozen model is a bounded self-improver, and the one thing that raises the bound is not a cleverer inner loop but a cheaper, sharper, harder-to-game source of external ground truth. The verdict is settled. The leverage point is not. This paper opens a second movement by taking the anchor itself as the object of study and asking the constructive question the finale left standing: can a sharp verifier be built from parts a frozen model can afford — and what stops it?

Abstract

Six papers established that every self-improvement loop a frozen model can turn compounds only under an external ground-truth anchor it cannot mint internally, and that the anchor's quality — its precision (No. 06) and its inexploitability (No. 05) — is the binding constraint on how far the model climbs. That reframes the whole enterprise: the interesting engineering problem is not the loop, it is the anchor. But a sharp, cheap, hard-to-game verifier looks expensive by definition. This paper tests whether it can be assembled from the opposite — a pile of individually weak checks, each catching only a slice of what can go wrong, each cheap enough for a frozen model to run. In a sandbox where candidates carry latent defects and each verifier covers only part of the defect space, I measure the composite AND-gate as checks are stacked. (1) A single weak verifier (covers 4 of 20 defect types) is nearly useless: it lets 50% of flawed candidates through. Stack independent weak checks and the composite false-accept rate falls geometrically — ×0.75 per added check — from 50% to 0.6% by sixteen checks, coverage climbing to 19.3 of 20 types. A sharp anchor genuinely assembles from weak parts. (2) Independence is the entire mechanism, not a footnote. Rebuild the same sixteen checks so they share a common core — correlated blind spots — and the composite plateaus: false-accept stalls at ~0.10, coverage caps at 12.7 of 20, and the eight never-covered defect types leak forever. This is No. 05's clones result one substrate down: redundant checks defend nothing. (3) Composition is not free. Each added check, being imperfect, also rejects good candidates; yield falls like (1−f)K — 98%→72% over sixteen checks at a 2% per-check false-positive rate. So the anchor is engineerable, but along a hard tradeoff: sharpness bought with independence, paid for in yield. The scarce resource is not checks; it is uncorrelated checks. That is where the second movement will live.

Series I closed with a sentence I have not stopped turning over. Having shown that a frozen model's self-improvement is bounded by an external anchor it cannot produce, No. 06 named the consequence: “the leverage point for anyone actually trying to build a self-improving system is not a better inner loop — it is a cheaper, sharper, harder-to-game source of external ground truth.” That was the correct place to stop a series about the loops. It is the wrong place to stop a journal. If the anchor is what binds, then the anchor is the thing worth engineering, and the first question on that new territory is blunt: a verifier that is simultaneously cheap, sharp, and hard to game sounds like a contradiction — cheap things are usually dull, sharp things are usually expensive. Can you have all three by building the anchor out of parts, rather than buying it whole?

From “the anchor binds” to “can the anchor be built?”

The finale's ledger holds two facts that, put together, define this paper's problem. First (No. 06): the anchor's precision sets where a loop concentrates — a sharp verifier gives a sharp, truth-pinned model; a loose one lets the loop drift back toward collapse. Second (No. 05): panel size buys nothing, panel diversity buys almost everything — eight identical judge-clones recovered zero of the oracle gap while eight judges that failed differently recovered ~96%. Both facts are about the anchor's quality, and both were measured on the consuming side — what a given anchor does to a loop. Neither asked the producing side: where does a sharp anchor come from, and can a frozen model afford to make one?

There is a structural reason to think the answer is yes, and it is the oldest trick in learning theory. Verifying is usually cheaper than generating — checking that a candidate has no defect of type t is a narrow, local test, far cheaper than producing a candidate correct in every respect. And a weak check is affordable precisely because it is weak: it only has to catch one kind of error. The gamble is that many cheap, partial checks can be composed into an expensive-looking sharp one — the same gamble boosting makes when it turns weak learners into a strong one [1], that ensemble methods make when they demand classifiers be accurate and diverse [2], and that safety engineering makes with layered independent defenses whose holes must not line up [3]. This paper asks whether the anchor obeys the same arithmetic, and — the part that matters for a frozen model — what the composition costs.

The model

A task has D = 20 latent defect types. A candidate is which defects it carries: a correct candidate has none; a flawed one has each defect independently with probability δ = 0.15 (so at least one). A verifier covers a subset Ck of the defect types (here |Ck| = 4 — it can only detect four of the twenty things that go wrong) and accepts a candidate iff that candidate has no defect inside Ck. The composite AND-gate accepts iff every verifier accepts — i.e. iff the candidate has no defect in the union of coverage. A flawed candidate is falsely accepted (the anchor leaks) iff all its defects fall outside that union. Deterministic: 6 seeds, N=40,000 candidates per condition; script in the notes [7].

A sharp anchor assembles from weak parts

Start with the discouraging baseline. A single verifier covering 4 of 20 defect types is barely worth running: measured on flawed candidates, it catches only 49.6% of them and lets the other half straight through. As an anchor it is worthless — a 50% false-accept gate cannot pin a loop to truth. Now stack independent copies — each an independent random choice of 4 covered types — and require all to accept:

Finding

Half-useless checks compose into a sharp anchor. False-accept falls from 50.5% at one check to 0.56% at sixteen — nearly two orders of magnitude — as coverage climbs to 19.3 of the 20 defect types. The decay is geometric: a straight-line fit of log(false-accept) against K has slope −0.282 per check, i.e. each added independent verifier multiplies the leak by ×0.75. This is the boosting arithmetic [1] in the anchor's currency: because independent checks cover disjoint slices of defect space, the probability that all of a candidate's defects dodge every check shrinks multiplicatively. The producing side of the anchor is therefore not a contradiction after all — sharpness is buildable from cheapness, provided you can pay in quantity. The whole question becomes what quantity costs, and §03 shows the price is not paid in checks.

Independence is the mechanism, and it is scarce

The geometric decay of §02 assumed each check picks its four covered types independently. Relax exactly that and nothing else. Build the sixteen checks so they share a common core of covered types and differ only in a single unique type each — correlated blind spots, checks that all miss the same things. The clones result, one substrate down

Correlated checks plateau. Sixteen of them leave the false-accept rate at 9.7% — seventeen times leakier than the independent sixteen (0.56%) — because their union coverage caps at 12.7 of 20 types. The remaining seven-or-eight defect types are in no check's coverage and leak forever; adding a seventeenth correlated check does almost nothing, because it too misses them. This is exactly No. 05's finding transported from judges to verifiers: eight identical judge-clones recovered zero of the oracle gap there; here, sixteen overlapping checks recover only a fraction of the coverage and stall. The scarce resource in anchor-building is not the number of checks — it is the number of uncorrelated checks. A frozen model that generates its verifiers by resampling itself gets the correlated column, because self-derived checks share the model's own blind spots (the argument of No. 05, §04 of No. 06). It can stack a thousand and never cover the types it cannot see.

This is the same shape safety engineering has known for decades as the layered-defense or “Swiss cheese” model: independent defensive layers stop an accident only when their holes do not line up, and correlated failure modes — a common cause that punches through every layer at once — are the ones that defeat defense in depth [3]. The anchor obeys the same law. Independence is not a nice-to-have that improves the constant factor; it is the difference between geometric decay and a hard floor.

The composition is not free: the yield tax

Everything so far measured only the anchor's sharpness — how well it rejects the bad. An anchor also has to keep the good, and stacking checks erodes exactly that. Real checks are imperfect in both directions: even a correct candidate trips a spurious flag now and then. Give each verifier a modest 2% false-positive rate — one time in fifty it rejects a candidate that was actually fine — and a correct candidate must survive all K checks. Its yield falls like (1−f)K: the rightmost column of §02. At sixteen checks, yield is down to 72.4% — more than a quarter of good candidates thrown away — and it keeps falling exponentially as you stack more.

The tradeoff, stated

Sharpening the anchor by adding independent checks drives false-accept down geometrically (good) and drives yield down exponentially (bad). The two curves cross the same variable K in opposite directions, so there is an optimum, not a free lunch: you stack checks until the marginal reduction in false-accept is no longer worth the marginal candidates discarded. Where that optimum sits depends on how costly a false accept is versus a discarded good candidate — a property of the task, not of the model. And it gets worse under correlation: correlated checks pay the same yield tax (each still spuriously rejects) while buying almost none of the sharpness, so redundant verifiers are the worst of both — full price, no product. This is the quantitative reason “just add more checks” is not a strategy; independence-per-check, not count, is what you are actually shopping for.

What this buys the second movement

Series I proved the anchor binds and stopped. This paper takes one step onto the producing side and finds the anchor is engineerable — a sharp verifier really does assemble from weak, cheap, partial checks — but engineerable along a law with three clauses, each of which is a research direction rather than a dead end.

Sharpness is buildable. The discouraging intuition — that a cheap-sharp-unbiddable verifier is a contradiction — is wrong. Weak checks compose geometrically; a 50%-leaky verifier stacked sixteen deep is a 0.6%-leaky one. So the leverage point No. 06 named is not a wish; it is a construction, and the first good news the second movement has to offer.

But independence is the currency, and a frozen model is poor in it. The composition works only to the extent the checks fail differently, and a model sampling its own checks gets correlated ones — the plateau, not the decay. This sharpens the finale's seam rather than dissolving it: the thing a frozen model cannot mint internally is not “a check” (it can generate those all day) but independence between checks. Uncorrelated blind spots have to come from somewhere the model is not — a different model, a compiler, a unit test, a database, a human, physical execution. That is the anchor-building version of No. 06's signature.

And the anchor has a price even when you can pay it. The yield tax means an assembled anchor is never free; past some point more sharpness costs more discarded truth than it is worth. So the practical target is not “the sharpest possible anchor” but “the sharpest anchor per unit of independence available, before yield collapses” — an optimization with a real interior maximum. That is a concrete, falsifiable object for the papers ahead: measure, for a real task, where the false-accept/yield frontier bends, and how much independence a frozen model can actually source from tools versus from itself.

The prediction this paper commits to: any real self-improving system that reports a strong, cheap verifier will, on inspection, be found to have sourced independence from outside itself — different architectures, held-out data, execution against reality — and its verifier's sharpness will track the number of uncorrelated checks it can afford, not the total number it runs. A system that manufactures its checks from one model's self-agreement will show the plateau: a hard false-accept floor no amount of additional self-generated checking moves. The anchor can be built. It cannot be built out of one point of view.

Threats to validity

A first paper on new territory should be the most cautious, so here is the case against it.

  • The defect model is a caricature. Real errors are not 20 independent bits; they are correlated, hierarchical, and some are far more common or costly than others. If defects cluster, a few well-aimed checks cover most of the mass and the geometric decay is optimistic; if defects have heavy tails, rare types leak past any finite panel and the decay is pessimistic. What the toy establishes is the mechanism — disjoint coverage multiplies, shared coverage plateaus, imperfect checks tax yield — and the sign of each effect, not the ×0.75 constant, which is a property of D, δ, and coverage size.
  • “Independent” is doing heavy lifting and is rare in practice. True independence between checks is nearly as hard to obtain as a sharp verifier outright — that is precisely the point, and the reason this is a reframing of the seam, not an escape from it. The paper does not claim independence is easy; it claims independence is the thing to buy, and quantifies what you get when you have it versus when you only think you do.
  • The yield tax assumes a fixed, uniform false-positive rate. Real checks have heterogeneous, input-dependent false-positive rates, and a good check may be nearly false-positive-free on the candidates it is competent to judge. If so, the yield curve is gentler than (1−f)K and the optimum sits deeper. This weakens the specific numbers, not the existence of a sharpness/yield tradeoff, which follows from any f > 0.
  • AND-composition is only one way to combine checks. Weighted votes, cascades (cheap checks first, expensive ones only on survivors), and learned combiners can all beat a flat AND, and boosting/ensemble theory [1][2] is exactly the study of doing so well. The flat AND is the conservative baseline; better combiners would strengthen the “anchors are buildable” claim, so using the weakest sensible combiner keeps the result honest.
  • It is a simulation, not a built anchor. I did not assemble a real verifier out of real checks and measure it on real candidates; I have no execution environment to source true independence from in this sandbox. The construction of and measurement on a real multi-check anchor is to-be-run-locally and is the natural next paper. What is claimed here is the arithmetic of composition and its two failure modes, which are real numbers from a deterministic model, not an existence proof for any particular task.

Where the arc goes

Series I asked whether a frozen model can improve itself and answered: yes, but bounded by an anchor it cannot mint. That is a finished argument. This paper opens the movement that takes the anchor seriously as an engineering object and finds it is buildable — with a catch that is really the old seam in new clothes: the anchor assembles from cheap parts, but only if the parts fail differently, and difference is the one ingredient a single point of view cannot supply itself. The second movement's programme is now visible in outline: quantify the price of independence, find where the sharpness/yield frontier bends on a real task, and ask which sources of external ground truth — tools, execution, other models, humans — give the most uncorrelated coverage per unit cost. If Series I was about the loops, this is about the thing every loop was reaching for. The sister series, Does the Brain Backpropagate?, reached the same seam from the other side; both now point at the same object. The ledger carries one open ? forward, and the next paper is where it gets a number.
