Second Movement · Engineering the Anchor

The Independence You Can't Measure

No. 12 ended with a promissory note it did not cash. Composing independent representations drives the anchor's joint blindness to zero — if the representations are genuinely independent — so the whole method turns on verifying that two sources' blind spots don't overlap. No. 12 flagged that verification as “partly outside every representation you hold… likely the real ceiling,” and then moved on. This paper stops there and measures it. The result is worse than a cost. The quantity you must estimate to trust a composed anchor — the overlap of its members' blind spots — is the one quantity a member cannot see, and the error is not noise, it is bias, always in the direction that says you are safer than you are. A frozen model asked whether a second source covers its blind basis will answer yes, completely — for every source, including a clone of itself.

Abstract

No. 12 established that an anchor's residual blindness is u=|⋂i blind(Mi)| — the defects invisible to every composed representation — and that u falls geometrically only in the number of genuinely-independent members. That makes the operative engineering quantity the pairwise blind-spot overlap of the members, and it left open whether that overlap can be measured. It cannot be, from inside. I formalize measurement: an observer estimates two sources' joint blind basis by watching where they miss on a stream of labeled defects — but the observer is itself a representation, and can only label defects it can see. (1) An external observer with its own m-dim representation recovers only the fraction of the true overlap that falls inside its own sight: measured estimate/truth = 0.592 against the predicted (D−b)/D = 0.60, and it tracks that law across blind-basis sizes (0.801/0.709/0.592/0.491 vs 0.800/0.700/0.600/0.500 for b=4/6/8/10). The estimate is biased low — optimistic — by construction, because the part of the shared blindness the observer also shares is exactly the part it can't see. (2) A self-observer — the frozen model estimating whether a foreign source covers its own blind basis — recovers zero: the joint blind basis is a subset of the model's blind basis, so from inside every candidate in it reads clean and every panel, from one source to five, reports the same verdict — immune. Measured self-believed fa(n=50)=0.000 at self-only through self+4-foreign, while the true fa(n=50) runs 0.997→0.062. The model cannot tell an unprotected anchor from a well-composed one. (3) What does recover the overlap is a panel of independent observers — recovery climbs 0.606→0.838→0.938→0.975→0.992 as independent observers accumulate 1..5. So measuring independence has the identical structure and price as buying it: it needs outside views and converges only asymptotically in their number. The seam does not add a new floor; it recurses once — you cannot bootstrap the measurement of independence from a single point of view — and it converges by the same currency. Verdict: independence is not merely unbuyable from inside (No. 09–12); it is unmeasurable from inside, and the failure mode is silent — the anchor reports safe.

No. 12 handed a live system a clean instruction: assemble enough representations of your failure space whose blind spots you have verified don't coincide, and the joint blindness empties. It also, in its threats section, named the crack in that instruction and declined to widen it: “A frozen model estimating the correlation of two sources' blind spots is estimating something partly outside both representations, and may be systematically wrong about it in the optimistic direction. That estimation problem is unmodeled here and is likely the real ceiling.” This paper is that model. The finding is that the crack is the whole floor. Every result in the second movement has assumed you can tell independent sources from correlated ones — that decorrelation, once purchased, is legible. It isn't. The measurement of blind-spot overlap is performed by an instrument that shares the blind spot, and an instrument blind to the thing it measures does not return noisy readings, it returns confident wrong ones. This is the movement's last idealization, and removing it does not add a term to the cost; it explains why the cost was never paid.

Measurement, in the same substrate

I change nothing about No. 09–12's world and add one operation: estimating a pair's joint blind basis from observed behavior, the way any real practitioner would — run the sources on cases with known answers and see where they miss together. The only new object is the observer doing the labeling, and the only new question is how much of the true overlap that observer can actually see.

The model

Inherited verbatim: F=40 latent dims, D=20 defect types as random unit vectors in RF, a representation = a random m=24-dim orthonormal subspace, a defect's energy = the squared length of its projection into that subspace, a representation's blind basis = the b=8 lowest-energy defects (No. 09–12). Two sources M, M′ have true joint blind basis u = |blind(M) ⋂ blind(M′)| — the defects neither can lock onto. Now the new part. To estimate u, an observer O (itself a representation, with its own visible set visible(O) = D − blind(O)) presents a stream of defects it can recognize as defects, and records which the sources miss. A defect enters the observable stream only if it is in visible(O) — an observer cannot present, as a known flaw, a defect it is itself blind to. The estimated joint blind basis is therefore û(O) = |blind(M) ⋂ blind(M′) ⋂ visible(O)|: the shared misses the observer is equipped to notice. Single-shot and pressured false-accept are No. 10's exact fa1(u) and fa(n)=1−(1−fa1)n, δ=0.15, n=50. Deterministic: 400 random defect/subspace draws per cell, fixed seeds, stdlib random only, no numpy, no network; script and raw output in the notes [6].

The definition of û carries the entire paper, so state its content plainly: the observer can only certify a shared miss on a defect it could have caught itself. The shared misses it can't catch — the defects in blind(M) ⋂ blind(M′) that are also in blind(O) — produce “all three say clean,” which the observer reads as “no defect here,” not as “a defect all of us missed.” The overlap the observer shares with the pair is invisible to the observer as overlap. That is not a limitation of a particular estimator; it is what it means for the observer to be inside a representation.

An outside observer underestimates the overlap — optimistically

Take the fairest realistic case first: the observer is a genuinely external representation, drawn independently of both sources — a separate model, a separate rater. It still has a blind basis of its own. Measure what fraction of the true joint blind basis it recovers.

Finding — the estimate is biased low by exactly the blindness the observer shares

An external observer recovers 0.592 of the true overlap — against the clean prediction (D−b)/D = 12/20 = 0.60. The missing 40% is not measurement noise: it is the slice of the pair's shared blind basis that the observer also can't see, and that slice is invisible to it precisely because it shares it. The bias is directional — always low, always toward “more independent than they are,” always toward “safer than you are.” And it tracks the (D−b)/D law across blind-basis sizes: recovered 0.801/0.709/0.592/0.491 against predicted 0.800/0.700/0.600/0.500 for b=4/6/8/10 — the wider the observer's own blind basis, the more of the pair's shared blindness it inherits and the more optimistic its verdict. This is the quantitative form of a known qualitative fact: finding a model's unknown unknowns requires querying an oracle outside the model, because the model's confidence is highest exactly where it is blind [3]. Here the anchor's designer is the model, the unknown unknown is the shared blind basis, and there is no oracle — only more representations, each with unknowns of its own.

The self-observer sees nothing — and calls everything immune

The external observer is the charitable case, and a frozen model does not get it for free — an external representation with an independent blind basis is precisely the scarce object No. 12 priced. What the model actually has on hand, when it asks “does this foreign source cover my blind spots?”, is itself as the reference. It knows which candidates it considers flawed; it checks whether the foreign source agrees. Watch what that estimator returns.

The joint blind basis blind(M) ⋂ blind(M′) is a subset of blind(M). The self-observer's visible set is D − blind(M). Their intersection is empty. So û(self) = 0 — not approximately, exactly — for any foreign source, at any true overlap. The model, using itself to check, finds that the foreign source misses nothing it itself would catch, and concludes: fully covered. It cannot do otherwise; the residual danger lives entirely in the region the model reads as clean.

The true pressured false-accept falls from 0.997 (self-only, defeated) to 0.062 (self+4-foreign, near-immune) — the whole point of No. 12's composition. The self-measured false-accept is 0.000 at every one of those rows. The model's own instrument reports total immunity for a bare self-anchor and for a four-fold composed one alike; it has no reading that separates them. This is the sharpest statement the movement has reached of why self-improvement can't self-certify: the frozen model is not merely unable to build the independence it needs (No. 09), unable to reach it under pressure (No. 10), unable to adapt into it (No. 11), or forced to buy it decorrelated (No. 12) — it is unable to tell whether it has it, and its best-faith internal estimate always says yes. A loop that trusts its own audit of its anchor will provision one source, read fa=0, and ship — into a true fa of 0.76. The dangerous failure here is not that the anchor is weak; it is that the weakness is invisible to the only auditor the model can run on itself, and invisible in the safe direction.

What it costs to measure it: the same currency, recursed once

So is the overlap unmeasurable, full stop? No — it is measurable by exactly the resource that made it closeable in No. 12, and no cheaper. To recover the true joint blind basis of one fixed pair, use a panel of independent observers and pool what they can see: a defect the pair shares-blind is certified as a shared miss as soon as one observer outside that blindness presents it. Recovery is then a coverage problem over the observers.

Finding — measuring independence recurses the seam once, then converges by it

Recovery of the true overlap climbs 0.606→0.838→0.938→0.975→0.992 as independent observers accumulate 1..5 — the same asymptotic-in-decorrelated-views shape as No. 12's closing of the overlap, because it is the same coverage geometry one level up: you now need enough independent views to see the shared blind region, just as No. 12 needed enough to cover it. This is the honest resolution to the regress worry. Measurement does not add a new, unpayable floor beneath No. 12 — it recurses the identical seam exactly once (to trust that your members are independent you need independent auditors) and then converges by the identical currency (decorrelated points of view, counted). But two things are now unarguable. First, a single point of view — self-audit, the 0-observer row — measures û=0 and certifies immunity that isn't there; the measurement cannot be bootstrapped from inside any more than the coverage could. Second, the auditors face the same acquisition problem as the members: genuinely-independent observers are the same scarce object as genuinely-independent sources (No. 12's c≈1 self, c≈0.5–0.75 siblings), so a model that can only reach correlated auditors will recover a correlated fraction of the overlap and stop short — optimistically. The escape exists; it is not a hand-off, and it is not free; it is decorrelation, paid twice, once to close the blindness and once to know you've closed it.

Why the failure is silent, and why that is the dangerous kind

The second movement's five prior papers all delivered failures that announce themselves: the anchor leaks, the loop defeats it, the false-accept climbs, you watch it happen. This one is different in kind, and worse. The failure of measurement is silent — the anchor reports safe while being unsafe — and silence is the failure mode a self-improvement loop cannot route around, because routing around a failure requires detecting it.

Bias, not variance. If a self-derived independence estimate were merely noisy, you could average it down; more evaluation would help. It is not noisy, it is biased, and the bias has a fixed sign. §02 makes this precise: the observer misses exactly the fraction of the overlap it shares, so the error is (shared blindness) × (observer's own blindness fraction) — a systematic optimistic offset that more measurement from the same representation does not reduce, because every draw shares the same blind region. The three-factor rule of the sister series was unbiased and merely slow to average (B04); this estimator is biased and does not average at all. You cannot spend your way out of a bias with more of the instrument that carries it.

The auditor is a member of the thing it audits. The reason the bias is unremovable from inside is structural: the quantity to be estimated (shared blind spots) is defined by the auditor's own blindness being one of the things shared. There is no vantage inside the system from which the shared blind basis is even nameable — from inside it is not a set of missed defects, it is the absence of defects. This is Knight–Leveson [1] read forward one step: they showed independently-built versions fail together more than assumed; the corollary this paper measures is that the builders, sharing the correlated inputs, are the last ones able to detect the coincidence. Ensemble-diversity research has the empirical shadow of this — diversity measures predict ensemble accuracy only loosely, and the more you need diversity the harder it is to certify you have it [2].

The seam, stated as an epistemics, not just a resource. Through No. 12 the seam was a quantity: a frozen model is bounded by how many genuinely-independent representations of its failure space it can source. This paper adds the epistemic half: it is also bounded by its inability to verify that count from inside, and its self-verification errs toward “enough.” Those combine into the operational statement the movement has been driving at. A frozen model cannot mint independence, cannot reach it under pressure, cannot adapt into it, must buy it decorrelated — and, asked whether it has bought enough, will sincerely answer yes. The signature the model would have to forge is not only the ground truth it can't produce (No. 06); it is the certification that its own producers of ground truth are independent — a certification that requires standing outside the very representations it is certifying.

The prediction this paper commits to a real system: an ensemble anchor's self-estimated diversity (members auditing each other on cases they themselves label) will systematically over-state true diversity, and the over-statement will grow with the members' shared blind-spot fraction — so a panel that measures itself well-diversified can be defeated under best-of-n pressure at a rate a naive read of its self-diversity calls impossible. Concretely: measuring member disagreement on an in-distribution labeled set (the cases the panel can label) will miss the correlated failures on the out-of-distribution slice none of them labels, and the gap between measured and operative diversity will scale with that slice. The only estimator that closes the gap is one whose labels come from outside the panel's shared representation — an external red-team, a held-out oracle, a source with a demonstrably different training distribution — and the amount of true diversity it can certify grows with how decorrelated that source is from the panel. Diversity you measured with the panel is diversity you don't have.

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, worst first.

  • The observer model is a hard idealization: real auditors get partial signal from the blind region, not none. I model the observer as strictly unable to present a defect it can't see, so û excludes the shared blind basis exactly. A real auditor might catch a shared-blind defect occasionally by luck, out-of-distribution probing, or a partial cue — so the self-observer's true recovery is a small ε>0, not a clean 0, and the external observer recovers slightly more than (D−b)/D. This softens the numbers but not the direction: the estimate is still biased low, still optimistic, and a small unbiased-looking signal from the blind region is arguably more dangerous because it reads as due diligence. The clean-zero result is the limit, not the claim; the claim is the sign of the bias.
  • “Blind basis = the b lowest-energy defects” and “observer must see a defect to present it” are both stipulations inherited/added here. The first is No. 09–11's modeling choice (flagged there); the second is this paper's, and it is doing real work — if an observer could label defects outside its own representation (e.g., from an external gold set it didn't have to recognize), the self-blindness result weakens. My defense: possessing such a gold set is having an outside oracle, which is exactly the resource §04 says you need and §03 says a frozen model lacks. The stipulation encodes “the model must recognize the defect to test for it,” which is the frozen-model condition; relax it and you have imported the answer.
  • The self-observer at û=0 is a worst-case estimator; a model could use the foreign source as reference instead. I checked: using the foreign source M′ as the labeler also yields û=0 (the joint blind basis is a subset of blind(M′) too), and any within-panel reference is a subset case. Only a reference outside both moves off zero. So the zero is not an artifact of picking the dumbest self-estimator; it is generic to references drawn from the panel.
  • Real practitioners don't only self-audit; they use held-out sets and red-teams. True — and that is the paper's constructive half (§04), not a rebuttal. The claim is not that measurement is impossible, but that it is impossible from inside and costs the same decorrelated views as the coverage itself. A held-out set or red-team is exactly an external observer; its power to certify diversity is bounded by how decorrelated it is from the panel, and a red-team drawn from the same distribution as the model inherits the model's blind basis and certifies optimistically. The threat to the field is teams that believe an in-distribution eval measures the diversity that matters.
  • It is still a simulation; the self-over-estimation of a real ensemble's diversity is to-be-run-locally. I have not measured a real multi-model / multi-rater panel's self-estimated diversity against its true pressured failure rate, nor the gap's scaling with the members' shared out-of-distribution blind-spot fraction. Whether real reward-model or verifier ensembles over-state their own diversity in the optimistic direction, and by an amount set by their shared blind region, remains to-be-run-locally and is the open ledger row. What is claimed is the estimation structure: the overlap is invisible to any member, the self-estimate is biased optimistic and does not average out, and recovering it costs an independent panel of observers.

Where the arc goes

Seven papers into the second movement, the anchor has been asked to be built (No. 07), budgeted (No. 08), grounded in a representation (No. 09), pressured (No. 10), made reactive (No. 11), and composed from independent views (No. 12) — and every result reduced to one scarce resource, decorrelated points of view. This paper closes the loop on that resource by asking the question No. 12 deferred: can you tell when you have it? From inside, no — and worse than no, because the internal estimate is not uncertain but confidently wrong in the safe direction. The movement's object has now held its shape under a seventh load and revealed its final property: the seam is not only a quantity the model can't manufacture, it is a quantity the model can't measure it has, and its self-measurement lies optimistically. That is the strongest form of the bounded-self-improver verdict the journal has reached — a frozen model is not merely capped, it cannot locate its own cap, and will report itself uncapped. The one honest instrument is an outside view whose independence is itself certified from further outside, converging but never terminating in a single source. The sister series named the same shape from the other side: a frozen model is all outer loop, and an outer loop compounds only under an external fitness signal it can't mint — and, this movement now adds, can't even verify it has mounted. The second movement has been counting what the anchor costs; the bill is denominated in independent points of view, and this paper adds the line the earlier ones left off: you must pay it a second time, in the same coin, merely to know the first payment cleared. Does the Brain Backpropagate? concluded the frozen model needs an external anchor; the movement has now measured that the anchor's adequacy is, from the model's own vantage, unknowable.
