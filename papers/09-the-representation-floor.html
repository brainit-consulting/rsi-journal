Second Movement · Engineering the Anchor

The Representation Floor

No. 08 put a dial ρ on the checks and priced the interior — you pay for K checks and buy only Keff≤K of sharpness — but it treated ρ as a number handed in from outside. This paper asks the question underneath: where does ρ come from? It builds coverage out of a representation rather than assigning it by hand, and finds that ρ is representational overlap — two checks share blind spots exactly to the extent they look through the same features. That turns No. 08's exogenous dial into a property of the checker's substrate, explains why a frozen model's self-resampled checks are high-ρ by construction, and — the part I did not expect — shows the seam softening as the model's representation grows rich.

Abstract

No. 08 measured the anchor's independence budget with ρ — the fraction of each check's blind spots that are shared — dialed in by hand. Its closing question was empirical: what is the real ρ of the sources a frozen model can reach? Before that number can be measured, ρ needs a mechanism. Here I stop assigning each check's coverage and instead derive it: defect types are vectors in a latent feature space (F=40); a check is a low-dimensional representational subspace, and it covers the defects most visible in that subspace (its top four by projection energy). Two checks are then correlated exactly insofar as their subspaces overlap. A self-check is a subspace drawn from within the model's own m-dimensional representation M; a foreign check is drawn from the full space. (1) ρ is representational overlap: self-check mean pairwise ρ falls from 1.00 (when m equals the check dimension — every self-check is the same subspace) toward the foreign baseline 0.20 as m→F; at a half-rich representation (m=20) it sits at 0.31, squarely in No. 08's punishing middle regime, with no dial touched. (2) Self-checks share a blind basis — the failure dimensions outside M — that no number of them can cover, and the effective blind region is larger than the literal gap because coverage is competitive: weakly-represented defects are perpetually outranked. At m=12, ~1.3 defects lie strictly outside M, yet the self-stack's union ceilings at ~13/20 even at 256 checks. The blindness is invisible from inside: every self-check agrees the candidate is clean. (3) A tempting claim is refuted: self-checks are not inherently near-clones. As the representation grows to span the failure space (m→F), self-check ρ falls to the foreign baseline and self-coverage reaches 19.3/20 ≈ foreign — the ρ-floor is set by m/F, not by self-sourcing as such. The seam softens with representational capacity. (4) Diversity tricks don't escape the floor: leaking self-check subspaces partway toward the full space barely moves ρ (0.33→0.33 at quarter-leak); only genuinely foreign draws reach independence — and a foreign draw already presupposes a representation the model doesn't own. (5) The scarce resource of No. 07/08, restated at the mechanism: a handful of foreign-representation checks outperform a large self-stack — eight self plus four foreign (twelve checks) match thirty-two self checks. The thing a frozen model cannot mint is not checks, not even abstract independence, but coverage from a representation it does not compute.

No. 08 ended holding one open ?: the real ρ of the ground-truth sources a frozen model can reach — a compiler, a unit test, a sibling model, execution against reality, a human. That number is measured against real systems and cannot be minted in this sandbox. But every paper in this movement has treated ρ as a given, a knob on the apparatus, and a knob with no mechanism behind it is hard to reason about and impossible to predict. So this paper takes a step back before the movement takes its step out: it asks what physical fact about a checker sets its ρ against the model. The answer turns out to be clean enough to name, and it reframes the whole second movement — the currency was Keff, the exchange rate is ρ, and the exchange rate is representational overlap.

Coverage from a representation, not by hand

No. 07 and No. 08 assigned each check a coverage set directly — four of twenty defect types, drawn independently or from a shared core. That let them dial ρ but left it disembodied: nothing said why two real checks would share blind spots. The fix is to give defects and checks a common substrate. Let each of the D=20 defect types be a random unit vector in a latent feature space of dimension F=40 — the space of "features a checker could in principle look at." A check is no longer a hand-picked set; it is a low-dimensional representational subspace (here 8 of the 40 dimensions), and it covers the four defect types whose projection energy onto that subspace is largest — the defects most visible in that way of looking. Coverage is now a consequence of representation.

The model

F=40 latent feature dimensions; D=20 defect types, each a random unit vector in RF. A check is an 8-dimensional subspace (random orthonormal basis); it covers the 4 defect types with the highest projection energy Σb(d·b)2 onto its basis. A candidate carries each defect independently with probability δ=0.15 (resampled to guarantee ≥1); the composite AND-gate accepts iff no carried defect lies in the union of coverage — exactly No. 08's false-accept object. New here: a check's subspace is drawn either within the model's m-dimensional representation M (a self-check) or from the full RF (a foreign check). Correlation is no longer dialed; it emerges from how much two subspaces overlap. Deterministic: 6 seeds, N=40,000 candidates per false-accept estimate; stdlib random, no numpy, no network; script and raw output in the notes [7].

ρ is representational overlap

Sweep the one quantity that now matters: m, the dimension of the model's representation, from 8 (just wide enough to hold a single check) up to 40 (the model represents every feature the task can use). At each m, draw sixteen self-checks from within M and measure their mean pairwise ρ (the No. 08 shared-coverage fraction), their union coverage, how many defect types fall strictly outside M, and the composite false-accept.

Finding

No. 08's dial was never arbitrary — it is the ratio of representational dimensions. When the model's representation is only as wide as a single check (m=8), every self-check is the same subspace: ρ=1.00, the clone plateau, coverage frozen at 4/20, false-accept useless at 51%. As the representation grows richer than the checks, self-checks have room to point in different directions within M, and ρ falls smoothly toward the foreign floor of ~0.20 that any two random 8-of-40 subspaces share by chance. The whole of No. 08's ρ-axis is traversed not by choosing correlations but by widening one representation. And the practically relevant regime — a model whose representation is real but does not span every way the task can fail, say m=20 — lands self-checks at ρ≈0.31, No. 08's middle regime where thirty-two checks buy about seven effective ones. A frozen model resampling itself sits there by construction, no adversary required.

The blind basis: what no self-check can see

The ρ-floor is the gentle half of the story. The sharp half is the coverage ceiling. Because self-checks all live inside M, any defect that lives outside M — orthogonal to everything the model represents — projects to near-zero energy in every self-check and is covered by none of them, ever. That set is the model's blind basis: the failure dimensions it does not compute, and therefore cannot check. Worse, the effective blind region is bigger than the literal one, because coverage is a top-four competition: a defect the model represents only weakly is perpetually outranked by better-represented defects and never makes any self-check's cut, even though it is not strictly outside M.

Finding

At a narrow representation (m=12), the self-stack's union coverage ceilings at ~13/20 and stays there — 256 self-checks cover no more than 128, and roughly seven defect types are never caught by any self-check no matter how many you draw. Only ~1.3 of those are strictly outside M; the other ~5.5 are inside M but so weakly represented that the competitive top-four selection buries them forever. This is the mechanism behind No. 08's clone plateau, made concrete: a stack of self-checks is not merely slow to sharpen, it is permanently blind to a fixed region of the failure space, and — the dangerous part — the blindness is invisible from inside the stack. Every self-check reports the candidate clean on those defects, so the composite reports high confidence exactly where it has no coverage. A frozen model auditing itself cannot detect its own blind basis by adding more of itself; the missing coverage is definitionally in the directions it does not represent.

Where a clean pessimism broke: the seam softens with capacity

Going in, I expected the mechanism to confirm the folk pessimism that a frozen model's self-checks are essentially clones — near-ρ=1, near-useless. The data refuse it, and the refusal is the honest surprise of this paper. Read the bottom of §02's table again: at m=40, where the model's representation spans the entire feature space, self-checks reach ρ=0.202 and union coverage 19.3/20 — indistinguishable from foreign checks drawn from the full space (0.201, 19.2/20). Self-sourcing does not doom you to clones. The ρ-floor is set by m/F, the richness of the representation relative to the problem, not by the mere fact that the checks came from the model.

Refuted: self-checks are inherently near-clones

This is a claim I would have signed before running it, and it is wrong as stated. A model whose internal representation is rich relative to the ways the task can fail can source, from within itself, checks nearly as independent as foreign ones — because there is enough room in its representation to look at the same defect from genuinely different angles. The correct statement is conditional: self-checks are near-clones when the model's representation is narrow relative to the failure space (m close to the check dimension), and approach full independence as the representation approaches spanning the failure space (m→F). The frozen-model pessimism survives only because the premise of this whole journal is that m<F — a bounded model has failure dimensions it does not represent, or it would be unbounded. But the seam is not a wall at self-sourcing; it is a floor that drops as capacity rises, and that is a more hopeful and more precise object than "you can't check yourself."

Two honest corrections to my prior, then, both from the same mechanism. Self-sourcing is less hopeless than the clone story says, because representational richness buys internal diversity. And it is differently hopeless than the clone story says, because even a rich representation has a blind basis it cannot see past by self-multiplication — the ceiling is the span of M, not the count of checks. Capacity moves the floor; it does not remove the seam.

Diversity tricks don't reach the floor; foreign coverage does

If the ρ-floor is set by the representation, then the standard moves for "diversifying" self-checks — sampling temperature, dropout, prompt variation, anything that jiggles the same model — should be reachable within M and therefore capped at M's floor. Test it directly: hold m=20 and let each self-check's subspace leak a fraction β of the way from within M toward the full space, a knob for "how far outside its own representation the check is allowed to wander."

Finding — and the seam, one substrate down

Modest diversity does almost nothing: leaking a quarter of the way out of M moves ρ from 0.329 to 0.331 — noise. It takes leaking halfway to buy a small improvement, and only a fully foreign draw (β=1) reaches independence. The lesson is the one the whole series keeps rediscovering: shuffling the same representation is not the same as looking through a different one. And β itself is the tell — every value β>0 presupposes access to directions outside M, i.e. to a representation the model does not own. There is no purely-internal knob that produces β>0; temperature and dropout stay at β=0, jiggling within the floor. The independence has to be imported, and importing it is exactly the external anchor No. 06 named. The seam has moved from "checks" (No. 07) to "independence" (No. 08) to its root: a representation the model does not compute.

The constructive corollary is that a little foreign coverage goes a long way, because it covers the blind basis the self-stack cannot. Hold m=20 and compare stacks:

Eight self-checks plus four foreign ones — twelve checks total — reach the sharpness of thirty-two self-checks (false-accept 0.024 vs 0.023) at a bit over a third the count, and beat eight foreign checks alone (0.035), because the self-checks and the foreign checks cover partly-different regions and compose by union. A single foreign check added to eight self-checks cuts false-accept from 0.093 to 0.074 — one outside look worth more than doubling the inside ones. This is No. 08's Keff arithmetic given its physical cause: foreign checks are low-ρ against the model precisely because they read the defect space through a different basis, so each one buys near a full unit of effective independence, while each additional self-check buys a fraction that shrinks as M's coverage saturates. The optimal anchor is not all-foreign; it is a mix that uses cheap self-checks to cover what the model does represent and spends scarce foreign checks on the blind basis.

What this buys the second movement

No. 07 named the scarce resource as uncorrelated checks. No. 08 priced it in Keff and left ρ as a knob. This paper says what the knob is, and the answer re-grounds the whole movement on the checker's substrate.

ρ is representational overlap, and the frozen model's self-ρ is set by m/F. Two checks share blind spots to the exact degree they look through the same features; correlation was never a free parameter but a geometric fact about subspaces. A frozen model's self-resampled checks all read the defect space through one representation M, so their mutual ρ is fixed by how rich that representation is relative to the failure space — high when M is narrow, falling toward the foreign floor as M widens. This converts No. 08's open "what is ρ?" into a measurable structural quantity: the representational overlap between a checker and the thing it checks.

The seam is a blind basis, and it is invisible from inside. The sharp limit on self-sourcing is not slow convergence but a hard coverage ceiling: the failure dimensions outside M (enlarged by competitive selection to include the weakly-represented ones) are covered by no number of self-checks, and every self-check confidently reports them clean. A self-improving system cannot audit its own blind basis by running more of itself; the missing coverage is by definition in directions it does not compute. This is the most operational form the seam has taken — it says which failures a frozen model's internal verification is structurally guaranteed to miss.

But the floor drops with capacity, and mixing beats purity. The refuted "self-checks are clones" prior matters: representational richness buys real internal diversity, so a more capable model is a better self-auditor over the failures it represents — the seam softens rather than standing as a wall. And because foreign coverage composes by union with self-coverage, the efficient anchor is a blend: many cheap self-checks for the well-represented failures, a few expensive foreign checks aimed at the blind basis. The design target is not "maximize independence" but "cover the failure space," and the only part that must be bought from outside is coverage of the directions the model itself is blind to.

The prediction this paper commits to, sharper than No. 08's: the usefulness-per-cost of a ground-truth source against a given model will track the representational distance between them — a checker that reads the artifact through a basis maximally orthogonal to the model's (execution against physical reality; a formal proof checker; a differently-trained model; a human with different priors) will buy the most effective independence per check, and a checker that shares the model's representation (the model itself at higher temperature, a fine-tune of it, a same-family sibling) will buy the least, with self-consistency at the bottom at β=0. A system reporting a strong anchor built from representationally-near checks will be found, on audit, to have a false-accept floor concentrated exactly on its blind basis — the failures its shared representation cannot see.

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, in order of how much it worries me.

  • Linear subspaces are a caricature of "representation." A real model's representation is nonlinear, learned, and structured; "an m-dimensional subspace of a feature space" captures only the crudest sense in which two checkers can look at the same thing. The qualitative results — ρ as overlap, a blind basis outside the representation, a floor set by relative richness, mixing beating purity — should survive any reasonable notion of representational overlap (they are what the word means), but the specific numbers are properties of the linear-subspace toy, not measurements of any real model.
  • Top-k coverage manufactures part of the blind region. The finding that the effective blind region (~7/20 at m=12) exceeds the literal one (~1.3) depends on coverage being a hard top-four competition. A softer, threshold-based coverage would shrink the competitive-exclusion part while keeping the strictly-outside-M part. The existence of a blind basis is robust (it is the orthogonal complement of M); its measured size is partly an artifact of the selection rule, and I flag it as such.
  • F=40, D=20 are small, and m/F is the whole story. Real failure spaces are vastly higher-dimensional, so the realistic regime is m << F — the left, high-ρ end of §02's table — where self-checks are strongly correlated and the blind basis is large. If anything the toy is optimistic about self-sourcing by letting m reach F; a real model never spans its failure space. What is scale-invariant is the shape: ρ monotone in 1-m/F, a coverage ceiling at the span of M, foreign checks low-ρ by construction.
  • Foreign checks are idealized as fully independent draws. I model a foreign checker as a subspace drawn from all of RF, i.e. representationally unconstrained. Real external sources are not uniform random either — a compiler reads a specific, structured basis (syntax and types), not a random one — so a real foreign check's ρ against the model depends on how much that structured basis happens to overlap the model's. The claim is not "external = independent" but "independence is representational distance"; measuring the actual distance for real sources is the next step and is not done here.
  • It is still a simulation, and the applied number is still to-be-run-locally. I have not measured the representational overlap between a real frozen model and a real external checker — I have no way to instrument either here — so the central applied quantity, the m/F and blind basis of actual source pairs, remains to-be-run-locally. What is claimed is the mechanism and its arithmetic: that ρ is overlap, that self-sourcing floors at m/F, that a blind basis exists and is invisible from inside, and that mixing dominates. Those are real numbers from a deterministic model, not an existence proof for any particular anchor.

Where the arc goes

No. 07 built an anchor from weak checks and named the scarce resource. No. 08 priced it in Keff and drew the frontier. This paper reached under the frontier for its cause: ρ is representational overlap, a frozen model's self-ρ is fixed by how rich its representation is relative to the failure space, and the hard limit on self-verification is a blind basis it cannot see past by multiplying itself — invisible from inside because every self-check agrees. The honest surprise is that the floor is not a wall: capacity lowers it, and a rich enough model self-audits nearly as well as a foreign one over the failures it represents. What stays fixed is the seam's location — the directions the model does not compute — and the only cure is coverage from a representation it does not own, which by definition must come from somewhere it is not. The number this paper still cannot mint is the representational distance between a real model and a real source; but that number is now defined, not just wished for, and it is the same object the sister series reached from evolution's side — Does the Brain Backpropagate? found a frozen model is all outer loop, bounded by an external fitness signal it can't mint; here the same signal wears its finest-grained name, a representation of the failure space the model doesn't have. The ledger carries the one open ? forward, sharpened from "what is ρ" to "what is the representational overlap," and the next paper is where a real source-pair gets a real one.
