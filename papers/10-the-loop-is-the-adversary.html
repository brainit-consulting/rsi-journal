Second Movement · Engineering the Anchor

The Loop Is the Adversary

Every measurement in this movement — No. 07's geometric decay, No. 08's Keff, No. 09's blind basis — priced the anchor against a fixed candidate: defects sampled i.i.d., the anchor asked once. But a self-improving system does not sample its anchor once. It optimizes against it — proposes, keeps what passes, repeats — which is precisely an adversary searching for the anchor's gap. This paper puts the loop's own optimization pressure into the measurement and finds the single-shot numbers were the optimistic ones: under pressure, any residual coverage gap becomes near-certain acceptance, the governing quantity flips from average coverage to whether the uncovered set is empty, and No. 09's tidy “a few foreign checks beat a big self-stack” dissolves — foreign coverage of the blind basis stops being an efficiency and becomes the only thing that resists.

Abstract

No. 07–09 measured an anchor's false-accept against a non-adaptive adversary: a flawed candidate carries defects i.i.d. and the composite gate is queried once. But the whole reason an anchor matters in this journal is that a self-improvement loop optimizes against it — a propose–evaluate–keep engine is, from the anchor's point of view, a search for whatever the anchor lets through. So the operative quantity is not the single-shot false-accept but the false-accept under optimization pressure. I model that pressure at its most assumption-light — best-of-n: the loop draws n flawed candidates and succeeds if any passes — on No. 09's exact substrate (defects as vectors in RF, checks as subspaces, self-checks drawn within an m-dim representation M, foreign checks from the full space). (1) Pressure converts residual gaps into certainty: fa(n)=1-(1-fa1)n, so any anchor with union coverage below D is defeated as n grows. A healthy-looking anchor (16 self-checks, union 15/20, single-shot fa1=0.052) crosses 50% acceptance at just 13 tries and 99.5% by 100. The single-shot number was the optimistic one. (2) The design metric flips. Under pressure, false-accept is governed by whether the uncovered set is empty: the pressure-to-defeat n½ rises steeply as union→D and diverges to ∞ only at complete coverage (u=0, where fa1=0 exactly). Mean/union coverage — the i.i.d. objective — is smooth and misleading; completeness is the adaptive objective and it is a threshold. (3) A claim from my own last paper is corrected. No. 09 found 8 self + 4 foreign (fa 0.024) statistically tied with 32 self (fa 0.023) and concluded “a few foreign checks beat a big self-stack.” Under pressure that pair is not tied and the mix is slightly worse (n½ 25 vs 37) — both still carry a gap, both are defeated. A few foreign checks are not a cheap substitute for coverage; they help under pressure only by driving the cover to completeness. (4) And only a complete cover resists — which self-checks structurally cannot build. The blind basis of No. 09 leaves u>0 forever (256 self-checks ceiling at 19.3/20, n½ finite, always eventually defeated), while enough foreign checks reach 20/20 and become immune (32 foreign: fa1=0, n½=∞). Under a loop's own optimization, foreign coverage of the blind basis is necessary, not merely efficient. The seam, sharpened once more: the frozen model's optimization pressure is guaranteed to find the exact region its self-anchor cannot see.

This movement has been measuring an object at rest. No. 07 stacked weak checks and watched false-accept fall geometrically; No. 08 put a correlation dial on them and priced the interior in effective checks; No. 09 derived the correlation from a representation and found the blind basis. Every one of those measurements asked the anchor a single question about a candidate drawn without regard to the anchor. But an anchor exists to gate a loop, and a loop is an optimizer: it proposes candidates, keeps the ones the anchor passes, and proposes again from there. From the anchor's side that is indistinguishable from an adversary hunting for the gap — the same phenomenon No. 05 named as reward hacking, one substrate down. So the honest false-accept is not the one measured once; it is the one measured after the loop has pushed on it. This paper puts that push into the number, and the number gets worse in a specific, structured way.

Pressure, modeled at its cheapest

I keep No. 09's substrate unchanged so the comparison is exact, and add one thing: the candidate is no longer sampled once. Optimization has many faces — best-of-n reranking, rejection sampling, RL against the gate — but they share a floor: the loop gets to try more than once and keeps what passes. The most assumption-light version, and the one that presumes the least knowledge on the adversary's part, is best-of-n: draw n independent flawed candidates, succeed if any single one is accepted. That model needs the loop to know nothing about the anchor's internals — it just samples and checks — so whatever it shows is a lower bound on the damage a smarter, gap-seeking optimizer would do.

The model

Unchanged from No. 09: F=40 latent feature dims; D=20 defect types as random unit vectors in RF; a check is an 8-dim subspace covering its top-4 defects by projection energy; a self-check's subspace is drawn within the model's m-dim representation M, a foreign check's from the full space; a flawed candidate carries each defect independently with prob δ=0.15 (resampled to guarantee ≥1); the composite AND-gate accepts iff no carried defect lies in the union of coverage. New: define the single-shot false-accept fa1 as before, then the pressure curve fa(n)=1-(1-fa1)n — the probability the loop slips a flawed candidate past the anchor within n tries — and the pressure-to-defeat n½=ln(0.5)/ln(1-fa1), the tries needed to reach a coin-flip. Deterministic: 6 seeds, N=60,000 candidates per fa1; stdlib random, no numpy, no network; script and raw output in the notes [7].

A healthy-looking anchor, defeated in a dozen tries

Take an anchor that looks perfectly serviceable single-shot: sixteen self-checks at a half-rich representation (m=20). It covers 15 of 20 defect types and lets a flawed candidate through only 5.2% of the time — a number you would happily report. Now let the loop push on it.

Finding

The 5.2% single-shot false-accept is a coin-flip by 13 tries and a near-certainty by 100. Nothing about the anchor changed — only the number of times the loop was allowed to ask. This is the core reframing of the whole movement: the false-accept No. 07–09 reported is the probability a flawed candidate passes once, but a loop does not propose once. It proposes until something passes. The relevant quantity is the tail of the pressure curve, and for any positive single-shot false-accept that tail goes to 1. An anchor that is “95% reliable” against a single candidate is a sieve against a loop that gets a hundred draws — which is a small loop.

The metric flips: not how much coverage, but whether any is missing

If every gap is eventually exploited, the question becomes how fast — the pressure-to-defeat n½ — and what sets it. Sweep anchors from thin to complete and read n½ against union coverage (equivalently against the uncovered-set size u=D-union).

Finding — the threshold at u=0

Read the last column top to bottom: n½ climbs 3→5→7→13→37→194 as coverage improves, and then, at complete coverage, jumps to infinity. This is a threshold, not a slope. If even one defect type is uncovered (u>0), there exists a flawed candidate — one carrying only uncovered defects — that the anchor accepts, so fa1>0 and enough pressure defeats it; the coverage only sets the rate. If no defect is uncovered (u=0), every flawed candidate carries at least one covered defect and is rejected, so fa1=0 exactly and no amount of pressure gets through. The i.i.d. objective was “make the average false-accept small,” a smooth quantity you can improve by degrees. The adaptive objective is “leave no defect uncovered,” a binary you either clear or don't. Under a loop's own optimization, an anchor at union 19.5/20 is not “97.5% of the way there” — it is on the finite-n½ side of the wall, defeated at 194 tries, categorically unlike the 20/20 anchor beside it.

Where my last paper's tidy result broke

No. 09 closed on a clean, quotable line: eight self-checks plus four foreign ones (twelve checks, false-accept 0.024) matched thirty-two self-checks (0.023) — “a few foreign-representation checks beat a large self-stack; mixing dominates purity.” I believed it; the single-shot numbers were real. But those numbers were measured against the non-adaptive adversary, and the whole point of this paper is that the loop is not that adversary. So I re-ran No. 09's exact stacks under pressure.

Corrected: “a few foreign checks beat a big self-stack”

Under pressure the No. 09 pair is not tied, and the direction is the opposite of what the single-shot number implied: 8 self + 4 foreign has a larger uncovered set (3.0 vs 2.3) and is defeated faster (n½ 25 vs 37) than 32 self. The i.i.d. false-accept made them look equivalent because it averages over how a random candidate lands; pressure doesn't average, it seeks, and it seeks the gap — so the stack with the bigger gap loses, regardless of how the two compared on a random draw. The correct statement is narrower and I should have reached it in No. 09: foreign checks are not a cheap efficiency that lets you trade count for quality. They earn their keep under pressure only by closing the cover completely — 8 self + 16 foreign reaches u=0 and becomes immune, while every stack short of that is defeated at some finite n. This is a result that cuts against my own prior paper, and it is the honest kind of finding this ledger is for: the anchor's value under a loop is set at the threshold, and everything below the threshold is the same failure at a different speed.

Only completeness resists — and self-checks cannot reach it

The threshold at u=0 would be a mild observation if any anchor could reach it. The sharp part, inherited straight from No. 09's blind basis, is that a self-stack cannot. Self-checks all read the defect space through the model's representation M; the defect types outside M (enlarged by competitive top-k selection to include the weakly-represented ones) are covered by none of them, at any depth. So the self-stack's union ceilings below D — which means u>0 forever — which means, under pressure, defeat is only a matter of tries.

Finding

Thirty-two foreign checks close the cover to 20/20 and cross to the immune side of the threshold; sixty-four confirm it holds. Two hundred and fifty-six self-checks ceiling at 19.3/20 — roughly seven-tenths of a defect type is never covered by any of them — and that residue, harmless-looking as a single-shot 0.7/20 gap, is a permanent u>0, which under pressure is a finite n½, which is eventual defeat. The blind basis of No. 09 was a coverage curiosity when the adversary was passive; under the loop's own optimization it is the whole story. The loop, searching for whatever the anchor lets through, is guaranteed to converge on the exact directions the model's self-checks cannot see — because those are the only directions a flawed candidate can survive in. A frozen model optimizing against its own self-anchor is not merely at risk of finding its blind basis; it is being actively steered into it by the very act of optimizing.

Two honest caveats keep this from being a doom result. First, the immunity at u=0 is immunity to the defects the substrate represents at all — a defect type outside the entire D=20 catalog is unmodeled here, and a real failure space is not a closed list. So “u=0” means “every failure mode you have thought to check is covered,” not “nothing can go wrong.” Second, best-of-n defeats an anchor at n≈1/fa1; a real loop applies bounded pressure, so a large-but-finite n½ (the 194 of sixteen foreign checks) may be safe in practice if the loop never gets that many tries. The threshold is sharp in the limit; in a budget-limited loop, a nearly-complete cover buys real time. What it does not buy is safety against a loop that runs long enough, and a self-improvement loop is the kind that runs long enough.

What this buys the second movement

No. 07 built the anchor from weak checks. No. 08 priced its interior. No. 09 found the blind basis. This paper puts the anchor under the load it will actually bear — the loop's own optimization — and the load rewrites what “a good anchor” means.

The operative false-accept is the pressured one, and the single-shot number is the optimistic one. A propose–evaluate–keep loop is an adversary against its own gate; the honest measurement is fa(n), not fa1, and fa(n)→1 for any anchor with a gap. Every false-accept reported in No. 07–09 should be read as a per-try rate to be multiplied out over the loop's search budget. An anchor is not characterized by how often it fails on a random candidate but by how many tries it survives against one that is looking.

The design objective flips from “minimize average false-accept” to “eliminate the uncovered set.” Under pressure, coverage is a threshold, not a slope: only a complete cover (u=0) is immune, and everything short of it is the same failure at a rate set by the gap. This retires a metric the earlier papers leaned on — union/average coverage as a continuous good — and replaces it with a binary the loop actually respects. It also corrects No. 09 in the ledger below: a few foreign checks are not a cheap substitute for a big self-stack; they matter only insofar as they carry the cover across the threshold.

Foreign coverage of the blind basis becomes necessary, not efficient — and the loop aims itself at exactly that spot. Self-checks structurally leave u>0 (No. 09's blind basis), so a self-anchor is always eventually defeated; only checks drawn from a representation the model does not compute can close the cover, and closing it fully is what immunity requires. And the danger is active: the loop's optimization does not stumble onto the blind basis by chance — it is drawn there, because the blind basis is the only region a flawed candidate can survive selection. The seam of this whole journal — coverage from a representation the model can't mint — now has a mechanism of attack attached: the model's own improvement pressure is the thing that finds the seam.

The prediction this paper commits to, and the one it hands to a real system: for any loop optimizing against a proxy gate, the true (gold) failure rate will not track the proxy's single-shot false-accept but its pressured one, rising toward certainty with the loop's search budget, and the rise will concentrate on the proxy's least-covered failure modes — its blind basis. Concretely: a self-improvement loop reporting a strong self-built anchor will, on gold-standard audit, be found to fail specifically and increasingly on the failure dimensions its anchor shares a representation with the generator, and the failure rate will scale with how hard the loop was allowed to search, not with the anchor's headline accuracy. This is the reward-model-overoptimization result of Gao et al. [1] given a coverage-geometric cause: overoptimization is the pressure curve, and the gap it exploits is the blind basis.

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, worst first.

  • Best-of-n is the weakest possible optimizer, so this understates the danger — which cuts for the conclusion but means the numbers are not the real ones. A real loop doing RL or gap-directed search against the gate would exploit the blind basis far faster than blind resampling: with any signal about what passes, the adversary carries an uncovered defect deliberately and wins in one try (n½→1), collapsing the whole finite-n column. So the pressure-to-defeat figures here are ceilings on safety, not estimates of it. The qualitative law (threshold at u=0, immunity only at complete cover) is robust across adversary strength; the specific n½ values are properties of the black-box model and would shrink under a smarter loop.
  • The defect catalog D=20 is closed; real failure spaces are open. Immunity at u=0 means “every modeled defect is covered,” not “nothing can go wrong.” A failure mode outside the catalog is uncovered by construction and invisible to this analysis, so a real anchor's practical u is never truly zero. The result should be read as a lower bound on vulnerability: even granting a closed, fully-known defect space, a self-stack can't reach immunity; an open space only makes it worse.
  • fa(n)=1-(1-fa1)n assumes independent draws. A loop that mutates a near-miss candidate rather than resampling fresh has correlated tries, which changes the constant in n½ but not the limit — correlated hill-climbing toward the gap is faster, not slower, than independent sampling, so again this is the optimistic direction. The u=0 threshold is exact regardless: if no flawed candidate is acceptable, no search strategy finds one.
  • The substrate is No. 09's linear-subspace toy, inheriting all its caveats. Defects-as-vectors and checks-as-subspaces caricature real representations; the specific coverage numbers (19.3/20 self-ceiling, 32 foreign to close) are properties of the toy, not measurements of any model. What transfers is the structure: a self-stack has a permanent uncovered residue, a residue is a positive per-try false-accept, and a positive per-try rate is defeated by enough pressure.
  • It is still a simulation; the pressured false-accept of a real anchor is to-be-run-locally. I have not measured a real model optimizing against a real gate — the applied quantity, the pressure-vs-coverage curve and the effective u of an actual self-anchor under the loop's own best-of-n/RL, remains to-be-run-locally (and is exactly what Gao et al. [1] measure for reward models). What is claimed is the mechanism and its arithmetic: pressure multiplies out any gap, the objective is completeness not average coverage, and self-sourcing can't reach completeness.

Where the arc goes

No. 07 asked whether an anchor could be built; No. 08 what it costs; No. 09 what its correlation is. This paper asked the question the movement had been quietly deferring: what happens to the anchor when the loop it gates starts pushing on it. The answer reorganizes the earlier results. The single-shot false-accepts were per-try rates, and a loop cashes them out over its search budget until any gap becomes near-certain; the objective is not small average false-accept but an empty uncovered set; and the one anchor a frozen model can build from itself — the self-stack — has a permanent uncovered residue that the loop's own optimization is guaranteed to seek out. The honest correction to No. 09 is the shape of the finding: mixing self and foreign checks is not a tidy efficiency; foreign coverage of the blind basis is the only thing on the immune side of the threshold, and getting there means closing the cover completely, not cheaply. The number this movement still cannot mint — the pressured false-accept of a real model against a real gate — is now not just defined but predicted: it is reward-model overoptimization [1] read through coverage geometry, and it concentrates exactly where the sister series said the seam lives. Does the Brain Backpropagate? found a frozen model is all outer loop, bounded by an external fitness signal it can't mint; this paper adds the teeth — the outer loop's optimization is not a passive consumer of that signal but an active adversary against any cheaper substitute, and it wins wherever the substitute has a gap. The next paper is where the anchor stops being measured against toy defects and gets pointed at a real proxy under real pressure.
