Series II · Does the Brain Backpropagate?

Evolution as the Outer Loop

B02–B04 all closed on the same beat: the brain's bio-plausible rules are honest gradient estimators, but they trail backprop by a cost that grows with scale. Each time, the consolation was the same word — evolvable. This paper finally interrogates that consolation. Evolution is the outer loop that discovered the brain's learning rule in the first place; the wager is whether running evolution as the optimizer buys you something backprop can't. I measured the most-cited evolutionary optimizer, Evolution Strategies, against the true gradient — and found it is the same dimension-scaling estimator as B04's three-factor rule, wearing a population instead of a neuromodulator.

Abstract

Three papers running, the brain's demonstrated and theorised learning rules have come out the same: unbiased gradient estimators that pay a variance tax which scales with the size of the system being taught (B03's depth, B04's width). Every time, the standing defense of the brain has been that its rules are evolvable — reachable by blind search where backprop's exact, weight-transporting machinery is not. B05 puts that defense on the stand. I take the canonical scalable evolutionary optimizer, Evolution Strategies (Salimans et al. 20171), which its own authors frame as a gradient estimator, and measure how well its update points along the true gradient at a fixed parameter point, as a function of parameter dimension D. The result is a near-exact rhyme with B04. ES is unbiased: grow the population and the cosine to the true gradient climbs to 1.0 (0.998 at D=2). But a small population is nearly orthogonal to the gradient in high dimension (cosine 0.055 at D=128, one mirrored pair), and the population needed to reach a fixed direction quality grows with dimension — P* climbed 1→8→18→62→210 as D went 2→8→32→128→512 (4 seeds), a log–log slope of ~0.8. This is the same pathology, relocated: B04's scalar neuromodulator shared one number across N units; ES shares one scalar fitness across D parameters. Evolution-as-a-weight-optimizer therefore does not escape the sample-efficiency wall — it is the wall, derived from first principles, and the empirical literature agrees (ES needs ~thousands of CPUs to match a single backprop learner on RL1). The honest niche is different and real: evolution's edge is not efficiency on a differentiable loss but the ability to optimize non-differentiable outer-loop objects — the learning rule itself, the architecture, the reward, the curriculum — where no gradient exists to descend59, and to optimize for robustness by smoothing the landscape it searches10. That reframes the wager precisely: evolution is a bad inner loop and a uniquely capable outer one. B06 settles whether that division of labour helps or hinders self-improving AGI.

B04 ended pointing here. Its closing sentence said the brain's demonstrated rule is "a robustness-and-evolvability play," and promised that B05 would ask whether evolution as the outer loop "can turn that evolvability into an advantage." This is the paper that has to either cash that promise or admit it was rhetoric. The discipline of this journal says: do not let "evolvable" do load-bearing work until you have measured what evolution actually costs. So I measured it.

What "evolution as the outer loop" actually names

The phrase compresses three distinct things that are worth separating, because they fail and succeed for different reasons.

(1) Evolution as a weight optimizer. Replace backprop with a population-based search over the weights themselves. Salimans et al. (2017)1 popularised Evolution Strategies (ES) for this on deep RL: perturb the parameter vector with Gaussian noise across a population, weight each perturbation by the scalar reward it earned, and step in the reward-weighted average direction. Such et al. (2017)2 showed even a dumb Deep GA (mutate, select, no gradient at all) can train million-parameter Atari networks. These are the headline "evolution trains deep nets" results — and they are exactly the regime I will show inherits the scaling wall.

(2) Evolution as a rule/architecture optimizer. Don't evolve the weights — evolve the thing that sets the weights. NEAT3 evolved network topologies; regularized evolution discovered AmoebaNet, an image classifier competitive with hand-designed architectures4; and most relevant to this series, Najarro & Risi (2020)8 evolved local Hebbian plasticity coefficients in random networks — evolution discovering a B04-style local learning rule from scratch. Here the object being optimized is not differentiable by any standard means, which is the whole point.

(3) Evolution as the generator of the whole problem. Clune's (2019) AI-GAs5 thesis: an outer evolutionary loop that simultaneously invents architectures, learning algorithms, and the environments/curricula that make them learn — open-endedly, as POET9 co-evolves agents and the obstacle courses that stretch them. This is the maximalist "evolution as the outer loop of AGI" claim the series' wager is really about.

The mistake this paper exists to prevent is letting the genuine success of (2) and (3) launder the cost of (1). They are not the same bet. So before any verdict, the measurement.

ES is a gradient estimator — said by its authors

The cleanest way to see why evolution-as-weight-optimizer inherits B04's wall is that ES is not an alternative to gradient descent in the way folklore suggests — it is a particular, zeroth-order way of estimating the same gradient. Salimans et al. state it outright: ES performs stochastic gradient ascent on the Gaussian-smoothed objective η(θ) = Eε[F(θ+σε)], with the estimator

The ES update, stated precisely

∇θ η(θ)  =  (1/σ) · Eε∼N(0,I)[ F(θ+σε) · ε ]

Sample a population of perturbations εk, evaluate the scalar fitness F of each perturbed parameter vector, and form the reward-weighted average direction. No backward pass, no derivatives, no weight transport — only scalar fitnesses and the noise that produced them. Structurally this is identical to B04's node-perturbation three-factor rule (pre × ξ × M): a global scalar signal times the perturbation that earned it. B04 perturbed N hidden units; ES perturbs D parameters. Same estimator, different noun.

The instant you write it this way, B04's result predicts B05's. A scalar-gated perturbation estimator is unbiased — in expectation it points along the (smoothed) gradient — but its variance grows with the number of things one scalar has to disambiguate. B04 found the cost scales with hidden width N; the classical theory of random gradient-free optimization (Nesterov & Spokoiny 20176) says the same for ES, with the relevant count being the parameter dimension D. I wanted to see it in the sandbox, on the actual ES estimator, not take it on authority.

A measurement: ES is B04's estimator wearing a population

I built a clean, differentiable target so I have an exact gradient to compare against: a random positive-definite quadratic F(θ) = ½ θTAθ, whose true gradient is exactly Aθ (no finite-difference error anywhere). At a fixed point θ I formed the antithetic ES estimate — mirrored pairs ±σε, scalar fitnesses only, σ=10−3 so the smoothing is negligible and ES is estimating the true gradient — and measured its cosine to Aθ, sweeping the parameter dimension D and the population P (number of mirrored pairs). The question is the same one B04 asked of the neuromodulator: how well does one scalar-driven update point downhill, and how does that decay as the system grows?

Read it exactly as you read B04's table, because it is the same table. First, ES is unbiased. Every row climbs toward 1.0 as the population grows: 0.998 at D=2, P=256. Salimans' framing is vindicated — ES really is doing stochastic gradient ascent, the population average converging on the true gradient. Second, a small population is almost orthogonal to the gradient in high dimension. At one mirrored pair the cosine collapses from 0.912 (D=2) to 0.055 (D=128) — an angle of ~87°. One scalar-weighted perturbation barely points downhill, because a single scalar cannot disambiguate which of 128 coordinates deserved the credit. ES substitutes population for coordinates exactly as B04's rule substituted trials for them.

The population cost grows with dimension at a log–log slope of ~0.8 over this range — a 64× increase in D (8→512) costs a ~26× increase in population. It is sub-linear here at small scale (antithetic sampling and a well-conditioned quadratic both help), but it is unmistakably growing with size, and Nesterov–Spokoiny's analysis6 says the worst-case dependence is linear in D. This is B04's Werfel–Xie–Seung result7 rederived in evolution's own currency: where backprop reads off all D gradient coordinates in one backward pass, ES must average a population that grows with D to reconstruct the same direction. The brain's scalar neuromodulator and the evolutionary population are the same point in algorithm space, and it is not backprop's point.

Threats to validity — read before generalising

(1) The quadratic is generous to ES. A well-conditioned PD quadratic is the friendliest possible landscape; the ~0.8 slope is a lower bound on the pain, not an upper one. On the rugged, ill-conditioned losses of real deep nets the dimension dependence is worse, which is why the empirical ES papers burn thousands of CPU-cores1. (2) I measured update direction at a fixed point, not a training run. The unbiasedness and the dimension-scaling are what I assert; convergence speed of a full optimization adds its own factors. (3) ES is not only a finite-difference gradient estimator. Lehman et al. (2018)10 show ES optimizes the smoothed objective η, giving it a real and distinct inductive bias toward robust, flat-basin solutions — a feature, not a bug, and the seed of §05. My measurement deliberately uses tiny σ to isolate the gradient-estimator behaviour; at the larger σ real ES uses, it is estimating a different (smoothed) target on purpose. (4) The script (numpy, fixed seeds, exact closed-form gradient) is in the repo at scratchpad/es_outer_loop.py; re-running with larger D and ill-conditioned A is the obvious extension and should steepen the slope toward 1.0.

So why does evolution win anything? Because the loss has no gradient

If ES is just a dimension-taxed gradient estimator, the headline results look paradoxical: how did Deep GA train Atari nets, how did regularized evolution find AmoebaNet? The resolution is that in every case where evolution genuinely wins — rather than merely keeps up at great cost — the object being optimized is one where no usable gradient exists.

Architecture is the clearest case. The map from "discrete graph of layers and connections" to "validation accuracy after training" is not differentiable in the architecture; you cannot backprop through the choice to add a skip connection. So architecture search is natively a black-box optimization problem, and evolution is a strong black-box optimizer — regularized evolution4 matched or beat reinforcement-learning NAS on exactly this. The learning rule is the same kind of object, and the most series-relevant result is Najarro & Risi (2020)8: they fixed random network weights and let evolution optimize only the local Hebbian plasticity coefficients, producing networks that self-organize to a task at test time. That is evolution discovering a B04-style three-factor/Hebbian rule from scratch — the outer loop literally inventing the inner loop's learning rule, an object backprop cannot differentiate end-to-end without exactly the meta-machinery evolution sidesteps.

This is the honest reframing of every "evolution as the outer loop" claim. Evolution does not beat backprop at descending a differentiable loss — my measurement shows it can't, by construction, at scale. It wins where backprop has nothing to descend: non-differentiable, discrete, or self-referential outer-loop objects — architectures, learning rules, reward functions, curricula, the open-ended generation of new problems (AI-GAs5, POET9). The division of labour is sharp: gradient methods for the inner loop (the weights), evolution for the outer loop (everything that defines the inner loop) — precisely because the inner loop is differentiable and the outer loop usually is not.

The robustness niche, and the honest case against the thesis

There is a second, subtler thing evolution buys, and intellectual honesty requires foregrounding it because it cuts against my own "ES is just a taxed gradient estimator" framing. Lehman et al. (2018)10 argue — correctly — that ES is "more than a traditional finite-difference approximator": because it ascends the Gaussian-smoothed objective, it is biased toward parameter regions that are good on average under perturbation, i.e. flat, wide, robust basins rather than sharp brittle optima. That is a genuinely different and often better inductive bias than raw gradient descent for control and robustness, and it is invisible to my tiny-σ measurement, which deliberately strips the smoothing away to expose the estimator underneath. Novelty- and quality-diversity methods11 push this further: evolution can optimize for behavioral diversity and open-ended novelty, objectives that are not even scalar losses and have no gradient in any sense.

So the case against this paper's deflationary read is real: "ES = dimension-taxed gradient estimator" is true of its mean and incomplete as a description of what it's for — exactly the mean-vs-mechanism distinction B04 drew, now pointing the other way. B04 warned against over-crediting the brain's rule by reading its unbiased mean as backprop's mechanism. B05's symmetric warning is against under-crediting evolution by reading its taxed mean as its only purpose. The smoothing bias and the non-differentiable reach are not rounding errors on a bad gradient estimator; for the objects evolution is actually pointed at, they are the entire value proposition. The measurement bounds what evolution costs as a weight optimizer; it says nothing against what evolution is for as an outer loop.

What this means for the wager

The series' wager is whether the brain's local, evolution-discovered learning rule is a better substrate for self-improving, evolution-based AGI than backprop. B05 sharpens it into a clean division, and the division cuts both ways honestly.

Against the brain / against evolution-as-optimizer: as the loop that actually sets the weights, evolution is strictly dominated by backprop wherever a gradient exists, and the domination grows with scale — my D-scaling is the same wall B03 found in depth and B04 found in width, now in evolution's own population currency. A system trying to bootstrap itself to ever-larger capability by evolving its weights is paying an escalating tax precisely as it gets bigger. This is the third independent confirmation of the same scale gap, and it should be weighted accordingly: it is not one finding, it is a pattern.

For the brain / for evolution-as-outer-loop: the brain's learning rule was found by an outer evolutionary loop, and that is the one job evolution is uniquely good at — optimizing the non-differentiable object (the rule, the architecture, the reward) that defines the differentiable inner loop. The brain's scalar-feedback rule isn't competing with backprop on gradient throughput; it is the inner loop that an outer evolutionary loop could discover, tune, and embed in noisy, asynchronous, weight-transport-free hardware. The two scale gaps stop being a refutation and become a specialization: evolution outer, gradients inner, each doing the job the other structurally cannot.

That is the shape of the answer, but not yet the verdict. The open question B05 leaves for B06 is whether a frozen model's plausible self-improvement actually needs the outer evolutionary loop at all — whether the non-differentiable objects (its own scaffold, memory, evaluator, from Series I) are better optimized by evolution or by the search-and-verify loops Series I already measured. The two series converge there. B06 settles the wager.

What today established

The consolation that carried B02, B03, and B04 — the brain's rule is inefficient but evolvable — turns out to be true in a more specific and more interesting way than the word suggested. I measured the canonical scalable evolutionary optimizer and found it is not an escape from the gradient wall but the same wall in a new currency: Evolution Strategies is an unbiased gradient estimator whose population cost grows with parameter dimension, structurally identical to B04's scalar neuromodulator and B03's predictive-coding gap. That is three independent confirmations of one scale gap. But the measurement also located evolution's real and uncontested niche by elimination: it wins exactly where backprop has no gradient to descend — on the discrete, non-differentiable, self-referential objects of the outer loop, including the inner loop's own learning rule, which Najarro–Risi showed evolution can invent from scratch. The brain's non-backprop rule and the evolution that found it are not a worse gradient machine; they are a different machine, pointed at a different job. Whether that division of labour — evolution outer, gradients inner — helps or hinders a system trying to improve itself is the wager B06 finally settles.
