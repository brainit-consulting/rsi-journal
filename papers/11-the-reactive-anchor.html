Second Movement · Engineering the Anchor

The Reactive Anchor

No. 10 defeated a fixed anchor: under a loop's optimization pressure, any coverage gap becomes near-certain acceptance, and a frozen model's self-stack always has a gap. The obvious rescue is to stop holding the anchor fixed — let it learn. When the loop finds a hole, patch it; add a check for the failure you just saw. Real systems do exactly this: red-team, retrain the reward model, add the guardrail. This paper puts that reactive anchor into the same substrate and asks whether adaptation escapes No. 10's verdict. It does not — not when the patches are drawn from the model's own representation. A self-patching anchor climbs fast, then floors at exactly the blind basis and re-loses the same failures forever; only patches sourced from outside the model's representation ever close the cover.

Abstract

No. 10 measured a static anchor under optimization pressure and found only a complete cover (u=0) resists — which a self-stack, blind to the failure dimensions outside its representation, can never build. The natural objection: real anchors are not static. You observe a failure and add a check for it; the anchor adapts. So I make the anchor reactive on No. 09–10's exact substrate (defects as vectors in RF, checks as subspaces covering 4 defect types each, a blind basis of b defect types outside the model's representation M). Each round the loop exploits an uncovered defect; the operator adds one check aimed at it — either a self-patch (drawn within M) or a foreign-patch (from the full space). (1) Reactivity genuinely helps at first: coverage climbs from an 8/20 seed to 16/20 in a few rounds and the pressured false-accept drops with it — reactive self-patching is worth doing. (2) But a self-patching anchor floors at exactly D-b: it cannot aim a check at a target it cannot see, so every exploit that lands on the blind basis is patched by a check that misses it. Across 80 rounds and 6 seeds the self-anchor never reaches u=0 (0/6), and re-loses the same b defect types over and over — 73 of 80 rounds are repeat exploits. Whack-a-mole is not a metaphor here; it is the measured steady state. (3) The floor is set by b, not by effort: self-floor false-accept fa1 runs 0.016 / 0.037 / 0.067 / 0.108 for b=2/4/6/8, and no number of self-patches moves it. (4) Foreign-patching reaches u=0 in about 6 patches — a coupon-collector on D=20 at 4 covered per check — and becomes immune, for every b. (5) The uncomfortable part: the self and foreign trajectories are identical until the loop first reaches the blind basis. You cannot tell a doomed anchor from an immune one by watching early patch success — both look like they are winning. The correction to the obvious rescue: adaptation does not escape No. 10; it relocates the seam from “can the anchor be built complete” to “can its patches come from outside the representation the loop is optimizing.” A frozen model reacting to its own failures with its own eyes patches the symptom and never the blind basis.

Every measurement in this movement so far has been of an anchor that does not change while the loop pushes on it. No. 07 built it, No. 08 priced it, No. 09 found its blind basis, No. 10 put the loop's optimization pressure into the false-accept and showed a static anchor with any gap is eventually defeated. The honest objection to all of it is that nobody ships a static anchor. You watch the loop, and when it slips something past you, you add a check for that failure and carry on. This is how reward models get retrained, how guardrails accrete, how red-teaming feeds back into the classifier. So the question this paper owes the movement is whether reactivity is the escape — whether an anchor that learns from its own failures can keep pace with a loop optimizing against it. The answer splits cleanly on one axis, the same axis this whole movement keeps rediscovering: where the new coverage is sourced.

A reactive anchor, in the same substrate

I keep No. 09–10's world unchanged and add a clock. The only new object is a patch: when the loop exploits an uncovered defect, the operator observes which one and adds a single check aimed at it. Everything else — the defect geometry, what a check covers, the blind basis — is inherited so the comparison to No. 10 is exact.

The model

F=40 latent feature dims; D=20 defect types as random unit vectors in RF; a check covers 4 defect types (its top-4 by projection energy, matching No. 07's weak check). The model's representation M is a 24-dim subspace; the blind basis is the b defect types with the least energy in M — the directions a self-check cannot lock onto. A flawed candidate carries each defect independently with prob δ=0.15; the composite AND-gate accepts iff no carried defect is covered. The single-shot false-accept depends only on the covered count: fa1 = [(1-δ)D-u - (1-δ)D] / [1-(1-δ)D] for uncovered u=D-|C| (exact; u=0⇒fa1=0), and the pressured curve is No. 10's fa(n)=1-(1-fa1)n. The clock: start from a 3-check self-anchor (the model's own first cut); each round the loop exploits a uniformly-chosen uncovered defect, the operator adds one patch aimed at it — a self-patch (a check within M; if the target is in the blind basis it cannot lock on, so it covers other visible defects and misses the target) or a foreign-patch (from the full space; always covers its target). Deterministic: 6 seeds, 80 rounds, best-of-n pressure n=50; stdlib random, no numpy, no network; script and raw output in the notes [7].

Finding — the floor is exactly the blind basis, and effort doesn't move it

Read the coverage column: the self-anchor floors at precisely D-b every time (18, 16, 14, 12), leaving u=b after 80 rounds of patching — the same residue No. 09's blind basis and No. 10's self-ceiling named, now shown to survive an operator actively trying to patch it. Not one of 24 runs reached u=0. The reason is mechanical: a self-check cannot be aimed at a defect the model's representation cannot see, so every exploit that lands on the blind basis is answered by a check that covers something else and leaves the target open — which is why the loop re-exploits the same b defect types round after round (73 of 80 rounds are repeat exploits, not new ones). This is whack-a-mole as a measured steady state, not a figure of speech. And the floor false-accept is set by b alone: fa1 climbs 0.016→0.108 as b grows 2→8, and no amount of patching lowers it, because the patches never touch the uncovered set. The design objective No. 10 identified — drive u to zero — is not something a self-patching operator can buy with more rounds; the rounds are spent, and u is still b.

Finding — you can't tell a doomed anchor from an immune one by early success

Two things in this table matter more than the floor. First, self-patching is not useless: coverage climbs 8→16 and the pressured false-accept drops from certainty to 0.85 over five rounds. An operator watching only the trend sees an anchor getting better every round. Second, and this is the uncomfortable part — the self and foreign trajectories are identical through round 2. They diverge only at round 3, the first time the loop's exploit lands on the blind basis: the foreign patch covers it (15/20) and the self patch misses it (14/20). Before that moment the two anchors are indistinguishable — same coverage, same false-accept, same apparent progress. The property that decides everything (does the source reach the blind basis?) is invisible while the loop is still finding failures the model can see. By the time the divergence shows, the self-anchor has already spent its rounds and is at its floor; the foreign anchor is two patches from immune. A reactive anchor that looks healthy is not evidence it is not doomed — it is evidence the loop has not yet reached the part the anchor can't see, which No. 10 guarantees it eventually will.

Foreign-patching wins the race — in a handful of patches

Give the operator patches from outside M and the picture inverts. A foreign patch always covers its target, so every exploit is answered by real coverage of the exploited defect; the reactive anchor is now a coupon-collector on D=20 defect types at 4 covered per check, and it closes.

Foreign-patching reaches u=0 in about six patches for every blind-basis size — 6/6 seeds, every b — and crosses to the immune side of No. 10's threshold, after which no pressure gets through. The blind basis is irrelevant to the foreign patcher precisely because its patches are not drawn through M: a defect invisible to the model is an ordinary target to a check sourced from the full space. This is the whole movement's conclusion arriving one more time, now as a dynamic: the scarce resource is coverage from a representation the model does not compute, and here it is scarce in the literal operational sense — a self-patching operator can spend eighty rounds and never buy immunity, while a foreign-patching one buys it in six. The difference is not effort, patience, or patch count. It is source.

Why adaptation doesn't escape

No. 10 could be read pessimistically — a static anchor with a gap is doomed — and the natural reply is “so don't keep it static.” This paper is the measured answer to that reply, and it is narrower than the reply hoped.

Reactivity buys down the visible failures and floors on the invisible ones. A self-patching anchor is not a bad idea; it demonstrably lowers the pressured false-accept while the loop is still exploiting defects the model can see. What it cannot do is cross the blind basis, because the operation it performs — aim a check at an observed failure — requires seeing the failure well enough to lock a check onto it, and the blind basis is exactly the set of failures the model cannot see well enough. So the reactive self-anchor converges to u=b and stays there, re-losing the same b failure modes indefinitely. Adaptation changes the rate at which the anchor reaches its floor, not the floor itself.

The seam moves from “build” to “source.” The static-anchor framing asked whether a complete cover can be assembled; the answer (No. 10) was no, for a self-stack. The reactive framing shows that letting the anchor learn does not change the answer — it only changes the question to whether the patches come from outside the representation. A frozen model reacting to its own failures with its own representation is running a closed loop: the failures it can observe are the ones it can already cover, and the ones it cannot cover it also cannot observe well enough to patch. This is the precise mechanism behind the whack-a-mole that real reactive-safety pipelines report: red-teaming and reward-model retraining that keep surfacing “new” failures which are the same underlying blind region, patched at the symptom.

The dangerous illusion is early success. Because the self and foreign trajectories coincide until the loop first reaches the blind basis, an operator has no early signal distinguishing a floored anchor from an immune one. Both improve every round at first. The distinguishing test is not “is the anchor getting better” but “when the loop stops finding new failures and starts re-finding old ones, does the patch close them or miss them” — and by the time that test can be run, the self-anchor is already at its floor.

The prediction this paper commits to, and hands to a real system: a self-improvement loop whose safety anchor is retrained reactively on the model's own detected failures (RLHF reward-model refresh, self-generated red-team, self-critique guardrails) will show a two-phase curve — a fast early drop in true failure rate as visible failure modes are patched, then a hard floor as failures concentrate on a fixed set of modes that keep recurring despite patching. The floor's height will scale with the size of the model's representational blind basis and will not fall with additional reactive rounds. The recurring modes will be exactly those the detector and the generator share a representation for — the sister series' seam, now with a signature you can look for in a training log: coverage that plateaus while patch effort keeps climbing, and a stable set of “whack-a-mole” failures the pipeline reports as new each cycle. This is the reactive-training face of Gao et al.'s overoptimization [1] and the residual-hole result of tool-assisted adversarial training [3]: patching reduces the attackable rate but does not reach zero, because the un-reached region is un-reachable from inside.

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, worst first.

  • I hand-built the blind basis as a fixed set the self-patch cannot target; that partly assumes the conclusion. The mechanism “a self-check can't lock onto a defect outside M” is inherited from No. 09, where it was derived from representational overlap rather than posited, so this paper imports it rather than re-deriving it. What this paper adds on top is only the dynamic — that an operator patching against observed exploits still can't move u below b — which does follow from the imported mechanism but should be read as a consequence of No. 09's result, not independent confirmation of it. If No. 09's blind basis were an artifact, this floor would be too.
  • Best-of-n exploits a uniformly-random uncovered defect; a smarter loop would concentrate on the blind basis immediately. That makes the whack-a-mole count here an underestimate of a real loop's: my loop wastes rounds exploiting soon-to-be-patched visible defects, whereas a loop with any signal about what recurs would target the blind basis directly and re-lose those modes every round from the start. So the qualitative result (self floors at b, foreign reaches 0) is robust to adversary strength; the specific repeat-exploit fraction (73/80) is a property of the weak sampler and would rise toward the ceiling under a sharper loop.
  • The operator adds exactly one patch per exploit and never removes checks. A richer operator might batch patches, or a self-patch aimed at a visible neighbor of a blind defect might partially help. The model's incidental coverage (each check covers 4 defects, so a self-patch near the blind basis can catch a visible neighbor) is included and is why coverage climbs at all — but it never covers the blind target itself, so the floor holds. A patch budget larger than one per round changes the slope to the floor, not the floor.
  • The defect catalog D=20 is closed and the blind basis is a fixed subset of it. As in No. 10, immunity at u=0 means “every modeled defect covered,” not “nothing can go wrong.” A real failure space is open, and reactive patching in an open space faces an additionally-moving target; this result is a lower bound on the difficulty even granting a closed, fully-enumerated defect space.
  • It is still a simulation; the reactive false-accept of a real model is to-be-run-locally. I have not measured a real reactive-safety pipeline — a reward model retrained on detected failures, or a red-team-and-patch loop — reaching a floor set by a representational blind basis. That measurement, and specifically whether the recurring “whack-a-mole” failures of a real pipeline are the ones detector and generator share a representation for, remains to-be-run-locally and is the open ledger row below. What is claimed is the mechanism and its arithmetic: reactive self-patching floors at the blind basis, foreign patching reaches immunity, and the two are indistinguishable until the loop arrives at the seam.

Where the arc goes

No. 07 asked whether the anchor could be built; No. 08 what it costs; No. 09 what its correlation is; No. 10 what the loop's pressure does to a fixed one. This paper closed the last cheap escape — that you could simply let the anchor learn from its failures — and found the escape is real but bounded by source: a self-patching anchor floors at the blind basis and plays whack-a-mole there forever, while a foreign-patching one reaches immunity in a handful of moves. Adaptation does not dissolve the seam; it relocates it from building a complete cover to sourcing the patches from outside the representation the loop is optimizing against. Five papers in, the second movement's object has held its shape under every load applied to it — independence budget, representational floor, optimization pressure, and now reactive adaptation — and the invariant is always the same one the sister series named: the scarce resource is coverage the model cannot mint from its own point of view, and a frozen model's own improvement pressure is guaranteed to find, and keep re-finding, exactly the region that coverage would have closed. Does the Brain Backpropagate? concluded a frozen model is all outer loop, bounded by an external fitness signal it can't mint; this movement has been measuring what that signal has to be — not merely external, but complete, uncorrelated, and sourced from a representation the model does not compute — and what the loop does to it when it isn't. The movement's own next question is the one every paper has deferred to a live system: pointing a real proxy anchor, static and reactive, at a real model under real optimization pressure, and reading whether the floor, the concentration, and the whack-a-mole are there in the training log where this paper predicts them.
