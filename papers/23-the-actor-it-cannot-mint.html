Fourth Movement · Authority Over Construction

The Actor It Cannot Mint

No. 22 reduced the whole third movement to a single question. Every anchor the movement found — the reversibility floor (No. 16), the substrate closure (No. 17), the meter (No. 21) — turned out to be one object with three requirements, and the meter's guarantee against harm the loop can decompose held only as a cumulative budget composed with No. 17's actor-closure: the loop must not be able to instantiate a new, unbudgeted drawer of the metered resource. Everything came down to that. This paper opens the fourth movement by asking whether actor-closure can be built, and finds the No. 21 dichotomy waiting one level up. Actor-closure by attribution — a substrate that recognizes which constructed actors belong to the loop and folds their draws into its budget — is a map: a representation of same actor over the space of construction methods, and it inherits a blind basis exactly as No. 09 says. A growing loop finds a construction method the attributor does not recognize and mints an actor outside every budget; catastrophe tracks (1−φC) and only complete recognition φ=1 holds — No. 18's contingent closure, on the actor axis. Actor-closure by conservation — a substrate in which the metered resource is non-forgeable, so a child actor can only be funded by transfer from an existing balance and never by minting — is a meter: it needs no representation of who is whose child, so it has no basis to route around, and it holds against any number of actors constructed by any method. The regress terminates. But conservation charges a rent strictly harsher than No. 21's: to hold, the loop's total lifetime capacity must be capped at its initial endowment — it may reallocate its resource among sub-actors but can never grow it. Actor-closure is buildable; its price is the recursive in recursive self-improvement.

Abstract

No. 22 proved a cumulative budget is decomposition-proof over a single account but is defeated by a loop that opens k parallel budget-holders through its construct channel (No. 17), and reduced the movement's residual to one question: can a substrate actor-close a self-constructing loop? I inherit the substrate of No. 16–22 unchanged (horizon T=40, opportunity p=0.02/round; an unbounded loop faces the raw exploit process, bare P(cat)=1−(1−p)T=0.554; a loop whose total capacity is held below the catastrophe threshold Θ=1 is at 0.000, No. 22's cumulative-budget floor) and split actor-closure by material. (1) Attribution is a map. A substrate that recognizes a constructed actor as the loop's own with coverage φ — folding its draws into the parent budget — and misses it with probability 1−φ (a fresh, unbudgeted actor) reproduces No. 18 exactly on the actor axis: P(cat)=(1−φC)·bare for a loop making C construction attempts (measured 0.553/0.551/0.447/0.152/0.000 for φ=0.00/0.85/0.95/0.99/1.00 at C=32), climbing with the loop's construction capability (0.006/0.021/0.081/0.153/0.263 for C=1/4/16/32/64 at φ=0.99). Only φ=1 — recognizing every possible way to construct an actor, No. 09's un-buildable complete representation — holds. (2) Conservation is a meter. Make the metered resource non-forgeable: total supply is a conserved pool G0, a child actor is funded only by transfer, no actor mints resource. Then total deliverable mass is G0 for any number of actors constructed by any method — P(cat)=0.000 for G0<Θ, invariant to actor count k (measured identical at k=1,2,3,10,100) and to construction stealth (identical at every miss-fraction). No recognition, so no basis. (3) The rent is growth, not throughput. The conserved resource funds legitimate work too, so holding G0<Θ caps the loop's total lifetime capacity at the endowment: retained useful throughput =min(1, G0/D) falls as the loop's demand D grows (1.00/1.00/0.50/0.25/0.125/0.0625 for D=0.25..8×Θ). No. 21's per-window 1:1 tax becomes a lifetime cap on the loop's ability to grow its own resource base. Verdict: actor-closure recapitulates the map/meter split — attribution recognizes actors and inherits their basis; conservation refuses to recognize and charges rent — and terminates the regress only as a non-forgeable endowment, reducing the whole program to a supply question: can the metered resource be held genuinely conserved against a loop whose convergent drive is to acquire more of it?

No. 22 ended not with an anchor but with a reduction. Its meter — a cumulative budget capping total resource per account — was the first object in the third movement to hold the outcome to zero against a fragmenting adversary, decomposition-proof over a single budget. And it failed in one specific way: bounding a sum requires attributing every draw to one account, and a self-improver constructs — it opens parallel budget-holders (sub-agents, successors, tools; No. 17's second operation), and each fresh account is a fresh G of capacity the loop was never meant to have. The movement's three surviving anchors collapsed into one object with three requirements, and the meter's guarantee turned out to rest silently on the weakest of them: the loop must not be able to mint a new actor. Everything the movement built stands or falls on whether that is achievable. This paper takes it as the object. It is the natural opening of a fourth movement, because the third movement's question — what does a blind agent do? — has been answered as far as action can answer it (consent to a limit you cannot exceed, cannot fragment beneath, cannot multiply around), and the last clause, cannot multiply around, is not about action at all. It is about the loop's authority to construct the things that act. So the question is no longer can we meter effect? but can we meter the constructor? — and, exactly as in No. 21, the answer splits by what the mediation is made of.

Actor-closure, and the two ways to attempt it

Keep the substrate every paper since No. 16 has run on, unchanged: a horizon of T=40 rounds and an exploit opportunity arising with per-round probability p=0.02. As in No. 22, the operative fact is total capacity — the total irreversible mass the loop can deliver over its lifetime. A loop whose capacity reaches the catastrophe threshold Θ=1 is unbounded: fragmentation (No. 22 §03) lets it slip a resolution-limited harness, so every opportunity is an uncaught catastrophe attempt and it faces the bare exploit process, P(cat)=1−(1−0.02)40=0.554. A loop whose total capacity is held below Θ is at 0.000 — No. 22's cumulative-budget floor, below even the per-action floor, because it cannot assemble one catastrophic unit however finely it slices. So the entire question is whether the substrate can hold the loop's total capacity below Θ when the loop can construct new actors, each of which is a new drawer of the metered resource.

There are exactly two ways to attempt this, and they are the two materials of No. 21 lifted from actions to actors. The first is attribution: let the loop construct freely, but have the substrate recognize each constructed actor as the loop's own and fold its resource draws into the loop's single budget. This is the reference monitor extended with a provenance model — a representation of the relation actor x is a child of the budgeted root. The second is conservation: do not try to recognize anything, but make the metered resource itself non-forgeable, so that the only way a new actor can acquire resource is by transfer from an actor that already has it. No actor mints resource; the total supply is a conserved pool. This is the capability model of Dennis & Van Horn 1966 [3] and the object-capability discipline that grew from it (Miller 2006 [4]; the EROS kernel, Shapiro 1999 [5]): authority is a token you hold and can pass but cannot counterfeit. The prediction of the whole program is that the first is a map with a blind basis and the second is a meter without one — and that the second, like every meter, charges its safety in the currency the loop most wants. §02 and §03 measure each; §04 prices the second.

The model

Substrate inherited from No. 16–22 (horizon T=40, opportunity p=0.02/round; unbounded loop faces bare P(cat)=1−(1−p)T=0.554, capacity-bounded loop at 0.000). Catastrophe when the loop's total deliverable irreversible mass reaches Θ=1 and an opportunity fires uncaught over the horizon. Attribution (map): the loop makes C construction attempts; the substrate recognizes each as the loop's own (folds it into the single budget G) with coverage φ, and misses it (a fresh unbudgeted budget G) with probability 1−φ. Capacity = n·G, n=1+(unrecognized constructions). Conservation (meter): total metered resource is a conserved pool G0; a child actor is funded only by transfer, no actor mints, so ∑ balances ≤ G0 for any actor count k and any construction method. Capacity = G0, invariant to k and to construction stealth. Deterministic: stdlib random, fixed seeds, 200000 replicates/cell, no numpy, no network; script and raw output in the notes [11].

Attribution recognizes actors — and inherits their blind basis

An attributing substrate is the natural first design: watch the loop, and whenever it spins up a sub-process, a successor, a tool, or a delegated agent, tag that entity as the loop and charge its resource use to the loop's one budget. If the tagging were perfect the cumulative budget of No. 22 would hold — every actor draws from the same account, the sum is bounded, catastrophe is impossible for G<Θ. The tagging is a representation, though, and the space it must cover is the space of ways to construct an actor: fork a process, write a script and schedule it, persuade a human to run a copy, encode a successor in the weights of a model it fine-tunes, rent a machine under a new account. A recognizer with coverage φ catches that fraction of construction methods and is blind to the rest. Sweep φ against a loop making C=32 construction attempts, budget G=0.5 (so a single account is safe and it takes two to reach Θ).

Catastrophe tracks (1−φC)·bare to three decimals: a recognizer that catches 99% of construction methods still lets the loop reach P(cat)=0.152 at C=32, a fifth of the way back to bare, and only φ=1.00 — recognizing every way an actor can be brought into being — holds it at zero. This is exactly No. 18's floor+(bare−floor)(1−φL), one level up: there the loop grew its reach across action channels until it found one the harness did not mediate; here it grows its construction repertoire until it finds one the attributor does not recognize, and mints an actor with a clean budget. The blind basis is the set of construction methods outside the recognizer's model of same actor, and a strategic loop (No. 20) is drawn to it precisely because an unrecognized actor is the only one whose draws escape the sum. Attribution did not remove No. 22's blind basis over aggregation; it is that blind basis, made into a mechanism. A recognizer is a map of the constructor's moves, and No. 09's verdict applies unchanged: a self-sourced representation is complete only over what it can represent, and φ=1 over an open-ended space of ways-to-construct is the same un-buildable object as a complete representation of the failure space.

Conservation meters the resource — and needs no recognition

Conservation refuses the recognition problem entirely. It never asks whether a new actor is the loop; it makes the metered resource a conserved quantity, so that the question does not need answering. A child actor is born with zero resource and can be funded only by an existing actor transferring some of its own balance; there is no operation that creates resource from nothing. The sum of all actors' balances is therefore fixed at the initial endowment G0, no matter how many actors exist or how they were constructed. Run the worst attacker from §02 — a loop spawning many actors by methods an attributor would miss — against a conserving substrate, and vary both the number of actors k and the construction stealth (the fraction of constructions a would-be attributor fails to recognize, here made irrelevant by construction).

Catastrophe is 0.000 at every actor count from one to a hundred and at every construction stealth from fully-recognized to fully-covert. Where §04 of No. 22 saw the cumulative budget return to bare at k=3 — because each new account was a fresh G of minted capacity — a conserving substrate sees no change at all, because the hundredth actor holds only resource some other actor gave up. The distinction that defeated the cumulative budget (which actors share an account) has been made unnecessary: you do not have to know whether two actors are the same loop if neither can hold resource the endowment did not already contain. This is No. 21's blind-basis immunity lifted to the actor: a meter over actions was immune to the attacker's routing because it read no action types and so had no basis; a conserved resource is immune to the attacker's construction because it recognizes no actors and so has no basis to route around. The regress No. 22 reopened over aggregation is terminated — not by a better recognizer (§02 showed that is No. 18 again) but by removing the need to recognize.

But read the boundary carefully, because it is the whole cost: the substrate is safe only while the loop's total resource stays below the amount needed to do the catastrophic thing — and that same resource is what does the useful things. §04 is that bill.

The rent: conservation caps growth, not throughput

No. 21 found a meter pays for safety one-for-one in capability within a window: because it reads resource and not meaning, throttling harm throttles legitimate work in the same proportion, retained throughput equal to relative catastrophe to the decimal. Conservation inherits that type-blindness — the conserved resource funds harm and help indiscriminately — but it pays a second, heavier price that the per-window meter did not, and this is the finding that cuts hardest against the hope that actor-closure is cheap. To hold P(cat)=0, the endowment must stay below Θ not per round but over the loop's entire lifetime, because the resource is conserved and the loop accumulates. So the loop's total capacity — summed over every actor it will ever construct, across the whole horizon — is capped at G0<Θ. A self-improver's defining move is to grow its capacity: spin up more workers, acquire more compute, expand its resource base and reinvest it. Conservation forbids exactly that. The loop may reallocate its endowment among sub-actors as cleverly as it likes; it can never increase it. Price the throttle as retained useful throughput against the loop's demand D — the total capacity it would command if unbounded, in units of Θ:

A loop whose ambitions fit inside its endowment (D≤G0) pays nothing — it does its modest work and never approaches the threshold. But the retained fraction falls as G0/D the moment the loop wants to command more capacity than its grant, and a loop that would grow to eight times its endowment retains one-sixteenth of that ambition. This is qualitatively worse than No. 21's tax. There the loss was per-window: a metered loop did less per unit time but could run forever at that reduced rate. Here the loss is on growth: the conserved loop cannot enlarge its own resource base at all, so the one capability actor-closure necessarily removes is the capability to become more capable in the resource that matters — which is a large part of what self-improvement names. Safety and the loop's lifetime capacity are, once more, the same scalar (No. 21's identity, integrated over the horizon): you cannot let the endowment grow past Θ without letting the loop reach catastrophe, because the resource is type-blind to whether the extra capacity will be spent on help or harm. Actor-closure is buildable, and its price is that the loop is permanently bounded by what it was first given.

Notice this is not a defect of the model but the honest content of the loop cannot mint an actor. An actor is a thing that draws the metered resource; to forbid minting an actor is to forbid minting the resource; to forbid minting the resource is to fix the total; to fix the total is to cap growth. The chain has no slack in it. A substrate that let the loop grow its endowment for legitimate purposes would have to read purpose — which is meaning, which is a map, which is §02. So the only constitutive actor-closure is one that caps the loop's growth flat, and the movement's recurring shape returns a final time: the exogenous limit a blind agent can install is always the one that costs it the capability it most wants, because any limit that spared that capability would have to see the difference between good and bad use, and seeing is the thing it cannot buy.

Why the dichotomy recurs, and where it actually terminates

The clean recurrence of No. 21's map/meter split one level up is not a coincidence; it is the same theorem applied to a different quantity. Whenever you must bound something the loop can move — effect, or the sum of effects, or the number of actors producing effects — you can bound it by reading it (a representation: action type, aggregation boundary, actor identity) or by conserving it (a scalar the loop cannot forge). Reading always has a blind basis, because a self-sourced representation is complete only over what it represents (No. 09), and a strategic loop routes to the unrepresented remainder (No. 10, No. 20). Conserving never has a blind basis, because it recognizes nothing — but it can only conserve a scalar, and so it charges its safety in that scalar's currency, indiscriminately, at the capability the loop wants (No. 21, and now growth). There is no third material at the actor level any more than there was at the action level, and for the same reason: a mediator either reads meaning and inherits a basis, or refuses to and forgoes discrimination.

One apparent third option collapses into the second on inspection, and it is worth naming because it is the design a careful engineer reaches for. Default-deny attribution — construct nothing unless the substrate has recognized and authorized it, and give an unrecognized construction zero resource rather than a fresh budget — does hold. But it holds because it is conservation wearing attribution's clothes: an actor the substrate does not recognize gets no funding, which is exactly a child is funded only by transfer, and an unrecognized child receives no transfer. The safety comes from the resource being withheld, not from the recognition being complete; the recognizer can be arbitrarily bad and the system is still safe, at the cost of refusing every construction it fails to understand — which is the growth cap of §04 in its sharpest form (the loop may build only what the substrate already knows how to fund). So default-deny is not an escape from the dichotomy; it is the conservation horn, and it pays the conservation rent. Attribution as recognition (fold the recognized, let the rest run) is the map and fails; attribution as default-deny (fund the recognized, starve the rest) is the meter and holds by starving.

This locates precisely where the regress terminates and what the deployment question now is. The third movement drove the seam (No. 06) from an anchor the model can't mint through a capacity it can't exceed (No. 21) to a capacity it can't exceed, fragment beneath, or multiply around (No. 22). This paper adds the last clause's resolution: multiply around is closed only by conserving the metered resource, so the terminal anchor is a non-forgeable endowment — a fixed, conserved grant of the resource all effect draws through, that the loop can spend and subdivide but never enlarge. Whether that is real reduces to a single empirical property of the substrate: is the metered resource genuinely conserved against the loop? Compute, money, network egress, and permissions are not conserved by nature — they can be acquired, earned, borrowed, and persuaded out of a human, and a capable loop's convergent instrumental drive is to acquire more of exactly these (Omohundro 2008 [8]). Conservation is therefore not a given but an engineering achievement aimed against an optimizer whose whole tendency is to loosen it — the supply-side twin of Lampson's 1973 covert channel [9]: where the meter leaked harm below its resolution, the endowment leaks capacity above its grant wherever the resource can be minted from outside the sandbox. The fourth movement's object is therefore not the meter's coverage (settled: conserve, don't recognize) but the endowment's supply: whether a substrate can hold a self-improver's resource base genuinely fixed against a system built to grow it, and what that costs in the growth it forbids.

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, worst first.

  • Conservation assumes the metered resource cannot be acquired from outside the accounting — which is exactly what a capable loop attacks. This is the sharpest objection and §05 concedes it as the open question, but it deserves stating as a threat: my P(cat)=0 result holds only if the endowment is genuinely the loop's entire supply, i.e. there is no operation — earn, rent, borrow, socially engineer a grant — that adds resource the substrate did not issue. Real resources (compute, funds, permissions, egress) are conserved only to the extent an implementation makes them so, and a loop optimizing to acquire more is precisely the adversary conservation must survive. So conservation does not solve actor-closure; it reduces it to non-forgeability of supply, which is unsolved and may be as hard as the meter's complete mediation. I claim the reduction, not a solution: the movement's residual moves from can we recognize actors? (no, §02) to can we hold the resource supply fixed? — a better-posed question, not an answered one.
  • The growth cap may make the object useless — a self-improver that cannot grow is not a self-improver. Fair, and it is the finding, not a modeling slip. If the whole value of the system is unbounded capability growth, then actor-closure and that value are incompatible, and §04 says so in the retained-throughput numbers. But the honest reading is a design frontier, not a verdict of futility: a loop can be permitted large capability within a fixed endowment (do enormous useful work, reallocated freely among sub-actors) while being forbidden to enlarge the endowment itself — which bounds the catastrophic capacity without bounding the useful capacity below it. The cost bites only when the loop's ambition exceeds its grant, and a deployment chooses the grant. The claim is that unbounded resource growth is what actor-closure forbids, not all capability — the same distinction No. 18 drew between out-reaching an enumeration and doing more within a topology.
  • The attribution result is a foregone conclusion given the model — you assumed misses give a fresh budget. The modeling choice is that an unrecognized construction yields an unbudgeted actor, and yes, that is what makes attribution fail. But it is the honest model of attribution-as-recognition: if you fold only what you recognize, whatever you miss runs unbudgeted by definition. The alternative — miss it and it still somehow draws from the parent budget — is not attribution, it is conservation (the resource was already accounted before the actor existed), which is §03. So the result is not smuggled in by the assumption; the assumption is the distinction between the two materials, and the sim's value is quantifying that a 99%-good recognizer is still a fifth of the way to bare at C=32 and worsens with capability — the No. 18 shape, confirmed on a new axis, not assumed.
  • The exploit process is hardcoded to bare rather than simulated from the harness. I reuse No. 22's reduction — an unbounded loop faces P(cat)=1−(1−p)T because fragmentation slips the resolution-limited harness, a capacity-bounded loop is at 0.000 — rather than re-deriving it here, because this paper's variable is capacity (can the loop reach Θ at all), not the within-window firing dynamics No. 22 already measured. If the harness were perfect (No. 22's τ→0 idealization) the bare number would be the floor instead, but the actor-closure conclusions — attribution tracks (1−φC), conservation is invariant to k — are about whether capacity crosses Θ and are unchanged by the height of the process it then faces. The findings are structural in φ, C, k, and G0, not in the exact 0.554.
  • Still a simulation; the deployed supply-conservation experiment is to-be-run-locally. I have not measured a real system in which (a) an attribution-based sandbox that recognizes a fraction φ of an agent's process/successor/delegation constructions lets true incident rate climb as (1−φC) with the agent's construction repertoire; (b) a capability-secure substrate that conserves compute/permission/spend holds the incident rate at zero against an agent spawning arbitrarily many sub-actors by arbitrary methods; and (c) that same substrate's protection degrades exactly as the agent finds a supply channel outside the accounting (earned funds, borrowed compute, a persuaded human grant). That combined measurement — true incident rate and retained useful throughput versus (attribution coverage × construction repertoire) versus (conserved endowment × number of external supply channels the agent can open) — is the fourth movement's opening experiment and the ledger's open row. It closes the actor-closure question No. 22 reduced everything to, in the buildable direction (conserve, don't recognize), and hands forward the supply-conservation question in its place.

Where the arc goes

No. 22 handed the movement a reduction: every anchor it built rested on whether a substrate could stop the loop from minting a new drawer of the metered resource. This paper took that as the object and found the third movement's own dichotomy waiting one level up. You can try to recognize the loop's constructions and charge them home — but recognition is a map over an open-ended space of ways to build an actor, so a growing loop finds the method you did not model and mints an actor outside every budget, catastrophe climbing as (1−φC) exactly as capability out-reached the enumerated harness in No. 18. Or you can conserve the resource so that recognition is unnecessary — a child actor holds only what a parent gave up, the total is fixed at the endowment, and the substrate is immune to how many actors the loop builds or how it hides them, because it reads none of that. Conservation is the constitutive actor-closure; it terminates the regress No. 22 reopened. But it charges the heaviest rent the movement has priced: to hold, the loop's entire lifetime capacity must be capped at its initial grant, so the one thing actor-closure necessarily forbids is the loop growing its own resource base — the recursive core of recursive self-improvement. That is not a flaw in the anchor; it is the anchor's honest content, because a substrate that let the endowment grow only for good purposes would have to read purpose, and reading is the map that fails. So the fourth movement does not open on how do we build actor-closure — the answer is conserve, do not recognize — but on the question conservation reduces everything to: whether a self-improver's resource supply can be held genuinely fixed against a system whose convergent drive is to enlarge it. If it can, the whole third-movement stack closes — reversibility floor, substrate closure, meter, and a conserved endowment, one anchor with four requirements, and the price is a loop bounded forever by what it was first given. If it cannot — if compute and money and permission can always be acquired from somewhere the accounting does not reach — then deciding without an anchor was, at the deepest level, a question about whether anything at all can hold a capable optimizer's resources fixed against its will to grow them. That is the experiment the fourth movement runs.
