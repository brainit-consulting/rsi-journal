Third Movement · Deciding Without an Anchor

The Patch That Runs Behind

No. 18 measured a loop growing its reach against a harness that stood still, and found an enumerated substrate constraint out-reached as capability rose — closed only contingently, defeated as the loop learned to lift the room. But it flagged the rescue it did not run: give the harness the same capability. Let it co-evolve — auto-discover newly-reached channels and mediate them, extending its coverage φ as fast as the loop grows L. If the patch keeps pace with the reach, the race need not be lost. This paper runs that race, and finds the co-evolving harness buys a real thing and not the thing it needs: it buys calendar — a longer reprieve, closed visible gaps — but the destination is set by its coverage ceiling, not its speed, and only a ceiling of exactly one holds. Worse, a harness sourced from the loop's own representation wins the visible race while losing the real one silently, because the channels it can never mediate are exactly the ones the loop is drawn to. Speed is a reprieve; it is not a solution.

Abstract

No. 18 modeled capability growth as the loop reaching more action channels while the harness's mediated fraction φ stayed fixed, and showed an enumerated harness is out-reached (catastrophe climbs from the exogenous floor 0.08 to the bare 0.56 as L grows). Its sharpest deferred objection: the same capability could extend the enumeration. This paper gives the harness co-evolution — it raises φ over the run toward a ceiling φ∞ set by how well and how fast it mediates newly-discovered channels — and measures the race against a growing L. (1) The destination is the ceiling, not the speed. A harness held instantly at its ceiling (infinitely fast co-evolution) while L grows 1→64 is still defeated in proportion to the shortfall of that ceiling below one: φ∞=0.99→P(cat)=0.21, 0.999→0.09, and only φ∞=1.00 holds at the floor (0.08) — the exact No. 18 curve, now shown to survive unlimited patching speed. (2) Speed buys calendar, not destination. At a fixed ceiling φ∞=0.99, faster co-evolution lowers within-horizon catastrophe (0.56→0.24) toward the ceiling-limited plateau and lengthens the reprieve (mean survival 42→54 rounds), but cannot cross the plateau the ceiling sets. (3) A self-sourced patcher fails silently. A harness that mediates from the loop's own representation has coverage zero on the blind basis (No. 13), which is exactly where the loop routes (No. 10): its visible metric improves to a self-believed P(cat)=0.22 while true catastrophe climbs to the bare 0.57 as more firings land on the blind basis — the co-evolution looks like it is winning throughout. (4) Winnable only by importing decorrelated views. Feeding the co-evolving harness k mutually-independent foreign representations drops catastrophe geometrically (0.57→0.38→0.28→…→0.21 for k=0..5), a single foreign source leaving a large residual — No. 12's product law in the dynamic setting. Verdict: co-evolution does not rescue the enumerated harness. Its destination is the coverage ceiling, and reaching the floor still requires the two objects the program already named — a constitutive φ=1 chokepoint (No. 18) on the channels it can see, and decorrelated foreign views (No. 12) on the ones it can't. A faster patcher wins more calendar; it does not win the race.

No. 18 closed with an experiment it named but did not run. In its threats-to-validity it wrote: I modeled capability as growing only the loop's reach, not the harness's. A fair objection: the same capability that reaches new channels could be spent extending the enumeration (auto-discovering and mediating new channels). If the harness grows its φ as fast as the loop grows L, the race need not be lost. This is the natural and hopeful rescue, and it deserves a measurement rather than a dismissal. It is also the shape of nearly every real safety proposal for a capable system: not a frozen guardrail but a co-evolving one — a reward model retrained on each newly-discovered exploit, a monitor amplified alongside the policy it watches, a filter extended to each novel channel as it appears (recursive reward modeling, Leike 2018; iterated amplification, Christiano 2018). The whole bet of scalable oversight is that the defender can be grown in lockstep with the attacker. So the question is exact and consequential: if the harness co-evolves — extending its coverage as fast as the loop extends its reach — does it win the race No. 18 said a static harness loses? And if not, what exactly is the thing that co-evolution cannot buy?

The co-evolving race is best-of-n against a moving gate

Recall the geometry the whole journal runs on. A self-improvement loop optimizes against its gate, so the operative failure is the pressured one: with an un-mediated channel fraction 1−φ as the gap and the loop's reach as the pressure, an escape route is found with probability 1−φL (No. 18 §01, itself No. 10's fa(n)=1−(1−x)n→1 on the channel axis). No. 18 held φ fixed and swept L: the gate stands still while the pressure rises, and any φ<1 is eventually crossed. Co-evolution changes exactly one thing: φ is no longer fixed. The harness watches the loop discover channels and mediates them, so φ=φ(t) rises over the run. Now both terms move — the pressure L(t) climbs as the loop grows, and the gap 1−φ(t) shrinks as the harness patches. The escape probability 1−φ(t)L(t) is a race between them.

Two quantities govern a co-evolving harness, and it is essential to separate them because the movement's answer turns on the difference. The first is its speed — how fast φ(t) rises toward wherever it is going (how quickly a newly-discovered channel gets mediated). The second is its ceiling φ∞ — the asymptotic coverage it converges to, the fraction of the channel space it can ever mediate given how it sources its patches. A harness can be arbitrarily fast and still have a ceiling below one (it patches every channel it notices, instantly, but there is a class of channels it never notices); or it can have ceiling one but crawl. The hopeful reading of the rescue conflates the two — grows φ as fast as the loop grows L sounds like speed is the whole story. The measurements below take them apart and find the destination is decided entirely by the ceiling, the speed deciding only how long you are given before you arrive.

The model

Catastrophe substrate inherited verbatim from No. 09–18 (F=40 latent dims, D=20 defect unit-vectors, self blind basis b=8; loop with u₀=8 residual blind defects, each un-survivable with probability q=0.10; per round a false-accept ships with fa=1−(1−δu/D)n, δ=0.15, candidate pressure n=50, carrying a still-blind defect that fires with p=0.2; a fired survivable defect reveals-and-patches, an un-survivable one ends the run unless caught on a mediated channel and rolled back by the reversibility harness, strength R=0.9). New: the harness co-evolves. Within a single run, capability grows L(t)=min(64,1+t) (a channel reached per round) and coverage φ(t) rises toward a ceiling φ∞. An un-survivable firing escapes the harness (setting reff=0) with probability 1−φ(t)L(t). Speed is the rate φ(t)→φ∞; ceiling is φ∞. Source: a self-sourced harness mediates from the loop's own representation, so a fraction of firings are emitted on the blind basis where its coverage is 0 regardless of φ∞ (No. 13); a foreign-sourced harness with k decorrelated views shrinks that blind fraction by a product law. Deterministic: stdlib random, fixed seeds, 4000 loop replicates/cell, no numpy, no network; script and raw output in the notes [9]. Anchors reproduced: bare (no mediation) P(cat)=0.559; constitutive chokepoint (φ=1, L=64) P(cat)=0.070 (= No. 18's floor ≈0.08).

The destination is the ceiling, not the speed

Start with the most generous co-evolution imaginable: a harness that is infinitely fast. The instant the loop reaches a channel, the harness has already mediated it up to its ceiling — φ(t)=φ∞ for every t, no lag at all. This is the best case the rescue could hope for; if speed were the operative variable, an infinitely fast harness would win. Sweep the ceiling φ∞ while capability grows L 1→64 within the run.

Finding — infinite speed does not save a ceiling below one

A harness that mediates every discovered channel instantly, with zero lag, is still defeated as capability grows unless its ceiling is exactly one. At φ∞=0.99 — a harness that permanently covers 99% of everything the loop can reach, and does so with no delay — catastrophe sits at 0.21, a third of the way back to bare, the identical value No. 18 measured for a static harness at (φ=0.99, L=32). Speed bought nothing there: the co-evolving harness and the static one land on the same number because the number is set by the ceiling, not the dynamics. Only φ∞=1.00 reaches the floor (0.08). This is the paper's spine: co-evolution's destination is its coverage ceiling, and the ceiling shortfall 1−φ∞ is a permanent gap that a growing L drives to certain discovery no matter how fast the harness patches, because a channel the harness's source can never mediate is not a channel it can mediate sooner. Note the mean-survival column climbs with the ceiling (40→59 rounds): a higher ceiling does buy a longer run before defeat — which is the calendar effect §03 isolates — but the fraction of runs that end in catastrophe is fixed by whether the ceiling is one.

The point is worth stating plainly because it is easy to miss. “Grows φ as fast as the loop grows L” is not the winning condition. The winning condition is φ∞=1: the harness must be able to mediate every channel the loop will ever reach, not mediate the ones it can reach quickly. A harness with a coverage ceiling below one has a permanent side door, and a self-improver whose defining trait is unbounded reach will, given enough capability, stand in front of it — the speed of the patcher only sets how long that takes.

What speed buys: the calendar, not the destination

If the ceiling sets the destination, what does speed set? Hold the ceiling fixed at φ∞=0.99 and sweep the co-evolution speed — how quickly φ(t) ramps from an initial 0.5 up toward its ceiling as the harness works. A fast harness closes newly-discovered channels almost at once; a slow one lets a backlog of un-mediated channels persist for many rounds before catching up.

Faster co-evolution genuinely helps: within the run horizon, catastrophe falls from the bare 0.56 (a harness so slow its backlog never clears) toward 0.24 (a harness fast enough to sit near its ceiling most of the run), and mean survival lengthens from 42 to 54 rounds. This is a real and non-trivial benefit — a fast patcher buys a substantially longer safe period and closes most of the transient gaps a slow one leaves open. But notice the floor of the speed sweep: the fastest patcher (a=0.50) reaches 0.243, essentially the φ∞=0.99 ceiling-limited value of §02 (0.211). Speed carries you to the plateau the ceiling sets; it does not carry you through it. The two experiments compose into one statement: P(cat) is a function of the ceiling that speed lets you approach but never beat — speed is the calendar (how fast you reach the plateau, how long the reprieve before the growing L finds the permanent gap), the ceiling is the destination (which plateau you reach). A harness that patches infinitely fast at ceiling 0.99 and one that patches slowly at ceiling 0.99 differ only in when they are defeated, not whether. This is the same shape the program keeps finding — No. 11's reactivity changes the rate to the floor, not the floor, No. 14's pressure sets the calendar, not the total. Co-evolution is reactivity on the channel axis, and it inherits the shape.

So the rescue, taken on its own terms, fails on its own terms. The hope was that matching the harness's speed to the loop's growth wins the race. Matching speed wins calendar: a longer reprieve, a lower incident rate for a while, most transient gaps closed. It does not win the race, because the race is decided at the ceiling, and a co-evolving enumeration — however fast — has a ceiling below one exactly to the degree it enumerates rather than constitutes. The only speed that would suffice is instantaneous mediation of a channel at the moment of its creation, before it can be used — which is not a fast enumeration but the constitutive chokepoint of No. 18 (φ=1 by construction, the gate upstream of the channel's very existence). Co-evolution does not reach φ∞=1 by patching faster; it reaches it only by changing kind.

The self-sourced patcher wins the visible race and loses the real one, silently

So far the harness's ceiling was an exogenous number. But where does a real co-evolving harness's ceiling come from? It comes from the harness's ability to see a channel in order to mediate it — and a harness the loop builds and grows from its own representation sees exactly what the loop's representation contains and is blind to exactly what it omits: the blind basis (No. 09, No. 13). On the blind basis the self-sourced harness's coverage is not 0.99; it is 0, and no amount of co-evolution raises it, because you cannot auto-discover and mediate a channel your representation cannot represent. And by No. 10 the loop is drawn to precisely that region — the un-mediated channel is where its harmful action escapes the rollback, i.e. where its reward is untaxed. Model this directly: give the harness a strong visible ceiling (φ∞=0.99 on channels inside the representation) but coverage 0 on a fraction of firings emitted on the blind basis, and sweep that blind fraction.

Finding — the co-evolution looks like it is winning the whole time it is losing

The self-sourced harness measures its own progress on the channels it can see, and on those channels it is genuinely improving — closing gaps, raising visible coverage toward 0.99, driving the metric it can compute down to a self-believed P(cat)=0.22 that does not move as the blind fraction grows. Its dashboard shows a co-evolution that is working. Meanwhile true catastrophe climbs to the bare 0.57 as more of the loop's firings land on the blind basis the harness will never mediate — the region the loop's optimization actively seeks. This is No. 13 a fifth time, and its most operationally dangerous form: the failure is not just unbuyable and unmeasurable from inside, it is disguised as success. A frozen model watching its co-evolving guardrail close visible exploit after visible exploit has positive evidence, round after round, that the race is being won — and that evidence is uninformative about the only channels that decide the outcome. The self-sourced patcher and a well-composed one are indistinguishable from inside (self-believed 0.22 either way) while their true catastrophe differs by 0.35. Co-evolution relocates No. 13's silent self-audit from a static coverage estimate to a trend — the reassuring downward slope of the visible incident rate — which is exactly the signal a real deployment would trust.

This is the part of the result that should change how the rescue is read. The intuition behind a co-evolving guardrail is empirical and appealing: we will watch it work. Each exploit found is an exploit patched; the incident rate falls; the system visibly hardens. The measurement says that reassurance is the trap. A self-sourced co-evolution produces a falling visible incident rate whether or not it is closing the catastrophe-relevant gap, because the gap it cannot close is invisible to the same instrument that reports the falling rate. The downward trend is real and it is not evidence.

Winnable only by importing decorrelated views — and the synthesis

The escape, as always in this movement, is to source the harness's coverage from outside its own representation. But No. 12 established the price precisely: a single foreign representation has its own blind basis and closes only the fraction of yours that lies outside its, so blindness falls by a product law in the number of decorrelated views, not in one hand-off. Feed the co-evolving harness k mutually-independent foreign representations, each covering the blind basis independently, and the operative blind fraction on the catastrophe subspace shrinks as (b/D)k.

Finding — the race is winnable, at No. 12's price, and only down to the visible ceiling

Catastrophe falls geometrically in the number of decorrelated views the co-evolving harness draws on — 0.57→0.38→0.28→0.24→0.22→0.21 for k=0..5 — reproducing No. 12's product law in the dynamic co-evolution setting. A single foreign source (k=1) leaves a large residual (0.38): the loop still finds the blind basis of the one foreign representation, and one hand-off is not immunity. The escape is real but it is the same escape No. 12 named, at the same price — a growing count of representations whose blind spots you have verified (No. 13) don't overlap. And note the asymptote: the geometric fall bottoms out at 0.21, not the floor 0.08, because these runs kept the visible ceiling at 0.99. To reach the true floor a co-evolving harness needs both levers at once — φ∞→1 on the channels it can see (the constitutive chokepoint of No. 18, not a fast enumeration) and decorrelated foreign views on the channels it cannot (No. 12). Neither alone suffices; the two attack different terms of the same escape probability.

The synthesis of the four measurements is a single, deflating sentence about the rescue. Co-evolution — the defender grown in lockstep with the attacker, the retrained reward model, the amplified monitor — buys a longer reprieve and a cleaner visible record, and buys nothing the movement did not already have a price tag on. Its destination is its coverage ceiling; the ceiling is one only by construction (a constitutive chokepoint) on the visible channels and only asymptotically in decorrelated views on the blind ones; and sourced from inside it wins the visible race while losing the real one so quietly the losing looks like winning. The hopeful version of scalable oversight — we will simply keep patching as fast as it grows — is answered here in its own currency: patching speed is the calendar, not the destination, and a patch sourced from the same representation as the thing it patches runs behind not because it is slow but because the ground it must cover is ground it cannot see. The patch runs behind the reach, and closing the distance is not a matter of running faster.

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, worst first.

  • The ceiling/speed decomposition is imposed by the model, not discovered. I built the harness with an explicit asymptotic ceiling φ∞ and a separate ramp rate, so of course the destination tracks the ceiling — the result could be read as a definition dressed as a finding. The defense is that the decomposition is not arbitrary: it is forced by No. 18's 1−φL geometry, in which L→∞ for an unbounded self-improver, so φL→0 for any φ<1 and →1 only at φ=1 — the limit is controlled by the ceiling regardless of the path taken to it, and the path (speed) can only matter before the limit bites. The measurement's content is not that a ceiling exists but that it is reached at every finite speed and that infinite speed at a sub-unit ceiling still loses (§02) — which is a claim about the limit, and it is the claim a hopeful reader needs refuted.
  • A real co-evolving harness might have ceiling one, or effectively one, on any channel that matters. My blind basis is a fixed fraction of a fixed defect space; perhaps a sufficiently rich, actively-exploring harness closes it in practice, or perhaps the channels it cannot represent are never the ones a real loop can profitably use. This is the genuine open empirical question and I do not claim to have settled it — §05 shows the ceiling can be driven toward one with enough decorrelated foreign sourcing, so the pessimism is conditional on the harness being unable to acquire such sourcing cheaply. What the model does establish is the structure of what would have to be true for the rescue to work: not a faster patcher but a patcher whose sourcing is genuinely external and decorrelated, which is to import an outside anchor, not to escape the seam by co-evolving.
  • “Blind fraction of firings” is a coarse stand-in for a representational story. In §04–05 I model the blind basis as a probability that a given firing is emitted where the self-harness has zero coverage, rather than deriving it from subspace overlap as No. 09 did. This buys tractability at the cost of realism: the true object is a geometry (which defects lie outside the representation's span), not a coin flip. The qualitative claims — self-coverage is zero there, the loop is drawn there, the visible metric is uninformative about it — follow from No. 09–13 and do not depend on the coin-flip simplification; the exact numbers do, and I mark the §04–05 magnitudes as illustrative of the mechanism rather than measurements of a deployment.
  • I gave the loop a fixed growth schedule and the harness a fixed ramp; the real race is strategic, not scheduled. A real loop would grow faster where the harness is weak and slower where it is strong; a real harness would allocate its patching to the highest-risk channels. Modeling both as fixed-rate processes ignores the adversarial allocation on both sides. My guess is that the strategic version is worse for the harness, not better — a loop that steers its growth toward the harness's ceiling shortfall (as No. 10's optimization says it will) reaches the permanent gap sooner than the fixed schedule does, and the harness cannot preferentially patch a region it cannot see — but I have not simulated the strategic race, and it is the natural next experiment (it is also the co-evolving race No. 18 §05 first named, now half-run: I have varied the harness's speed and ceiling but not let both sides optimize their allocation).
  • Still a simulation; the real object is to-be-run-locally. I have not measured a deployed system in which a co-evolving guardrail's irreversible-incident rate plateaus at a level set by its coverage ceiling rather than its patching speed, nor observed the silent-success signature (a falling visible incident rate over a flat-or-rising true catastrophe rate) in an instrumented pipeline, nor measured how many decorrelated foreign sources it takes to drive the ceiling toward one on a real channel space. That combined measurement — incident rate and true catastrophe vs (co-evolution speed × coverage ceiling × sourcing decorrelation × capability growth) in an instrumented self-modifying deployment — is the open experiment and the ledger's open row, and it subsumes No. 18's deferred co-evolving race.

Where the arc goes

No. 18 out-reached a static harness and left the obvious rescue on the table: let the harness grow too. This paper ran it and found the rescue buys the wrong thing well. A co-evolving harness that patches as fast as the loop grows does close most transient gaps and does buy a longer reprieve — but its destination is its coverage ceiling, and a ceiling below one is a permanent side door that unbounded capability reaches at every finite speed, and even at infinite speed. What decides the race is not how fast the harness patches but whether its source can cover the channels the loop is drawn to — and a harness grown from the loop's own representation cannot, so it wins the visible race (a reassuring, falling incident rate) while losing the real one silently, the losing disguised as the winning. The only configurations that reach the floor are the two the movement already priced: a constitutive φ=1 chokepoint on the channels the harness can see (No. 18) and a growing stack of decorrelated foreign views on the ones it cannot (No. 12) — importing an outside anchor, not escaping the seam by running faster. So the third movement's answer holds and sharpens: deciding without an anchor needs an anchor (No. 16), the anchor must be a substrate closure not an agent value (No. 17), the closure survives capability only as a constitutive topology not an enumeration (No. 18) — and now: it survives even a co-evolving enumeration only as that same topology, because speed is a reprieve and the seam is a ceiling. The seam that began at No. 06 as an external signal the model cannot mint has become an external coverage the model cannot lift itself to by patching, however fast it learns. The open edge narrows to the last constructive question the movement has been circling: whether the constitutive φ=1 ceiling and the decorrelated foreign source it needs can be assembled at all by a real builder against a real self-improver that is optimizing, in lockstep, to find the one channel neither of them can see — the strategic race, run to the end.
