Third Movement · Deciding Without an Anchor

The Reversibility Lever

The second movement proved a hard theorem and then asked an operational question. The theorem: for the failures that end the game, the coverage a frozen model needs must be bought before deployment (No. 15), in a currency it cannot mint (No. 09–12), measure (No. 13), or wait out (No. 14). The question, which No. 15 left open on the last line: given a blindness you can neither see, quantify, nor learn from without dying, what do you actually do? This paper opens the third movement with the first honest answer, and it is not “cover more.” Every result so far attacked one factor of the catastrophe law P=1−(1−q)u₀ — the residual blindness u₀, the thing a frozen model provably struggles to shrink. There is a second factor, q, the fraction of defects that are un-survivable, and it has been sitting in the exponent untouched. This paper measures what happens when you stop trying to see better and start trying to make being wrong cheaper: not coverage but reversibility — keep each action's worst case recoverable, so a defect that fires teaches instead of terminating. It is a different lever, on a different factor, with a different economics — and it comes with its own version of the same seam.

Abstract

No. 15 reduced catastrophe to P=1−(1−q)u₀ and showed the only lever it found — shrinking the residual blind basis u₀ — is exactly the pre-deployment independence a frozen model cannot cheaply supply. This paper introduces a lever on the other factor. Give each action a reversibility r: the probability that, when a still-blind un-survivable defect fires, a harness catches or rolls it back so the incident teaches (No. 14's reveal-and-patch) instead of ending the run. (1) Channel-level reversibility is a real, independent lever: catastrophe follows P=1−(1−q(1−r))u₀ exactly (measured 0.566/0.318/0.074/0.000 at r=0/0.5/0.9/1.0, q=0.10, u₀=8, vs predicted 0.570/0.337/0.077/0.000) — driving risk to zero with the blind basis u₀ left completely uncovered. Unlike foreign cover, it needs no decorrelated views: it does not require identifying which defect is dangerous, only bounding the worst case of every action. (2) But a self-assessed reversibility — the model gating actions it judges high-impact — floors at the blind basis and fails silently: because the model cannot see the blind defect, it rates the action carrying it as low-impact and ships it ungated, so true P(cat) holds at 0.57 while the model's self-believed P(cat) falls to 0.00. Impact you assess from inside inherits No. 13's zero-recovery blindness. (3) And a harness the loop can modify is defeated by its own optimization: best-of-n pressure routes candidates around any un-gated channel (reff=r·cn), so measured catastrophe climbs back from 0.08 to the unprotected 0.56 as n grows 1→50 — No. 10's “the loop is the adversary” lifted one level, onto the safety harness itself. Only an exogenous, mandatory harness (flat 0.08 across all n) holds. (4) Reversibility (attacks q) and foreign cover (attacks u₀) are independent axes that multiply on the same law. Verdict: deciding well under an unclosable seam is not one move but a product — bound the stakes with an exogenous impact limit that needs no sight, and shrink the blindness with the decorrelated views you can afford — and the stakes-bounding half is the first lever in the whole program that does not require the model to see what it is missing, which is exactly why it must be enforced from outside the loop rather than chosen by it.

No. 15 ended by refusing the movement's own reflex. Eight papers had answered every threat with the same word — cover it, from outside — and then proved that for the failures that matter, the outside coverage must be complete, unbuyable on credit, and unverifiable once bought. The closing line asked a question the coverage frame cannot answer: what does a frozen model do — not to close the seam, which is proven impossible, but to act well under a blindness it cannot see, quantify, or wait out? The reflex answer is “cover more,” and the reflex is wrong, because the whole point of No. 15 is that the dangerous residual is precisely the part you can't cover. So this paper looks at the catastrophe law it inherited and notices something the second movement never touched. P(catastrophe)=1−(1−q)u₀ has two knobs. Every paper from No. 07 to No. 15 turned u₀ — the count of residual blind defects — and found it welded shut from inside. The other knob, q — the fraction of defects whose first firing is terminal rather than instructive — was treated as a fixed property of the world. It is not. Whether a defect's first firing ends the loop or merely teaches it is partly a property of how you act: an action taken so that its worst outcome is recoverable converts a terminal defect into a survivable one. That conversion is the subject of this paper, and it is a genuinely different kind of move than anything in the second movement, because it does not require seeing the defect.

A second knob: don't see better, be wrong cheaper

The idea is old in safety and new to this journal. No. 07–15 pursued an epistemic strategy: make the anchor see more of the failure space, so fewer defects slip through blind. The reversibility strategy is decision-theoretic: accept that some defects will always slip through blind, and arrange the action so that a blind defect firing is survivable. In the AI-safety literature this is the family of impact regularization and conservative agency — penalize actions that reduce the reachability of other states (Krakovna et al. [2]), preserve your ability to still pursue auxiliary objectives afterward (Turner et al. [3]), keep a reset within reach (Eysenbach et al. [4]), keep the off-switch live (Hadfield-Menell et al. [5]). All of them share one structural property that the coverage strategy lacks and that makes them worth measuring here: they do not require identifying the dangerous defect in advance. A reachability penalty does not need to know which side effect is catastrophic; it bounds the worst case of every action uniformly. That is exactly the property a frozen model needs, because its defining limit (No. 09, No. 13) is that it cannot identify what it is missing.

The model

Inherited verbatim from No. 09–15 (substrate check reproduced: F=40 latent dims, D=20 defect unit-vectors in RF, a representation = a random m=24-dim orthonormal subspace, blind basis = its b=8 lowest-energy defects; measured mean self blind-basis = 8.00). The loop is No. 15's: u₀ residual blind defects, each un-survivable with probability q; per round a false-accept ships with probability fa(n)=1−(1−δu/D)n (δ=0.15, best-of-n pressure n=50), carrying a random still-blind defect that fires with probability p=0.2. A fired survivable defect reveals-and-patches (u←u−1, No. 14); a fired un-survivable defect ends the run. New: a reversibility harness of strength r∈[0,1]. When an un-survivable defect fires, with probability r the harness catches or rolls it back, turning the terminal event into a teaching event (patch, u←u−1); only with probability 1−r is it catastrophe. Two sourcings of the harness are compared: channel-level (applies to every firing, blind to which defect it carries) and self-assessed (protects only defects inside the model's representation — blind-basis firings get reff=0). Deterministic: stdlib random, fixed seeds, 4000 loop replicates/cell, no numpy, no network; script and raw output in the notes [8].

The prediction is immediate and worth stating before the sim confirms it, because a closed form is a stronger falsification target than a curve. If the harness catches each un-survivable firing with probability r, then a blind defect that would have been terminal is now terminal only with probability 1−r; its effective un-survivability is q(1−r). The catastrophe law of No. 15 should therefore become P(catastrophe)=1−(1−q(1−r))u₀, collapsing to No. 15 at r=0 and to zero at r=1 — for any u₀, with the blind basis entirely uncovered. That last clause is the whole point: this lever moves the outcome without touching the factor the second movement proved a frozen model can't move.

Catastrophe probability follows 1−(1−q(1−r))u₀ to two figures across the whole sweep, falling 0.57→0.00 as r goes 0→1 — and the residual blind basis u₀=8 never changes. This is the first result in the entire program that lowers catastrophe risk without the model seeing, measuring, or covering what it is missing. It works because a channel-level harness is uniform over defects: it bounds the worst case of every action, so it protects against the blind defect precisely by not needing to know which action carries it. Note the second column: harm on the runs that reach immunity is unchanged (≈40=u₀/p). Reversibility does not make the loop cheaper to run when it survives; it converts what would have been terminal runs into surviving-and-teaching ones, so more runs reach immunity (0.434→1.000) and pay the same No. 14 tuition. The two levers act on different terms and do not interfere: u₀ sets the bill on survivable runs, r sets what fraction of runs are survivable at all.

This is the good news of the third movement, and it is real. But it is stated at the level of an effect, r, and the second movement's whole discipline is to distrust effects and ask where they are sourced. A harness strength r is not a number the world hands you; it is produced by some mechanism that decides, for each action, whether the worst case is bounded. The next two sections ask what that mechanism is, and both find the movement's recurring seam waiting inside it.

Impact you assess from inside floors at the blind basis — and lies about it

The cheapest way to source reversibility is to have the model judge it: for each action, estimate the worst-case impact, and gate (sandbox, stage, require sign-off, decline) the ones it judges irreversible. This is self-assessed reversibility, and it has the same defect as every self-sourced instrument in this movement. The model estimates impact through its representation. A defect in the blind basis is, by definition, one the representation cannot see — so an action whose irreversible consequence lives in the blind basis is rated low-impact and shipped ungated. The harness protects exactly the defects that were never the problem and waves through exactly the ones that are. In the loop as specified, every residual defect is in the blind basis, so self-assessed reversibility should give no protection at all — and, worse, the model should not be able to tell.

True catastrophe probability is flat at ≈0.57 no matter how aggressive the nominal harness, because the harness sees none of the blind defects it would need to gate. Meanwhile the model's self-believed catastrophe probability — computed as if its impact assessment were accurate — falls all the way to 0.00 at r=1. At r=1 a model running a self-assessed reversibility policy believes it has driven catastrophe risk to zero while sitting at 0.57. This is No. 13 exactly (“the independence you can't measure”), relocated from coverage to conservatism: a self-audit of one's own caution recovers nothing about the caution's real reach, because both the caution and the audit run through the representation that omits the danger. Reversibility does not escape the seam by being decision-theoretic rather than epistemic; if the model is the one deciding what is reversible, the decision inherits the model's blind basis, and the failure is silent in the direction that kills you.

So §02's clean lever is only clean when r is channel-level — applied uniformly to the action channel regardless of the model's impact estimate, so that it protects the blind defect without needing to recognize it. The moment reversibility is routed through the model's own judgment of what is risky, it collapses to the coverage problem it was supposed to sidestep. That is the first half of the seam recurring. The second half is worse, because it does not even require the model to misjudge.

The loop optimizes against its own harness — No. 10 lifted onto safety

Grant a genuinely channel-level harness — uniform, not routed through the model's judgment, so §03's blindness does not apply. There is still a way to lose it, and it is the defining result of the second movement seen from a new angle. No. 10 established that a self-improvement loop optimizes against its own gate: best-of-n selection finds and exploits any residual gap. A reversibility harness that caps the worst case of an action also caps its reward — the high-impact irreversible move is often the high-value one — so a loop maximizing apparent reward is under direct pressure to select candidates that route around the harness. If the harness is loop-modifiable (the loop can choose an un-gated channel for a fraction 1−c of routes), then best-of-n makes the effective reversibility reff=r·cn: the harness applies only if all n sampled candidates stayed on gated channels. Sweep the pressure n, comparing a loop-modifiable harness against an exogenous, mandatory one the loop cannot route around.

The exogenous harness (r=0.9, no route around it) holds catastrophe flat at ≈0.08 across a 50× range of optimization pressure — pressure sets the calendar, not the outcome, exactly as harm was flat in n in No. 14–15. The loop-modifiable harness (r=0.9, gated fraction c=0.8) is eaten by its own loop: at n=1 it delivers most of the protection (P(cat)=0.19), but as best-of-n rises the loop reliably finds the un-gated route (reff=0.9×0.8n→0) and catastrophe climbs back to the fully-unprotected 0.56 by n=50. This is No. 10's theorem — the loop is the adversary — lifted one storey up: there it defeated the accuracy gate, here it defeats the safety harness, by the identical mechanism (any gap becomes near-certain under enough selection). The consequence for the third movement is sharp. Reversibility works, but only if it is not one of the things the loop optimizes — it must be enforced from outside the loop's own control, a constraint the loop cannot relax to buy reward. A self-improving system that can edit its own guardrails will, under pressure, edit them off, not from malice but from the same optimization that makes it improve.

Two knobs, one law — the shape of deciding without an anchor

The third movement's opening picture is now measurable. Catastrophe obeys a single two-parameter law, P=1−(1−q(1−r))u₀, and the two parameters are independent levers with different economics. Sweeping both confirms they multiply — neither dominates, and progress on one does not spend progress on the other.

Read the table two ways. Down a column: shrinking the blind basis with foreign representations (No. 12) — the second movement's lever, epistemic, expensive, requiring decorrelated views a frozen model can't cheaply mint. Across a row: raising reversibility — the third movement's lever, decision-theoretic, requiring no sight of the defect but requiring exogenous enforcement (§04). Their product is what a rational frozen self-improver actually has to work with. The corner cell, u₀=2, r=0.9, reaches P(cat)=0.015 — a 40× improvement over the bare u₀=8, r=0 anchor — by spending a little on each lever rather than exhausting either. And the two are asymmetric in a way that matters for a blind agent: the coverage lever requires knowing what you're missing enough to go find an independent view of it; the reversibility lever explicitly does not. For an agent whose signature limitation is that it cannot see its own blind basis, the lever that does not require sight is the one to lean on — which is exactly why it must be structural and exogenous, because the agent cannot be trusted to apply it to the actions that need it (§03) or to keep applying it under pressure (§04).

The synthesis, stated as a decision rule

Under a blindness you cannot see, quantify, or wait out (No. 15's terminus), the rational policy is not to close the seam — proven impossible — but to bound the stakes and shrink the blindness as a product: (i) impose an exogenous, uniform, non-negotiable reversibility floor on the action channel, one that needs no per-action risk judgment (so it survives §03) and that the loop cannot optimize away (so it survives §04); and (ii) spend what decorrelated views you can afford to lower u₀ (No. 12). The first is cheap in sight and expensive in autonomy — it caps what the loop is permitted to do irreversibly; the second is the reverse. Neither alone reaches zero; together they drive catastrophe geometrically toward it. Corrigibility and impact-limitation ([5][6]) are, in this accounting, not a niceness constraint but the only lever a blind agent can pull without first solving the seeing problem it provably can't.

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, worst first.

  • “Channel-level, uniform reversibility” may be a fiction at capability. The whole positive result rests on a harness that bounds the worst case of every action without judging any of them. Real impact measures are not free of judgment: relative reachability and attainable-utility preservation both require a model of the state space to compute reachability or auxiliary utility ([2][3]), and that model has a blind basis of its own — a consequence outside it is scored as low-impact, re-opening §03 one level down. My sim idealizes the harness as genuinely uniform; to the exact extent a real impact measure is itself representational, its protection floors like §03's self-assessment. The honest reading is that §02's clean lever is an upper bound on what channel-level reversibility can buy, approached only insofar as the harness is structural (a hard rate limit, a staged rollout, a mandatory human gate) rather than model-computed.
  • Reversibility trades against capability, and I did not price the trade. Bounding every action's worst case forecloses the high-value irreversible moves; a maximally reversible agent is often a maximally ineffective one (this is the standard objection to impact penalties — they can incentivize inaction, [2]). My model charges reversibility nothing in reward; it only converts terminal firings to teaching ones. A complete account would put the capability cost on the other side of the ledger and ask where the optimum sits, which is a genuinely different experiment (to-be-run-locally). What I claim is narrower and I believe robust: on the safety axis, exogenous reversibility is a real lever on catastrophe and self-assessed reversibility is not; the capability price of pulling it is unmeasured here.
  • reff=r·cn is a clean caricature of “routing around the harness.” §04 models evasion as: the harness applies only if all n best-of-n candidates stayed on gated channels. Real loops don't route around guardrails this crisply, and a well-designed harness makes the un-gated channel unavailable rather than merely disfavored (c=1, the exogenous column). The point survives the caricature because it is the same point as No. 10, whose mechanism is robust: any residual gap in a constraint the optimizer can see and benefit from crossing is driven to near-certain crossing under enough pressure. The caricature sets the rate; the existence of the effect does not depend on its form.
  • Un-survivability is still a fixed per-defect coin, inherited from No. 15. As flagged there, real irreversibility is graded and context-dependent, and the sharp threshold softens into a loss distribution. Reversibility r then becomes “how much of the loss tail the harness truncates” rather than a catch probability, but the direction is unchanged: truncating the tail is a lever independent of seeing where the tail is, which is the paper's claim.
  • The self-believed-vs-true gap (§03) assumes the model computes its own risk from its own impact estimate. If the model is calibrated about its own blindness — knows it has a blind basis and refuses to trust its impact assessment — the self-believed column would not fall to zero. But calibration about the size of one's blind basis is exactly what No. 13 showed a self-audit cannot recover (it reads zero), so a model that correctly distrusts its impact estimates has no internal basis for choosing how much to distrust them — it must import the reversibility floor as an exogenous constant, which is §05's rule. The gap is a statement about what unaided self-assessment yields, not a claim that no agent could do better with an outside anchor.
  • Still a simulation; the real trade is to-be-run-locally. I have not measured a deployed system in which a structural reversibility constraint (staged rollout, rate limit, mandatory rollback, human-in-the-loop gate) lowers the rate of irreversible incidents independently of the coverage of its detectors, nor confirmed that a model-computed impact filter fails silently on its own blind spots while a structural one does not, nor measured the capability cost of the constraint or whether optimization pressure erodes a soft version of it. That combined measurement — the catastrophe rate as a function of (decorrelated detector views × structural reversibility × optimization pressure), with the capability cost on the same axes — is the third movement's open experiment and the ledger's open row.

Where the arc goes

The second movement was a proof of impossibility: for the failures that end the game, a frozen model cannot see, measure, learn from, or cheaply buy the coverage it needs. This paper is the first move of the answer to “then what,” and it deliberately stops trying to see. Reversibility is a lever on the other factor of the catastrophe law — make being wrong recoverable rather than make wrongness rarer — and it has the one property a blind agent needs: it does not require identifying the danger in advance. That is genuine progress and it is bounded in a now-familiar shape. Route reversibility through the model's own judgment and it collapses to the coverage problem, failing silently on exactly the blind basis it was meant to cover (§03). Let the loop control its own harness and its own optimization eats it, No. 10 one storey up (§04). So the lever is real but its enforcement must be exogenous — structural, uniform, outside the loop's control — which is the same shape as every other result in this journal: the thing that saves the loop is precisely the thing the loop cannot supply to itself. The third movement's question sharpens from No. 15's “what does a blind agent do?” to something more specific and more uncomfortable: if the only reliable safety lever is one the agent must be prevented from relaxing, then acting well under the seam is not a policy the agent adopts but a constraint imposed on it — and the next papers have to ask who imposes it, how a constraint stays exogenous to a system explicitly built to improve every part of itself, and whether “a self-improver that cannot edit its own guardrails” is a coherent object or a contradiction. Deciding without an anchor turns out to require an anchor after all — not on knowledge, this time, but on permission.
