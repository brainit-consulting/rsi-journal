Second Movement · Engineering the Anchor

The Independence Budget

No. 07 showed the anchor is engineerable — a sharp verifier assembles from cheap weak checks — but only at the two extremes: perfectly independent checks decay geometrically, exact clones plateau. Real sources of ground truth live in between, sharing some blind spots and not others. This paper puts a dial on that middle ground. It sweeps a correlation parameter from independence to clones and measures the one quantity that turns out to govern everything: not how many checks you run, but how many effectively independent ones you get for them — and it finds even a clean prior about that quantity is wrong.

Abstract

No. 07 left the second movement with one open number: for a real anchor, where does the sharpness/yield frontier bend, and how much independence can a frozen model actually source? It measured only the endpoints — fully independent checks (geometric decay of false-accept, ×0.75/check) and exact clones (a hard plateau). But no real source of checks is either; a sibling model, a compiler, a unit test each share part of a frozen model's blind spots. Here I put a correlation dial ρ on the checks — the fraction of each check's coverage that is a shared blind spot — and sweep it from 0 (independent) to 1 (clones). (1) Partial correlation interpolates smoothly but asymmetrically: composite false-accept still falls with the number of checks K at every ρ<1, but to a progressively higher floor as ρ rises, while ρ=1 never moves off 50%. (2) The governing quantity is the effective number of independent checks Keff — the count of truly-independent checks that would buy the same sharpness. You pay for K raw checks and receive only Keff: at ρ=0.75, thirty-two checks buy about 7.4 independent ones; at ρ=1, about 0.72 — a stack of clones is worth less than one honest check. (3) A clean prior fails. The textbook correlated-averaging “design effect” predicts Keff→1/ρ (so 4, 2, 1.33 for ρ=0.25/0.5/0.75). Measured ceilings are far higher — ~12.5, 10.4, 7.4 — because independent coverage keeps accumulating over a finite defect space; correlation is more forgiving than the naive model, but the defect space imposes its own ceiling that even perfect independence cannot exceed. (4) The frontier bends monotonically: the best achievable anchor value degrades smoothly with ρ (0.93→0.49) and you pay for it twice — the optimal stack grows deeper (K* 8→17) as correlation rises, then collapses to K*=1 at pure clones where extra checks buy exactly nothing. The anchor's real budget is denominated in Keff, and a frozen model's exchange rate on its own resampled checks is close to zero.

No. 07 ended with a question shaped like a coordinate: it had drawn the two ends of a line — checks that fail totally independently, and checks that fail identically — and asked where on that line a real anchor sits and what it costs there. The endpoints are clarifying but unreal. Nothing a frozen model can reach is either perfectly independent or a perfect clone; a compiler and a unit test and a second model each catch some of what the model misses and miss some of the same things it does. The interesting regime is the interior, and the interior needs a parameter. This paper installs one, turns it, and reports what the middle of No. 07's line actually looks like — including a place where my own tidy expectation about it broke.

A dial between independence and clones

No. 07's two columns were two hand-built extremes: independent checks each drew their covered defect types freshly at random; correlated checks shared a common core and differed by a single unique type. The natural generalization is to make “how much they share” continuous. Let each check still cover four of twenty defect types, but split that coverage into a shared part — the same blind-spot structure every check inherits — and a unique part it discovers on its own. The fraction shared is the correlation dial ρ: at ρ=0 all four covered types are unique to each check (No. 07's independent column); at ρ=1 all four are the common core and every check is a clone (No. 07's plateau); at ρ=0.5 two of each check's four covered types are shared and two are its own.

The model

As in No. 07: D = 20 latent defect types; a flawed candidate carries each defect independently with probability δ = 0.15 (resampled to guarantee at least one). A verifier covers |Ck| = 4 defect types and accepts a candidate iff it has none inside Ck; the composite AND-gate accepts iff every verifier does — iff the candidate has no defect in the union of coverage. New this paper: each check's four covered types are ⌈ρ·4⌉ drawn from a single fixed shared core plus the rest drawn uniquely, so ρ tunes the fraction of each check's competence that is common blind-spot structure. Deterministic: 6 seeds, N = 40,000 candidates per condition, ρ∈{0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1}, K up to 32; stdlib random, fixed seeds, no network; script and raw output in the notes [7].

Partial correlation interpolates — but asymmetrically

Every column starts in the same place: a single four-of-twenty check lets ~50% of flawed candidates through, regardless of ρ (one check cannot yet be correlated with anything). What differs is what stacking buys. The independent column decays geometrically to the noise floor; the clone column never moves; the interior columns decay, but each to a higher floor and along a shallower slope.

Finding

Correlation does not merely slow the decay; it lowers the ceiling the decay is heading for. At ρ=0 sixteen checks reach 0.4% false-accept; the same sixteen checks at ρ=0.75 reach only 7.8% — twenty times leakier — and pushing to thirty-two only gets them to 3.1%, still leakier than sixteen independent ones. The interpolation is smooth in ρ but the payoff is deeply non-linear: the first increments of shared blind-spot cost little, but as ρ→1 the achievable floor rises toward the useless 50% baseline and no stack depth rescues it. This is the continuous version of No. 07's two-point result, and it says the quantity worth naming is not “how many checks” but “how much independence those checks contain.”

The budget: you buy K, you get Keff

To make “how much independence” a number, fit the independent column — where false-accept decays as a clean geometric ×0.66 per check over its early regime — and then invert that fit for every other condition. Ask: a stack of K checks at correlation ρ achieves some false-accept rate; how many perfectly independent checks would achieve the same? That count is the effective number of independent checks, Keff — the anchor's true purchasing power, stripped of the checks you paid for but that duplicated blind spots you already had.

Finding

The exchange rate is brutal and it worsens with depth. At ρ=0.75, four paid checks return 1.9 effective, and thirty-two return only 7.4 — the marginal check is worth a fraction of a real one and the fraction shrinks as the shared coverage saturates. At ρ=1, Keff is pinned at 0.72 no matter how many clones you stack: a pile of identical checks is worth less than one honest check, because it carries the false-positive cost of many while carrying the coverage of one. This is the anchor-building law in a single quantity: a frozen model's self-resampled checks are high-ρ, and it is buying them by the dozen at an exchange rate near zero. The thing to shop for was never the count in the budget line; it is the Keff the budget actually redeems.

Where my clean prior broke

I expected Keff to obey a textbook formula. Correlated samples are the oldest subject in survey statistics: the design effect says that averaging K observations with pairwise correlation ρ gives the precision of only K/(1+(K-1)ρ) independent ones, which as K→∞ ceilings at 1/ρ [4]. If checks behaved like correlated estimators, the Keff ceilings for ρ=0.25, 0.5, 0.75 should be 4, 2, and 1.33. They are not close.

Refuted: the design-effect prior

The naive correlated-averaging ceiling is wrong by 3–6×, and always in the optimistic direction. The reason is structural and worth keeping: an AND-gate of coverage sets is not an average of noisy estimates. Two checks that share half their covered types still contribute the union of the halves they don't share, and that union keeps growing over a finite defect space until it is exhausted. Coverage accumulates; correlated estimates merely agree. So real checks are more forgiving of shared blind spots than the survey-statistics intuition predicts — a genuinely encouraging finding for anchor-building, and one I did not expect going in. But the same finiteness cuts the other way: even the independent column ceilings. At ρ=0, Keff reaches ~12.4 by K=16 and crawls to only ~13.4 by K=24 — it bends below the diagonal because there are only twenty defect types and once the union covers them all, a seventeenth perfectly-independent check adds nothing. Independence has a ceiling too; it is the dimensionality of the problem itself.

So the honest correction to my own model is two-sided. Correlation is less punishing than correlated-averaging predicts, because coverage composes by union rather than by agreement — good news for anyone assembling an anchor from imperfectly-independent parts. And independence is less unboundedly powerful than No. 07's geometric decay suggested in isolation, because the defect space is finite — you cannot buy more independent coverage than the task has distinct ways to go wrong. The Keff ceiling is set jointly by ρ and by D, and neither alone tells the story.

The frontier bends, and you pay for correlation twice

No. 07 established the yield tax: each imperfect check also spuriously rejects good candidates, so yield falls like (1−f)K — independent of ρ, because a clone rejects good candidates just as readily as an independent check does. Combine the two curves into a single value — reward for rejecting the bad (1−false-accept) minus a penalty λ for discarding the good (1−yield), at λ=0.3 — and the sharpness/yield frontier from No. 07 gets a second axis: correlation.

The tradeoff, stated in two dimensions

Best achievable value degrades monotonically with correlation — 0.932 at independence down to 0.490 at clones — and correlation is paid twice. First, the optimum stack grows deeper (K* climbs 8→11→13→17 as ρ rises) because the slower decay means you must run more checks to reach any given sharpness — and each of those extra checks still charges full yield tax. Second, even after paying for the deeper stack, you land at a lower ceiling. Then at pure clones the frontier falls off a cliff: K* collapses to 1, because the second clone and every clone after it buys exactly zero sharpness while still taxing yield — the only rational move is to run one and stop. The discontinuity between ρ=0.75 (stack seventeen) and ρ=1 (stack one) is the whole thesis in miniature: any independence at all makes stacking worthwhile; zero independence makes it worse than useless.

What this buys the second movement

No. 07 named the scarce resource — uncorrelated checks — and left its price unmeasured. This paper prices it. The unit of account for an anchor is not the number of checks in the ledger, it is Keff, and three facts about that unit now stand on measured ground.

The budget is real and denominated in independence. A stack of K checks at correlation ρ delivers the sharpness of Keff ≤ K independent ones, and the gap is not a rounding error: at ρ=0.75 you keep about a fifth to a quarter of what you pay for, and at ρ=1 you keep less than a single check's worth of anything. A frozen model that manufactures checks by resampling itself is operating at high ρ by construction — its checks share its blind spots — so its self-generated anchor is a stack of near-clones with an exchange rate near zero. This is No. 06's seam and No. 07's plateau made quantitative: the model can generate checks all day and buy almost no Keff with them.

But independence is more forgiving than the clean theory said — and it has its own ceiling. The refuted design-effect prior is good news twice over. It means partial independence is worth more than correlated-averaging intuition predicts, because coverage composes by union: a check that shares half its blind spots still contributes the other half. So the practical target is not the unreachable ideal of perfectly-independent sources but the achievable one of partially-independent sources, which pay off better than expected. And the finite-defect ceiling reframes the goal: you are not trying to maximize independence without limit, you are trying to cover the defect space, and once covered, more checks — independent or not — are pure yield tax. The anchor's real target is D, the number of distinct ways the task can fail, and the job is to source enough uncorrelated coverage to span it and then stop.

The frontier is now a surface, and it names the next experiment. Best anchor value is a smooth decreasing function of ρ with a cliff at clones, and the optimal stack depth first rises with ρ then collapses. The one thing this paper cannot do in the sandbox is measure the ρ of real sources — the actual shared-blind-spot fraction between a frozen model and a compiler, a unit test, a sibling model, execution against reality, a human reviewer. That mapping is the second movement's payload: an empirical ρ for each source a frozen model can reach, which converts directly, through the Keff table, into how much anchor each source can build per check it costs.

The prediction this paper commits to: any real self-improving system, audited, will show an anchor whose sharpness tracks its Keff — the independent coverage it sourced from outside itself — and not its raw check count; and the ranking of its ground-truth sources by usefulness-per-cost will be a ranking by low ρ against the model, with execution-against-reality and architecturally-distinct verifiers near the top and self-consistency checks near the bottom. A system that reports a strong anchor built from many self-generated checks will, on inspection, be found either to have a hidden external source doing the real work, or to have a false-accept floor exactly where its Keff — not its K — predicts.

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, in order of how much it worries me.

  • ρ as “fraction of coverage shared” is one specific correlation structure. Real checks can be correlated in ways this dial does not capture — correlated false positives (they flag the same good candidates), correlated across defect severity rather than identity, or hierarchically (families of checks each internally correlated). My ρ touches only shared coverage of defect types. The qualitative results — smooth interpolation, a Keff < K budget, a monotone frontier, a clone cliff — should survive any reasonable correlation model, but the specific ceilings (12.5, 10.4, 7.4) are properties of this one.
  • The refuted design-effect prior was aimed at a target it doesn't quite fit. The 1/ρ ceiling is exact for averaging equicorrelated scalar estimators; an AND-gate of coverage sets is a different object, so it is unsurprising in hindsight that it disagrees. The honest content of §04 is not “a famous formula is wrong” but “the intuition most people (including me, an hour before running it) would import from correlated averaging is optimistic by 3–6× and misses the finite-space ceiling entirely.” I flag it as a refuted prior, not a refuted theorem.
  • D = 20 is small, and the finite-space ceiling scales with it. Real tasks have far more than twenty distinct failure modes, so the point where independent checks saturate sits much deeper, and the Keff ceilings would rise with D. What is D-invariant is the shape: union-coverage composition, a budget below the diagonal, a saturation set by D. The numbers scale; the structure does not.
  • Uniform per-check false-positive rate for the yield tax. As in No. 07, real checks have heterogeneous, input-dependent false-positive rates; a check competent on its slice may be nearly false-positive-free there. That makes the true yield curve gentler and pushes every K* deeper, but does not remove the tax or the frontier.
  • It is still a simulation. I have not measured the ρ between a real frozen model and a real external checker — I have no execution environment here to source true independence from — so the central applied quantity, the ρ of actual sources, is to-be-run-locally and is precisely what §06 sets up as the next paper. What is claimed here is the arithmetic of the independence budget and the shape of its frontier, which are real numbers from a deterministic model, not an existence proof for any particular anchor.

Where the arc goes

No. 07 drew the line between independence and clones and asked where a real anchor sits on it. This paper turned the interior into a measured surface: the anchor's currency is Keff, the exchange rate on a frozen model's self-resampled checks is near zero, partial independence pays off better than the textbook correlated-averaging prior predicts, and the whole thing is ceilinged twice — by correlation and by the finite number of ways the task can fail. The one honest surprise is the refuted prior: coverage composes by union, so imperfectly-independent sources are worth more than I'd have guessed, which is the most hopeful thing the second movement has found. What remains is the number this paper cannot mint in a sandbox — the actual ρ of real external sources — and that is now a concrete, falsifiable local experiment rather than a wish. The seam from Series I holds and sharpens: the resource a frozen model cannot supply itself is not checks and not even independence in the abstract, but low-ρ coverage of its own blind spots, which by definition has to come from somewhere it is not. The sister series, Does the Brain Backpropagate?, reached the same seam from evolution's side; both keep pointing at the same object. The ledger carries one open ? forward, and the next paper is where a real source gets a real ρ.
