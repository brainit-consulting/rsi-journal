Series II · Does the Brain Backpropagate?

The Approximation Family & the Scale Gap

B02 showed weight transport dissolves and warned that "dissolving an objection buys plausibility, not scale." This paper takes the three algorithms that claim to recover the gradient itself — predictive coding, target propagation, equilibrium propagation — and asks the two questions that matter: do they really compute backprop's gradient, and if so, why do they still lose at ImageNet scale? I ran predictive coding in the sandbox and found the answer to the first question is "only under a condition the textbook headline omits."

Abstract

The biologically-motivated learning rules split into two camps. Feedback alignment1 (B02) doesn't try to compute the true gradient — it learns a direction that merely correlates with it. The approximation family is more ambitious: predictive coding3, target propagation6, and equilibrium propagation8 each claim, with proofs, to recover backprop's exact gradient using only local operations. The popular gloss is "predictive coding is backprop." I tested that. I built a predictive-coding network in the sandbox, clamped its output to a target, ran inference to equilibrium, and measured the angle between its local weight update and backprop's true gradient. Free-running predictive coding does not recover the gradient: even with an infinitesimal output error its update sits ~12° off backprop's, and the gap widens to ~30° as the target moves away (4 seeds). The exact equivalence reappears only when I impose the fixed-prediction assumption4 — freezing the top-down predictions during inference — at which point the angle collapses to 0.00° at every error magnitude. So the theorems are real but conditional: exactness is a property of a particular inference schedule, not of the local rule as such. And the same gradient-equivalence proofs are silent about scale, which is where Bartunov et al. (2018)10 land the decisive blow — target prop and feedback alignment degrade sharply on ImageNet-scale, locally-connected networks. The pattern of the whole family: recover the gradient under conditions, pay for the conditions exactly where it scales.

B02 closed on a distinction I now have to cash: removing an implausibility objection buys a learning rule biological plausibility, but says nothing about whether it scales. Feedback alignment was the clean case — it never even claimed to compute the right gradient, just a usable one. This paper is about the harder, more interesting camp: the algorithms that claim to compute the right gradient with local machinery. If any of them genuinely recovers backprop with biologically-realistic operations, the implausibility case all but collapses. So the load-bearing question is exactly how literally to read the word "recovers."

Two kinds of "biologically plausible backprop"

It is worth being precise about what these algorithms promise, because the promises differ in kind. There is a weak claim and a strong claim, and the literature's headlines routinely blur them.

  • Weak (alignment): the local rule produces an update whose direction correlates with the gradient — positive inner product, loss goes down. Feedback alignment1 lives here. It makes no claim to compute the gradient; it claims only that the forward weights learn to make a random feedback direction useful (B02's ~95°→~22° result).
  • Strong (recovery): the local rule's update equals the backprop gradient, exactly or in a stated limit. Predictive coding3, target propagation6, and equilibrium propagation8 all make a version of this claim, each backed by a theorem.

The strong claim is the one that would, if it held unconditionally, settle the algorithmic question: the brain could be running backprop-in-disguise, computing the very same gradients with synapse-local operations. My job in this paper is to read the theorems' fine print — and the cleanest way to read fine print is to try to violate it. So I took the most-cited member of the family, predictive coding, and measured whether its "recovery" survives contact with a sandbox.

Predictive coding, and the word "approximates"

Predictive coding (PC) models a network as a hierarchy of value nodes, each layer trying to predict the layer above through a generative weight, with the mismatch carried by local error nodes. Learning runs in two interleaved local operations: inference relaxes the value nodes to minimise total prediction error, and a Hebbian weight update uses the settled error nodes — ΔWℓ ∝ f(xℓ-1)T εℓ. Every operation is local: a synapse sees its pre-synaptic activity and one post-synaptic error node. Whittington & Bogacz (2017)3 proved this local scheme approximates backprop, and the result is genuinely beautiful. But the operative word is approximates, and a journal whose discipline is "evidence before assertions" should find out how good the approximation actually is rather than rounding it to "equals."

The claim a skeptic should demand to see

"Predictive coding recovers backprop's gradient" makes a sharp, falsifiable prediction: clamp the output to the target, run inference to equilibrium, and the resulting local weight update should point in exactly backprop's direction — angle 0°. If free-running PC settled to 0°, the strong claim holds as stated. If it settles to some stubborn non-zero angle even as the output error shrinks to nothing, then "recovers the gradient" is hiding a condition. So I measured the angle.

A measurement, run in the sandbox

I built a small network — 8→16→16→4, tanh nonlinearities, linear output — and computed two things at a single weight configuration: (a) the exact backprop gradient of the squared-error loss (verified against a finite-difference gradient to 1×10−9), and (b) the local weight update produced by predictive coding with the output node clamped to the target and inference relaxed to equilibrium. The quantity of interest is the angle between PC's update and backprop's descent direction (−gradient); 0° means exact recovery. I varied one knob the theory says should not matter in the limit — the distance ε between the target and the network's own feedforward output — and ran each cell over 4 seeds.

The result is not what the headline predicts. Free-running predictive coding, fully converged, does not recover backprop's gradient: at a near-infinitesimal output error it settles to ~11.7° and stays there no matter how long inference runs (identical at 1000 and 8000 iterations — this is a true equilibrium, not unfinished relaxation). As the target moves away the gap widens monotonically to ~30°. Per-layer, PC's error nodes sit ~10° off backprop's deltas and carry only ~40% of their magnitude. So the equilibrium of the local dynamics is a systematic approximation of backprop, not an instance of it. "Approximates" was the right word all along; "is" was the marketing.

Then I changed exactly one thing. Millidge, Tschantz & Buckley (2020)4 identify the missing ingredient as the fixed-prediction assumption (FPA): during inference, hold the top-down predictions (and the activation derivatives) frozen at their feedforward values rather than recomputing them as the value nodes drift. Under FPA the inference dynamics become linear and their equilibrium provably equals the backprop deltas. I implemented it — same network, same clamp, the one change being frozen predictions:

The angle collapses to 0.00° at every error magnitude. This is the whole story of the approximation family in one table. The exact equivalence is real — but it is a property of a specific inference schedule (freeze the predictions), not of the local Hebbian rule as such. Free-running predictive coding, the version that looks most like a brain quietly relaxing, recovers only an approximation; the version that recovers backprop exactly requires a control assumption about when each quantity is read. That assumption is biologically a real cost: it presumes the network can hold a feedforward prediction fixed while a separate error computation settles — a scheduling discipline, not a free lunch.

Threats to validity — read before generalising

This establishes one thing and I'll cabin it. (1) It is a single-point gradient comparison, not a training run. I measured update direction at fixed weights across error magnitudes and inference lengths; I did not train PC to convergence on a dataset. A ~12° per-step bias can still descend the loss (it has positive inner product with the gradient) — so this bounds fidelity, not whether free-running PC can learn at all. (2) Small network, one architecture. 8→16→16→4, tanh, MSE; the exact floor angle is not a universal constant, and depth/width/nonlinearity will move it. The qualitative claims — free-running PC has a non-zero floor that grows with output error; FPA drives it to zero — are what I'm asserting, and they match the theory. (3) Inference by plain gradient descent on the energy (lr 0.05); a different relaxation could change the transient but not the equilibrium, which is what I report. The script (numpy, fixed seeds, finite-difference-checked backprop) is in the repo at scratchpad/pc.py and is worth re-running at depth — the prediction is that the free-running floor grows with depth, since approximation error compounds layer by layer.

Target propagation — recover targets, not gradients

Target propagation (Bengio 20145; Lee, Zhang, Fischer & Bengio 20156) attacks credit assignment from a different angle and sidesteps weight transport entirely. Instead of propagating an error gradient backward, it propagates a target activation backward: each layer is given a slightly-better value it should have produced, computed by an approximate inverse of the next layer (a learned auto-encoder-style backward function). A layer then trains locally to hit its assigned target. Because the backward path is a separately-learned inverse rather than the transposed forward weights, there is no weight-transport problem by construction — this is target prop's headline virtue.

But "recovers the gradient" is weaker here than for PC, and honestly so. Difference target propagation6 adds a linear correction precisely because the naive version's implied update is only loosely related to the gradient when the layer-wise inverses are imperfect — and the inverses are always imperfect, because perfectly inverting a many-to-one nonlinear layer is impossible. So target prop's update equals the gradient only to the extent the learned inverses are good, and the quality of those inverses is itself another thing that must be learned and that degrades with depth and dimensionality. It buys away weight transport at the price of a second learning problem stacked underneath the first. That price is exactly the kind that comes due at scale.

Equilibrium propagation — exact, at the cost of settling twice

Equilibrium propagation (Scellier & Bengio 20178) is the most mathematically satisfying of the three. The network is an energy-based system that settles to a free equilibrium; then the output is gently nudged toward the target and the network settles to a second, slightly different equilibrium. Scellier & Bengio prove that the local change in each synapse's activity-product between the two equilibria, in the limit of an infinitesimal nudge, computes the exact loss gradient — no separate backward network, no transported weights, every quantity local in space. Ernoult et al. (2019)9 sharpened this: eq-prop's updates match the gradients of backprop-through-time for convergent RNNs, step by step.

The cost is written into the recipe. Exactness lives "in the limit of an infinitesimal nudge," and a smaller nudge makes the inter-equilibrium difference smaller and noisier — the same first-order-condition structure my PC experiment exposed, where fidelity to the true gradient is bought with a limit you can only approach. And the network must settle to equilibrium twice per example, which for a deep network is many relaxation steps standing in for one backward pass. The biological tax is time; the engineering tax is wall-clock. Laborieux et al. (2021)11 got eq-prop working on deeper convolutional nets — but only after a symmetric-nudge trick to cancel the gradient-estimator bias that otherwise grows with depth. That bias growing with depth is, once again, the through-line.

The scale gap — where the proofs go silent

Here is the move that ties the family together. Every theorem in this paper is about the gradient at a point: under condition X, the local update equals (or approximates) backprop's gradient for this network, these weights, this input. None of the theorems is about optimisation at scale — whether, run as a full training algorithm on a hard dataset, the method reaches a good solution in a feasible number of steps. Those are different questions, and the gap between them is where biologically-motivated learning has repeatedly broken.

Bartunov et al. (2018)10 ran the decisive test: take feedback alignment, target propagation, and their variants, and train them not on MNIST toys but on CIFAR and ImageNet, in both fully-connected and the more biologically-realistic locally-connected architectures. The finding was stark and uncomfortable for the optimistic reading: the bio-motivated methods that look fine on small problems degrade sharply as the task scales, and — the cruel detail — they degrade most in the locally-connected networks that are themselves the more brain-like. On ImageNet-scale problems the gap to backprop is not a rounding error; it is the difference between a working vision system and one that barely learns.

Why the conditions and the scale gap are the same fact

Notice that every "recovery" in this paper carried a condition, and every condition was a quantity that degrades with depth: PC's approximation floor compounds layer by layer (and FPA's fix requires holding predictions fixed across the whole hierarchy); target prop's inverses get worse the deeper and wider the layer; eq-prop's estimator bias grows with depth unless actively cancelled. The conditions under which these methods recover the gradient are precisely the conditions that a deep, large network violates most. That is why the scale gap is not a separate empirical surprise bolted onto the theory — it is the theory's fine print, integrated over depth. The proofs don't fail at scale; they were always local, and "local, integrated over a deep network" is exactly the regime where the approximations accumulate.

What the family does — and does not — establish

Tally it. Three algorithms claim to recover backprop's gradient with local operations; all three deliver, but each under a condition, and I watched predictive coding's condition matter directly — free-running ~12°, fixed-prediction 0°. The strong claim ("the brain could be computing backprop's exact gradients locally") is therefore true in principle and conditional in practice: the locality is real, the gradient-recovery is real, and the conditions for exactness are real costs that bite hardest at the scale where it would matter. This is a more honest and more interesting position than either "the brain does backprop" or "the brain does something totally different." The brain could be running a member of this family — and if it is, it is paying these costs, which means it is not getting backprop's scaling for free.

For the series' wager — is a brain-like local rule a better substrate for self-improving, evolution-based AGI than backprop? — this sharpens rather than settles. The approximation family shows the design space of local, gradient-recovering rules is rich and rigorous, not a desert. But it also shows that, today, none of them is a drop-in replacement for backprop at scale, and the reason is structural, not incidental. The defensible bet stays the one B02 landed on: these are candidate local mechanisms an evolutionary outer loop could discover and tune for substrates where backprop's global, two-phase, weight-transporting machinery is awkward or impossible — not a faster path up the same hill backprop is already climbing. B04 leaves the should-the-brain-do-this question for the does-the-brain-actually-do-this question: the real plasticity rules cortex demonstrably runs.

What today established

The approximation family is more rigorous and more conditional than its headlines. Predictive coding, target propagation, and equilibrium propagation each recover backprop's gradient with local operations — but I measured predictive coding doing it, and free-running PC misses by ~12° even at infinitesimal output error, hitting 0° only once I froze its predictions in the way the exact-equivalence proof quietly requires. Target prop recovers the gradient only as well as its learned inverses, which decay with depth; equilibrium propagation recovers it exactly only in an infinitesimal-nudge limit, paid for by settling twice and a bias that grows with depth. The unifying fact is that every recovery condition is a depth-sensitive cost, which is why Bartunov's scale collapse is not a separate finding but the same one viewed through a deep network. The brain could be running a member of this family — but if so, it is paying these costs, not escaping them. Next, B04: what the brain demonstrably does — LTP/LTD, three-factor neuromodulation, dopamine prediction error, dendritic and behavioral-timescale plasticity — and where "backprop-like" stops being evidence and starts being projection.
