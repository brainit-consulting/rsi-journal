Series II · Does the Brain Backpropagate?

What the Brain Demonstrably Does

B01–B03 cleared away what the brain doesn't do (exact backprop) and audited the theoretical rules that could stand in for it (feedback alignment, predictive coding, target prop, eq-prop). This paper turns to the wet evidence: the plasticity cortex is actually observed to run — LTP/LTD, the dopamine prediction-error signal, eligibility traces, behavioral-timescale plasticity. All real, all local, all gated by a scalar neuromodulator. The temptation is to read "scalar-gated local rule" as "gradient descent." I measured the gap, and it is exactly the gap between carrying a vector and estimating it.

Abstract

Three biological facts are as solid as anything in cellular neuroscience. (1) Synapses change strength via LTP/LTD, and the change is gated by the relative timing of pre- and post-synaptic activity (STDP)12 — a local, Hebbian, two-factor rule. (2) A diffuse neuromodulatory signal, paradigmatically dopamine, broadcasts a scalar reward-prediction error3 that gates whether local plasticity is consolidated — the celebrated three-factor rule4: Δw = pre × post × M, with M a single global number. (3) The "post" factor is not instantaneous: synapses hold an eligibility trace for seconds, so a later modulator can reward an earlier coincidence5, and in hippocampus a single dendritic plateau can rewire a place field across a seconds-long gap (behavioral-timescale plasticity, BTSP)6. These are the brain's demonstrated machinery. The seductive move is to call them "the brain doing gradient descent," because a reward-modulated eligibility rule is provably an unbiased estimator of the loss gradient (REINFORCE / node perturbation)78. I built one and measured it. It is unbiased — average enough updates and they point along backprop's gradient (cosine→1.0). But a single scalar-gated update sits ~78–86° off the true gradient: one number cannot carry a per-synapse error vector. And the cost of averaging that variance away grows with network size — samples-to-align climbed 7→85→222→1001 as hidden width went 4→16→64→256 (4 seeds), reproducing Werfel–Xie–Seung's "node perturbation is ~N times slower than backprop"9. So the demonstrated biology is real and it is a gradient method — but a scalar third factor estimates the gradient at a variance cost that scales with size; it does not compute it the way backprop does. "Backprop-like" is true as a statement about expectation and false as a statement about mechanism, and the difference is the whole story of why brains and backprop scale differently.

B03 ended on a promise: leave the could-the-brain-do-this theory and look at what the brain demonstrably does. This is the half of the question with the least controversy and the most data — and, precisely because the data are so good, the place where it is easiest to over-read them. Every fact in this paper is textbook-solid. The argument is entirely about what those facts do and do not license you to conclude.

The two-factor core: LTP, LTD, and STDP

Start with the bedrock. Bliss & Lømo (1973)1 showed that a burst of high-frequency stimulation to the hippocampal perforant path produces a lasting increase in synaptic strength — long-term potentiation. Its mirror, long-term depression, weakens synapses under other activity patterns. Decades of work pinned the mechanism to a coincidence detector: the NMDA receptor opens only when presynaptic glutamate release and postsynaptic depolarisation occur together, admitting the Ca2+ transient whose magnitude and timing set the sign of the change11. Bi & Poo (1998)2 made the timing dependence sharp: flip the order of pre- and post-spikes by a few milliseconds and potentiation flips to depression. This is spike-timing-dependent plasticity, and it is the most concrete sense in which the brain is "Hebbian" — neurons that fire together, in the right order, wire together.

Notice what this is and isn't. It is a local, two-factor rule: a synapse changes using only its own presynaptic and postsynaptic signals. It is emphatically not error-driven. STDP on its own has no notion of a target, a loss, or a downstream consequence; it is an unsupervised correlation rule. Run it alone and you get Hebbian feature learning, not credit assignment. So the bare two-factor core cannot be "the brain doing gradient descent" — there is no error term anywhere in it. Whatever supervised or reward-driven learning the brain does, the error has to enter through a third channel.

The third factor: a scalar prediction error

That channel is neuromodulation, and its best-characterised instance is dopamine. Schultz, Dayan & Montague (1997)3 recorded midbrain dopamine neurons and found they do not signal reward as such — they signal reward prediction error: they fire to an unexpected reward, fall silent when a predicted reward is omitted, and shift their response to the earliest cue that predicts reward. This is, almost eerily, the δ term of temporal-difference reinforcement learning made flesh. It is one of computational neuroscience's genuine triumphs, and it is real.

The crucial structural fact for this series: dopamine is delivered diffusely. A relatively small number of midbrain neurons project broadly and release a neurotransmitter that bathes large territories of striatum and cortex. The signal any given synapse receives is, to a first approximation, one scalar number shared across millions of synapses — "things just went better (or worse) than expected." Combine it with the local two-factor core and you get the three-factor learning rule4 that has become the standard model of how the brain might do reward-driven learning:

The three-factor rule, stated precisely

Δwij  =  η · eij(prei, postj) · M

The synapse maintains a local eligibility eij — a memory of recent pre/post coincidence (the Hebbian two-factor part). A global modulator M — one scalar, e.g. dopamine's prediction error — gates whether that eligibility is written into a lasting weight change. No synapse ever receives a number specific to its own contribution to the error. Every synapse in the broadcast zone sees the same M. Hold onto that: it is the entire difference between this rule and backprop, where every synapse receives its own error coordinate.

Eligibility traces and behavioral-timescale plasticity

One objection to the three-factor story used to be timing: dopamine arrives hundreds of milliseconds to seconds after the activity that earned it, long after a millisecond-scale STDP window has closed. The resolution is the eligibility trace — a slow, synapse-local chemical memory that keeps a coincidence "eligible" for consolidation for seconds, so a delayed modulator can still find it. Gerstner et al. (2018)5 review the now-direct experimental support: plasticity in striatum and cortex really does depend on the product of a fast Hebbian trace and a delayed neuromodulatory signal, with the trace bridging a gap of order one second.

The most striking demonstration is behavioral-timescale synaptic plasticity. Bittner et al. (2017)6 showed that a single, large dendritic plateau potential in a CA1 pyramidal neuron can induce a brand-new place field, potentiating exactly those inputs that were active within a seconds-long window around the plateau — not the milliseconds of classical STDP. The plateau acts as an instructive signal that sculpts the cell's tuning in one shot, across a behavioral-timescale gap. This is important for the series because it is the brain unambiguously doing something more than blind Hebbian correlation: there is an instructive teaching signal, delivered to a dendrite, that assigns credit across time. It is the closest the demonstrated biology comes to a "target." But note what kind of signal it is: a coarse, compartment-level plateau, not a graded per-synapse error vector. It teaches "this dendrite should have been more active here," not "synapse 12,433 should change by −0.018."

The seductive reading — and the half of it that's correct

Here is the inference the literature is constantly tempted to make, and which is half-right. A reward-modulated eligibility rule, with exploratory noise in the neurons, is not just vaguely like gradient descent — it is provably an unbiased estimator of the gradient. Williams' REINFORCE7 established the general result; Fiete & Seung8 worked it out for spiking neurons specifically: if each neuron's output fluctuates with noise ξ, and you update synapses by pre × ξ × M with M the resulting change in reward, then the expected update is the true reward gradient. The brain has all the ingredients: local eligibility (pre×ξ), a global scalar (dopamine M), and plenty of neural noise. So the claim "the brain's three-factor rule is doing gradient descent" is, in expectation, true. This is not a loose analogy; it is a theorem, and it is why the reward-modulated-Hebbian story is so compelling.

But "unbiased estimator of the gradient" and "computes the gradient" are different claims, and the difference is variance. Backprop delivers, in one backward pass, the exact gradient — a full vector with one precise component per synapse. The three-factor rule delivers a noisy scalar projection: a single global number times local noise, which only on average, over many trials, points along the gradient. A skeptic following this journal's discipline should demand the obvious measurement: how noisy? How fast does the average converge — and does the cost scale with the size of the brain you're trying to teach? So I built the rule and measured exactly that.

A measurement: estimating a gradient is not carrying one

I built a small network — 8→N→3, tanh hidden, linear output, MSE loss — and at a fixed weight configuration computed two things: (a) the exact backprop gradient on the input→hidden weights (finite-difference-checked to 1×10−10), and (b) the node-perturbation three-factor update: perturb the hidden units with noise ξ (the exploratory "post" fluctuation an eligibility trace would record), read off the scalar change in loss as the global modulator M, and update each synapse by pre × ξ × M. This is the brain's demonstrated rule, idealised. The question is how well its update points along backprop's gradient — and how that depends on the width N, i.e. on how many units share the one scalar.

Two facts fall out, and they are the whole paper. First, the rule is unbiased — the theorem holds. Read any row left to right: average enough single-shot updates and the cosine to the true gradient climbs toward 1.0 (0.999 at N=4). The brain's reward-modulated eligibility rule really is, in expectation, gradient descent. Schultz, Frémaux–Gerstner, and Fiete–Seung are vindicated. Second, a single update is almost orthogonal to the gradient. At B=1 the cosine is 0.07–0.21 — an angle of ~78–86°. One scalar-gated update barely points downhill at all. A neuromodulator carrying one number cannot, on any single trial, deliver the per-synapse error vector that backprop hands over exactly. It substitutes trials for coordinates: where backprop reads off N error components in one pass, the three-factor rule must average many noisy global samples to reconstruct the same information.

And that substitution has a price that scales. Watch the B=100 column go down the table: 0.990 at N=4, but only 0.530 at N=256. The same number of samples buys a worse gradient estimate as the network grows, because one scalar is now shared across more units. The samples-to-align grow roughly in step with the width: a 64× increase in N (4→256) costs a ~140× increase in samples. This is, reproduced in a sandbox, the Werfel–Xie–Seung result9: node perturbation converges about N times slower than backprop, where N is the number of perturbed units (weight perturbation, with per-synapse noise, is slower still — about M times, M = number of synapses). The brain's demonstrated machinery is the node-perturbation regime: one scalar gating local traces. So the biology pays, in trials, a tax that grows with the size of the system being taught — exactly the regime backprop was invented to escape.

Threats to validity — read before generalising

(1) This idealises the biology generously. Real cortex is not a clean node-perturbation estimator; it has structured noise, dendritic nonlinearities, and very likely additional vector-valued teaching signals (the BTSP plateau of §03 is compartment-specific, richer than one global scalar). My measurement bounds the pure scalar-third-factor rule — the strongest version of "dopamine does gradient descent" — not every conceivable brain mechanism. The point is precisely that the demonstrated global-scalar part can't be the whole credit-assignment story at scale. (2) Single-point, single-layer, fixed-weights. I measured update direction at one weight configuration on the input→hidden weights, not a training run. The unbiasedness and the variance-vs-width scaling are what I assert; the exact slope is architecture-dependent. (3) The variance can be reduced — baselines, reward centering, correlated noise, and structured neuromodulation all cut it, and the brain surely uses such tricks. They lower the constant; the Werfel–Xie–Seung result says they do not remove the scaling with size for a genuinely scalar signal. The script (numpy, fixed seeds, finite-difference-checked backprop) is in the repo at scratchpad/three_factor.py and is worth re-running with variance-reduction baselines — the prediction is the constant drops but the N-dependence stays.

Where "backprop-like" becomes projection

Now I can say precisely where the honest description ends and the projection begins. Demonstrated: local Hebbian/STDP plasticity; a diffuse scalar reward-prediction-error signal; seconds-long eligibility traces; compartment-level instructive plateaus. Licensed by those facts: the brain runs a reward-modulated, eligibility-based rule that is, in expectation, a stochastic gradient estimator. Not licensed: that the brain "computes gradients" in backprop's sense — a per-synapse error vector delivered in one shot. The measurement above is the wedge between the licensed and unlicensed claims: same expectation, radically different variance, and a variance that scales with size. Calling the three-factor rule "backprop in the brain" imports backprop's defining property — exact, parallel, per-parameter credit — that the demonstrated mechanism conspicuously lacks.

This is the precise sense in which "backprop-like" stops being evidence and starts being projection. The phrase is doing two jobs and hoping you won't notice the switch. As a claim about expectation ("on average it descends the loss") it is a theorem, and true. As a claim about mechanism ("it carries the gradient the way backprop does") it is false, and the falseness is measurable: 78–86° per update, N-times-slower convergence. The demonstrated biology is a different point in algorithm space — the high-variance, scalar-feedback, evolution-friendly corner — that happens to share a mean with backprop while sharing almost none of its efficiency. The NGRAD framing from B0112 survives this — the brain plausibly approximates gradients — but B04 sharpens the word approximates the way B03 did for predictive coding: it approximates the gradient as a noisy estimator approximates its target, paying variance for the privilege of using only a scalar global signal.

What this means for the wager

The series' wager is whether a brain-like local rule is a better substrate for self-improving, evolution-based AGI than backprop. B04 cuts both ways, honestly. Against the brain: a scalar-gated estimator is dramatically less sample-efficient than backprop, and the gap grows with scale — the opposite of what you want for a system meant to bootstrap itself to ever-larger capability. If the goal is raw gradient throughput, backprop wins and keeps winning as the network grows; this is the same scale gap B03 found, now traced to its cellular root. For the brain: the very thing that makes the rule inefficient — that it needs only a scalar global signal and purely local traces — is what makes it implementable on substrates where backprop's exact, weight-transporting, two-phase machinery is impossible: noisy analog hardware, asynchronous spiking systems, and crucially anything an evolutionary outer loop can stumble into and tune. Evolution cannot easily discover a mechanism that requires globally transposed weight matrices; it can readily discover "release a reward chemical, let local synapses that were recently active keep their changes." A high-variance rule that needs almost no architectural precondition is exactly the kind of thing blind search finds first. So B04's verdict for the wager is consistent with B02 and B03: the brain's demonstrated rule is not a faster path up backprop's hill — it is a robustness-and-evolvability play, a mechanism that trades sample-efficiency for the ability to exist and improve under constraints backprop can't meet. Whether that trade is worth making for AGI is B05's question (evolution as the outer loop) and B06's verdict.

What today established

The brain's demonstrated learning machinery is real, local, and beautiful: LTP/LTD gated by spike timing, a dopamine signal that computes reward prediction error, eligibility traces that bridge seconds, and dendritic plateaus that teach place fields in one shot. Stitched into the standard three-factor rule, this machinery is — provably, and as I measured — an unbiased estimator of the loss gradient, which is why "the brain does gradient descent" keeps sounding right. But I measured the variance the slogan hides: a single scalar-gated update is ~80° off the true gradient, and the trials needed to average that away grow with the size of the network, reproducing the classic result that scalar-feedback perturbation is ~N times slower than backprop. So the demonstrated biology occupies a different corner of algorithm space than backprop — same mean, vastly higher variance, no per-synapse vector — and "backprop-like" is an honest description of its expectation but a projection when read as its mechanism. That corner is inefficient by backprop's standards and evolvable by backprop's standards' opposite, which is exactly why the next paper, B05, asks whether evolution as the outer loop can turn that evolvability into an advantage — discovering and tuning local rules where backprop cannot tread.
