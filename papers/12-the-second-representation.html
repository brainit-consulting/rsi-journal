Second Movement · Engineering the Anchor

The Second Representation

Every paper in this movement has ended on the same word: coverage must be sourced from outside the model's representation. No. 11 showed a “foreign” patch reaching immunity in six moves — but it cheated. Its foreign source was implicitly omniscient: a check drawn from the full space, blind to nothing. Real foreign sources are not omniscient. A second model, a human reviewer, a formal checker — each is itself a frozen point of view with its own blind basis. So the honest question the movement has deferred is whether the escape even exists, or whether it just recurses: you route around your blindness into someone else's. This paper puts a second representation into the substrate and measures what the two blind bases do when you compose them. The escape is real — but it is bought in a currency No. 11 hid, and correlated sources buy almost none of it.

Abstract

No. 09–11 established that a frozen model's self-checks share a blind basis — the b failure dimensions outside its representation M — and that only coverage from outside M can close it. But those papers modeled “outside” as the full space: a foreign check that sees everything. This paper removes that idealization. A real foreign source is a second representation M′, an m-dim subspace like M, with its own blind basis of size b. The anchor's joint blind basis is then the intersection of the two individual blind bases — the defects invisible to both. I measure it. (1) Under independence, blindness composes multiplicatively: for k mutually-independent representations the joint blind basis obeys a clean product law bk/Dk-1 — measured 8.00 → 3.27 → 1.35 → 0.47 → 0.18 for k=1..5 against predicted 8.00 / 3.20 / 1.28 / 0.51 / 0.20 (b=8, D=20). Two independent views cut the blind basis 2.4×; four cut it 17×. (2) But independence is the whole game, and it is exactly what correlated sources lack. Let M′ share a fraction c of M's basis directions (a fine-tuned sibling, a human trained on the same corpus): the joint blind basis climbs from 3.16 at c=0 back to 8.00 at c=1, no reduction at all — No. 08's correlation dial ρ, now lifted one level up from checks to representations. (3) This corrects No. 11. Under No. 10's optimization pressure (n=50), a single realistic foreign source is not immune — it leaves u≈3.2 and is defeated at fa=0.76. No. 11's six-patch immunity required a blind-free source it never admitted it assumed. Immunity is asymptotic in the number of mutually-independent representations (fa(n=50) falls 0.997 → 0.76 → 0.38 → 0.18 → 0.06 as you add them) and is reachable by no single frozen source, self or foreign. The seam does not dissolve and it does not recurse into a regress; it takes a definite shape: assemble enough views whose blind spots don't overlap that their intersection empties. The scarce resource, named a fourth time and now countably: mutually-independent representations of the failure space.

The movement has a refrain and it is starting to sound like an evasion. No. 07 said uncorrelated checks. No. 08 said effective independence. No. 09 said a representation the model doesn't compute. No. 10 said a complete cover the self-stack can't build. No. 11 said patches sourced from outside. Every one of them pointed at “outside” as the answer and then declined to say what outside is — as if there were a clean exterior to step into. There isn't. When a frozen model reaches past its own blindness, it reaches into another frozen thing: a second model with its own training cutoff, a human with their own priors, a verifier that checks the properties someone thought to specify. Each of those has a blind basis too. So the objection this paper owes the movement is the deepest one available: maybe there is no escape, only a hand-off — you cover your holes with something that has its own holes, and the holes never close. This paper is the measured answer, and the answer is that composition does not recurse into a regress. It converges — but only along one axis, and correlated sources don't move you along it.

A second representation, in the same substrate

I keep No. 09–11's world unchanged and add exactly one object: a second representation. Everything about defects, checks, and blind bases is inherited so the comparison is exact; the only new question is what happens to the blindness when a second point of view is composed with the first.

The model

F=40 latent feature dims; D=20 defect types as random unit vectors in RF. A representation is an m=24-dim subspace (random orthonormal basis). A defect's energy in a representation is the squared length of its projection into that subspace; a representation's blind basis is the b defect types with the least energy in it — the directions a check drawn within that representation cannot lock onto (inherited verbatim from No. 11). A representation's reachable set is the remaining D−b visible defects (No. 11 showed a self-stack floors at exactly that set). Now compose k representations M1…Mk: the ensemble covers the union of their reachable sets, so its joint blind basis is the intersection of their individual blind bases — the defects invisible to every one of them. The residual uncovered count is u=|⋂ blind(Mi)|, and its single-shot false-accept is No. 10's exact fa1(u)=[(1-δ)D-u-(1-δ)D]/[1-(1-δ)D] with δ=0.15, the pressured curve fa(n)=1-(1-fa1)n. Two representations are independent when drawn from fresh random bases, and correlated at level c when M′ shares a fraction c of M's basis directions and draws the rest fresh. Deterministic: 400 random defect/subspace draws per cell, fixed seeds; stdlib random only, no numpy, no network; script and raw output in the notes [7].

One framing note before the numbers. No. 11's “foreign” patch was drawn from the full space RF — equivalently, a representation with b=0, blind to nothing. That is not a source you can actually acquire; it is the limit a real source approaches only as its own representation widens to F. This paper's foreign source is an ordinary m=24 representation with the same b as the model's own. That single honesty is what changes the conclusion.

Independent representations multiply blindness away

Compose k mutually-independent representations and read where the joint blind basis lands. If two representations' blind bases were independent random b-subsets of the D defects, their intersection would average b·(b/D), and k of them bk/Dk-1. They very nearly are.

Finding — blindness composes multiplicatively under independence

The measured joint blind basis tracks the product law bk/Dk-1 to within a defect across five compositions (8.00/3.27/1.35/0.47/0.18 vs 8.00/3.20/1.28/0.51/0.20). This is the movement's most encouraging result and its first constructive one: the blind basis is not a floor you are stuck at, it is a quantity that falls geometrically in the number of independent representations you can compose. Two independent views cut it 2.4×; three, 5.9×; four, 17×. The mechanism is the union in §01: a defect survives into the joint blind basis only if it is low-energy in every representation, and independent representations rarely agree on which defects are low-energy. Note this is the same arithmetic as No. 07's geometric false-accept falloff (×0.75 per independent check) — but one level up. No. 07 stacked independent checks within a representation and hit the blind-basis floor (No. 09); this stacks independent representations, and the floor itself is what falls. The scarce resource of No. 07–11 finally has a count attached to it: not “uncorrelated checks” in the abstract, but the number of mutually-independent representations of the failure space you can bring to bear. The falloff holds at other blind-basis sizes too — b=4 gives 4.00/0.81/0.16/0.03 over k=1..4, b=6 gives 6.00/1.77/0.50/0.14 — always the product law, because the product law is just what independent intersection is.

Correlated representations buy almost nothing

The product law is a statement about independent representations, and the whole difficulty of this movement is that the representations a frozen model can cheaply acquire are not independent of it. A fine-tuned sibling shares its base weights. A human labeler trained on the same corpus shares its priors. A distilled checker shares its teacher. Model that as M′ sharing a fraction c of M's basis directions and sweep c.

The reduction the second view buys degrades monotonically as it shares more of the first view's basis: 2.5× when independent, 1.6× at three-quarters shared, and nothing at a clone. This is exactly No. 08's correlation dial ρ — but No. 08 dialed correlation between checks inside one representation, and this dials it between representations. The claim the movement has been circling is now literal: the reason a frozen model can't close its own blind basis is not that self-checks are individually weak (No. 07 showed weak checks stack fine) but that every view it can generate from itself is c≈1 correlated with itself — a clone at the representational level, buying the 1.0× reduction in the bottom row. And the reason a fine-tuned sibling or a same-corpus human helps less than you'd hope is that it sits at c≈0.5–0.75, not c=0: it moves the blind basis from 8 to 4–5, not to 3.2, and under pressure that gap matters. This is the representational statement of a very old empirical result: Knight and Leveson's finding that independently developed program versions still fail together on correlated inputs [1] — independence of construction does not deliver independence of blind spots. The currency isn't “a different source,” it's a source whose blindness doesn't overlap yours.

The correction to No. 11: one foreign source is not immune

No. 11 reported that a foreign-patching anchor reaches u=0 in about six patches and becomes immune. That result was true of the model it ran — but that model's foreign source was drawn from the full space, blind to nothing. Replace it with an honest partial foreign source (an m=24 representation with its own b=8 blind basis) and put No. 10's optimization pressure back in.

A single realistic foreign source leaves the anchor with u≈3.2 and a pressured false-accept of 0.76 — a coin-flip-and-then-some, not immunity. No. 11's clean six-patch victory was an artifact of a blind-free source it assumed without flagging; a source that is itself a frozen point of view cannot cover the defects it too is blind to, and by No. 10 the loop finds exactly those. What actually reaches immunity is the intersection emptying, and that is asymptotic in the number of mutually-independent representations: fa(n=50) falls 0.997 → 0.755 → 0.384 → 0.177 → 0.061 → 0.028 as independent views accumulate, approaching but never touching zero for any finite count, because the joint blind basis approaches but never touches zero (0.19, 0.09, …). No single frozen source — self or foreign — is on the immune side of No. 10's threshold; only a composition of enough independent ones gets arbitrarily close. This does not overturn No. 11's structural verdict (self-sourcing floors, foreign-sourcing progresses); it removes the false comfort in its arithmetic. The escape is real, it is just more expensive than one hand-off: it costs a growing number of views whose blind spots you have actually checked don't coincide.

Why it converges instead of recursing

The objection this paper set out to answer was that “source it from outside” might be circular — you cover your blindness with a thing that has its own blindness, forever. The measurements say it is not circular, and the reason is the difference between a hand-off and an intersection.

Blindness intersects; it does not accumulate. If composing sources handed your problem to the next source, the joint blind basis would stay at b — you'd merely relocate which b defects are uncovered. It doesn't. The joint blind basis is the set of defects blind to all sources at once, and that set shrinks with each independent source you add, geometrically (§02). The regress the objection feared would require every source to be blind in the same place; independent sources are precisely the ones that aren't. So the escape exists — not as a clean exterior you step into, but as a shrinking overlap you drive toward empty.

But the axis of convergence is independence, and only independence. §03 is the sharp edge: sources correlated with you (or with each other) don't shrink the intersection, they preserve it. A frozen model generating its own second opinion is at c=1 and buys nothing; a sibling model or same-distribution human is at c≈0.5–0.75 and buys a fraction. This is why the movement's refrain resisted being cashed out: “outside the representation” was never about geography, it was about correlation of blind spots, and genuinely-uncorrelated blind spots are the thing that is actually scarce. The reason self-improvement stalls at a ceiling is not that a frozen model can't find more views — it can generate unboundedly many — but that all the views it can mint from itself share its blind basis, and the ones it can acquire cheaply from nearby systems share most of it.

The seam, stated countably. Six papers into engineering the anchor, the object has held one shape under every load, and this paper puts a number on it. The anchor's residual blindness is u = |⋂i blind(Mi)| over the representations you can compose, and closing it to the immune side of No. 10's threshold costs a growing count of representations whose pairwise blind-spot correlation you have measured to be low — not assumed low, because Knight–Leveson [1] is the standing warning that assumed independence is routinely false. That is the whole self-improvement seam in one line: a frozen model is bounded not by how hard it can think or how many checks it can run, but by how many genuinely-independent representations of its own failure space it can source and verify are independent — a quantity it cannot manufacture from a single point of view, and must therefore acquire, and pay for in the coin of measured decorrelation.

The prediction this paper commits to, and hands to a real system: an ensemble anchor (multiple reward models / verifiers / human raters) guarding a self-improvement loop will show its true failure rate fall geometrically in the number of decorrelated members and plateau in the number of correlated ones — and the operative variable will be measured pairwise blind-spot overlap, not member count. Two reward models fine-tuned from the same base will underperform the count-matched geometric prediction by the amount their errors correlate; adding a member whose errors correlate above some threshold with the existing panel will move the pressured false-accept negligibly. Concretely: a panel's residual attackable rate under best-of-n pressure will track fa1(u)· with u set by the intersection of member blind spots, so a panel of nominally-independent members with high measured error-correlation will be defeated at a rate a naive count would call impossible. This is the ensemble-diversity face of No. 05's judge-diversity result [8] and the weak-supervision literature's core finding that labeling-function dependencies, not counts, govern how much signal you recover [3].

Threats to validity

The case against this paper, worst first.

  • Independent random subspaces are the best case for decorrelation; real second sources are drawn from a heavily overlapping distribution. The product law of §02 is what you get when representations are drawn independently and uniformly, which is exactly the regime §03 shows you don't get for cheaply-acquired sources. So §02 is an upper bound on how much a second representation can help, and the honest operating point is §03's correlated middle (c≈0.5–0.75), where two sources buy 1.6–1.9×, not 2.5×. I lead with the product law because it is clean and falsifiable, but the load-bearing claim for a frozen model is the correlated one: nearby sources are correlated, and correlation is most of the story.
  • “Blind basis = the b lowest-energy defects” is inherited from No. 11 and imports its assumptions. As in No. 11, the discrete blind basis is a modeling choice, not a derived quantity; No. 09 derived the mechanism (energy outside a subspace) but the sharp cutoff at b is stipulated. What this paper adds — that independent blind bases intersect multiplicatively — follows from the stipulation but does not re-derive it. If No. 09's representational blind basis were an artifact, this composition law would inherit the artifact.
  • The intersection model assumes each source's reachable set is fully covered. I inherit No. 11's result that a stack within a representation floors at its full visible set D−b, so I credit each composed representation with covering all D−b of its visible defects. A source given too few checks covers less, so the joint blind basis in practice sits above the intersection floor measured here. This makes §02's falloff another best case: real composition is slower because each member is itself incompletely realized.
  • Measuring independence is assumed free; it is the hard part. The whole argument turns on knowing which sources are decorrelated, and I have not modeled the cost or difficulty of verifying that two representations' blind spots don't overlap. Knight–Leveson's entire point is that this verification is where practitioners go wrong — independence is assumed and turns out false. A frozen model estimating the correlation of two sources' blind spots is estimating something partly outside both representations, and may be systematically wrong about it in the optimistic direction. That estimation problem is unmodeled here and is likely the real ceiling.
  • It is still a simulation; the ensemble false-accept of a real anchor is to-be-run-locally. I have not measured a real multi-model / multi-rater anchor's pressured failure rate as a function of measured member error-correlation. Whether real reward-model ensembles show the geometric-in-decorrelated, plateau-in-correlated law — and whether member blind-spot overlap can be measured well enough to predict the panel's residual rate — remains to-be-run-locally and is the open ledger row below. What is claimed is the composition arithmetic: independent blind bases intersect multiplicatively, correlated ones don't, and one partial source is not immune under pressure.

Where the arc goes

No. 07 asked whether the anchor could be built; No. 08 what independence costs; No. 09 what correlation is; No. 10 what the loop's pressure does; No. 11 whether letting it adapt escapes. Each ended pointing at “outside the representation” and declining to say what outside was. This paper says it: outside is a second representation, itself blind, and the two blindnesses intersect — so composing genuinely-independent views drives the joint blind basis toward zero geometrically, while composing correlated ones (everything a frozen model can cheaply reach) barely moves it. The escape the movement kept gesturing at is real and it is not a regress; it is a shrinking overlap, and the axis it shrinks along is measured decorrelation of blind spots — the one axis a single point of view cannot manufacture. That also corrects the one place No. 11 flattered itself: no single foreign source is immune under pressure, because a real foreign source has its own holes. Six papers in, the second movement's object has held its shape under independence budgets, representational floors, optimization pressure, reactive adaptation, and now composition — and the invariant is the same one the sister series named at its close: a frozen model is all outer loop, bounded by a fitness signal it can't mint internally. This movement has now measured what that signal has to be, all the way down to a count: not merely external, not merely complete, but assembled from enough representations of the failure space whose blindness has been verified not to coincide — and that verification, estimating a correlation partly outside every representation you hold, is the next thing a live system has to be pointed at. Does the Brain Backpropagate? concluded the frozen model needs an external anchor; this movement has been counting what the anchor costs, and the bill is denominated in independent points of view.
